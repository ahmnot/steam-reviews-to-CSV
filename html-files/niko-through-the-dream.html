Il est assez difficile pour moi de décrire l'expérience vécue en jouant à Niko: Through The Dream tant l'émotion ressentie se scindait entre envoutement et frustration, entre émerveillement et colère. Je me partage encore entre ces sentiments contraires et c'est pourquoi mon avis ne peut être tout blanc ou tout noir. Tels les dessins réalisés au crayon qui content l'histoire du jeu, celui-ci est en nuances de gris.

Tout commence avec l'histoire d'une jeune fille qui, après avoir peint son corps et son visage d'étranges symboles, s'enfuit de son village le sourire aux lèvres. Sa joie s'évapore lorsqu'elle s'approche de la tombe d'un proche décédé, et après avoir versé quelques larmes elle reprend sa route, sautillant de plus belle jusqu'à une prairie où elle s'endort allongée dans l'herbe. Là, de petits êtres profitent de son sommeil pour entrer dans sa tête et laisser le monde onirique prendre le pas sur la réalité.

Sur un total de 18 niveaux assimilables aux différentes phases du rêve de la jeune femme, vous allez être soumis à diverses épreuves. Après un début qui se voulait centré sur l'émotion et la narration, ce dernier aspect est complètement relégué au second plan par la suite, réduisant l'histoire à de simples images à assembler ou à de petites séquences animées que l'on découvrira sur les niveaux. Il faut donc reconstruire soi-même un pseudo-scénario qui au final est très anecdotique, décousu et il faut le dire, sans plus d'intérêt.

Le jeu s'ouvre sur un décor magnifique, épuré et enchanteur. Et ce sera une constante dans le jeu : les environnements sont très réussis et réunissent des univers différents. Tantôt le style est japonisant avec des temples, des cerisiers en fleurs, tantôt il sera plus obscur et gothique avec un cimetière, une forêt d'arbres morts ou encore plus abstrait avec des néons lumineux et des formes géométriques.
Les splendides environnements de Niko cachent un gameplay moins reluisant.

Personnellement, je m'attendais à un puzzle-game à la première personne avec comme principaux atouts la réflexion et la relaxation, cette dernière étant provoquée par des lieux minimalistes et une musique douce propice au laisser-aller. Il semble d'ailleurs dérisoire d'avoir ajouté des ennemis, cette donne créant un réel décalage avec l'atmosphère du jeu.  Même les niveaux de style "réflexion" étaient moins inspirés par moments, dans la veine de "je fais pousser de l'herbe en marchant, ça active des mécanismes qui colorent le décor, c'est joli mais sans intérêt" ou encore les classiques jeux musicaux et memory. On alterne donc régulièrement entre des phases intéressantes et des phases enquiquinantes.

Si vous vous dites que ce ne sera pas frustrant pour vous, sachez néanmoins que le jeu, bien que pourvu de checkpoints, ne sauvegarde pas ces derniers si vous quittez la partie. Ainsi, si une phase à skill s'avère compliquée et que vous fatiguez, vous devrez la recommencer dans son intégralité. Ce n'est pas négligeable si l'on prend en compte que certains niveaux sont longs et regorgent de puzzles.
Par ailleurs, une fois un niveau terminé vous ne pourrez pas revenir dessus depuis le menu (si vous avez oublié un collectible c'est tant pis pour vous) et comme le jeu dispose d'une sauvegarde unique si vous avez le malheur de cliquer sur "New Game" il n'y aura pas de retour possible.

Au final, on peut dire que Niko allie savamment timing, skill, dextérité et réflexes mais que c'est tout simplement dommage pour un jeu qui appelait d'avantage à la contemplation et au creusage de méninges d'avoir opté pour un gameplay de ce type. Une impression de facilité se dégage de ce choix. Lorsqu'enfin on a droit à de la réflexion, on oscille entre des pics d'originalité suivis de près par du déjà-vu, les puzzles n'étant pas toujours transcendants. De plus, les niveaux manquent de lien, aucun fil conducteur ne se détache et on est plutôt tentés d'y voir une histoire du type "c'est un rêve donc on y met ce qu'on veut !". En définitive, on subit plus qu'on ne s'amuse réellement et ce malgré le grand soin apporté aux environnements résolument splendides du jeu. Ne vous laissez donc pas envouter par les screenshots et demandez-vous si, malgré tout, c'est un jeu qui peut vous plaire avec le gameplay qu'il propose.

Qui est Niko ? Quels sont les détails de son histoire ? Que lui est-il arrivé pour qu'elle se retrouve coincée dans ce rêve ? Autant de questions pour lesquelles Niko : Through the Dream vous fournira des réponses qui, je le crains, ne vous satisferont guère. Au mieux, des insinuations, quand il ne s'agit pas d'indications partielles, sinon carrément nébuleuses. Lui en tenir rigueur ne serait toutefois pas lui rendre justice. Après tout, le rêve est par nature irrationnel, décousu, énigmatique et confus. Aussi, comprenez ce que vous voudrez, l'essentiel est peut-être juste d'en saisir l'essence.

Mais reprenons dans l'ordre : Niko : Through the Dream est-il un simulateur de marche, comme le prétendent bon nombre d'évaluations ? Non, assurément ! Pour deux raisons qui ne souffrent en aucun cas qu'on les discute : une narration trop diffuse, parfois sciemment mise en retrait, et un vide évident sur le chapitre de la contemplation. Il ne lui reste plus dès lors que sa composante puzzle-game. A ce titre, l'expérience proposée est tout à fait honnête ; à défaut de révolutionner le genre. Prise dans son ensemble, dimension onirique incluse, elle n'en paraît que plus convaincante.

Niko : Through the Dream se propose donc de vous faire voyager à travers 18 chapitres, chacun d'eux recelant une symbolique spécifique et, il faut bien l'admettre, parfois obscure. Seuls tiendront lieu de narration des crayonnés animés (un par chapitre), plus ou moins dissimulés d'ailleurs. En règle générale, malgré l'absence de phylactères, on en perçoit sans mal la teneur ; encore que certains puissent paraître sibyllins, tant leur lien avec l'histoire est ténu. A ces croquis s'ajoutent deux cinématiques : une en introduction, une en conclusion. Si la première éclairera quelque peu votre lanterne (Niko pleure la perte de son père ; au cours d'une sieste, une bestiole s'immisce dans son esprit, ladite bestiole étant vraisemblablement à l'origine du rêve), la dernière, en revanche, risque peut-être de brouiller les pistes.

Mais, franchement, qu'importe ! La magie opère, d'autant mieux du reste que cette ambiance mystérieuse a tôt fait de vous ensorceler. Au banc des accusés, la bande son, qui instille dans l'atmosphère un je-ne-sais-quoi de fascinant, sans compter qu'elle colle à merveille aux diverses situations. Dans chaque chapitre, il s'agira d'atteindre une porte de sortie, en l'activant parfois au préalable, ce qui nécessitera de surmonter des énigmes - sans la moindre indication, notez bien. Enigmes variées dans l'ensemble, basées notamment sur l'agilité, la collecte et l'exploration, la logique, l'observation, la mémorisation ou la reconnaissance (de sons, de formes), l'infiltration, la vitesse et j'en oublie... Autant dire qu'aucun chapitre ne ressemble à un autre !

Dans le fond, j'entends. Car, dans la forme, l'esthétique est à l'évidence épurée et plutôt uniforme. Même si des couleurs s'y invitent çà et là (rarement dans le paysage, plus souvent dans le processus qui régit les différents puzzles), ombre et lumière se taillent la part du lion dans ce royaume-là. Un royaume que j'ai pris grand plaisir à parcourir, soit dit en passant. Peut-être l'aurais-je parcouru avec un plaisir accru, si l'on m'avait laissé le loisir de sélectionner un niveau. Pour y remédier, je me suis vu réduit à user de subterfuges peu commodes : un grand merci à Look, tant que j'y pense !

Pour finir sur une note positive, j'ajouterai simplement que Niko : Through the Dream propose un rapport qualité / prix des plus séduisants. A bon entendeur !

Verdict : 4/5 - Très bon, une valeur sûre !

Niko: A Pick ‘n’ Mix of a puzzle game

This is a very atmospheric and beautiful game. Most of the puzzles look incredibly complicated at first glance but once you get into solving them you realize they are logical and quite easy. This to me is the hallmark of a great puzzle. The problem at times is that the game takes on so many forms and feels like a pick ‘n’ mix…this seems to have been intentional by the developers but honestly it just felt to me that they didn’t have enough of a main core mechanic to go on so they Frankenstein-ed together every possible puzzle they could think of. There is everything here from a Coconut Stall, parkour, sliding block puzzle, combination locks, shadow puppetry (yeah you heard me), stealthing, water container puzzles, swimming and agility puzzles, object manipulation, a moment straight out of Q.U.B.E., mazes, abstract Myst-like puzzles, a scene from Ghostbusters. I could go on. Yes this does keep the game very fresh but it also keeps me very confused.

-----
PROS

+ graphics. The game has a smooth Portal type of black, white and shades of grey palette which makes the colours pop when they appear. The designs are pretty and each level is distinct.

+ music. To accompany a very chill and smooth game you are going to need some beats to reflect that and Niko has an awesome soundtrack. Unfortunately the in game sounds themselves are sparse and aren’t up to much.

+ art. There are some really beautiful hand drawn animations in the game. Unfortunately we only get them at the very start and at the very end. I would have loved to have seen more of these scenes between each level. This would have possibly paced the game better and developed the story so I could make more sense of it.
-------

- “hey, my kid has this sliding car game called Rush Hour. This level let’s throw in some puzzles from that!”
“why?”
“doesn’t matter, it’s all just a dream anyway.” The puzzles in Niko feel so random that at points it feels like a series of mini games rather than a cohesive puzzle-exploration game with a proper story.

- story. The ‘story’ in Niko feels like something you might have had to have been following the company through devlogs on Discord or something because it is virtually non-existent. I am all for ahem….Filling in the Gaps but you need to give me something to work with. There is not a whole lot to go on in this dream.

- options. I am not one to get bent out of shape over a lack of options. I rarely tinker with them. But at one point in the game I felt like I was pressing the wrong key so I wanted to rebind or at least find out what the keys were…..nope. You are left in the dark already mechanics wise (which I don’t mind figuring out for myself) but please at least show me in the options which keys I should be pressing.

You kind of just have to hope you have hit a point of no return and the game will load back up to that point when you get back to the game after dinner.

So I would say at full price you can decide if that’s a good time:cash ratio. I got it for 3 bucks on a sale and I am totally satisfied with that. It is a very relaxing puzzler that has a mix of creepy, chill and hectic moments in the game. If you like your puzzle games with no real central game mechanic and more 1st person puzzle vignettes from across the genre then give this a shot.

Produit remboursé

I bought Niko: Through The Dream partially because of its art style, but more because it was being billed as a puzzle game.

As a work of minimalist art, Niko: Through The Dream may well be among the top 10% of games on Steam (though I suppose that's not saying much with ♥♥♥-artists like Digital Homicide on the loose). As a puzzle game, however, Niko not only falls short of the mark, but it drops the arrows before even drawing back the bowstring.

As you'll note, I've only played 35 minutes of this game. Those 35 minutes were enough to tell me that the developer doesn't seem to know very much about puzzle design. Let me spoil the first three levels for you (apparently the developer's packed the game with a good 18 of them, so I should think this to be acceptable spoiler-wise).

First, a few technical notes. Apparently, the resolution in the config tool is the resolution of the *window* containing the game, and the resolution in-game is the resolution the game runs at. Tabbing out doesn't pause the game (I think this is a quirk of Unreal), unlike Unity games. Apparently people have some trouble with save files, and there isn't a level select menu, but I'll be damned if I'm opening up the game again. One thing I will note is that it hasn't overheated my computer (unlike some 3D-, even a few 2D-games), so at least it's optimized reasonably well.

Anyway, having gotten somewhat annoyed at the resolution fiasco that I had when I initially started up the game, I entered the first level. I immediately noted that the walking speed was very slow, and combined with the lack of a "run" button, this indeed is a "walking simulator". Slow walking in video games tends to annoy me, due to the fact that I only have so much time in my life, and putting a slow walk when you could up the walking speed is literally just wasting the player's time.

But puzzles. The first level contains two puzzles; the first one involves looking around the map to find four symbols, and their order. These symbols are etched onto four stones, each one having either one to four markers below it. However, each marker has a different orientation, but the orientation is irrelevant to the actual puzzle. Apparently the puzzle creator likes red herrings?

Having found these four symbols, I proceeded to four buttons that were in a row, in the corresponding order. But the annoying thing is that these four symbols were *just* far enough apart that I had to move the character to click each one. This annoyance could easily have been solved by putting the buttons closer together or by increasing the range of interaction of the character.

The second puzzle in the level is somewhat akin to a simplified Rubik's Cube. No real complaints here, actually

Between the second level's start and the puzzle, there is a really long path that you have to walk down at the snail's pace that you walk, for apparently no reason at all other than to pad out the game, just like how I'm adding irrelevant information to this particular sentence in the review just to pad it out. Not even Flow Match (sorry, "Soko" Match) or Rolling Shapes did this!

But anyway, the second level's puzzle? A maze. Mazes are solvable in log-time, so I don't at all consider these puzzles. But it gets worse; you can actually circumvent this maze by just walking on the thin ledge around the maze, and get to the exit.

Suppose, however that you do decide to enter the maze like I did. You can't reach the exit that easily, because the gamedev put in what can best be described as "slot machines" in the maze with spinning wheels with symbols on them. You have to input the symbols into the machine by stopping each wheel when the correct symbol lines up with the small circle on each one.

I say "correct" because each slot machine requires a different combination of symbols. Which is fed to you right on the adjacent wall.

So in short, this isn't even a puzzle. It's literally a skill test considering the speed at which the wheels spin. And you have to do this with three different machines, in sequence.

And when you do exit the maze and see the level exit, you might just walk towards it... and fall to your doom because you didn't think to jump over that tiny little gap. So you have to do the whole level all over again. Something tells me this game wasn't playtested.

The third level involves lots of water and resurfacing for air. The only thing I would call a puzzle in this level is underwater, really large, and easy, and is made much more annoying by the fact that you have to keep resurfacing for air halfway through solving. Then after this, there's a section where you have to pull four levers and spin a wheel three times all without resurfacing, because the first lever raises the water level to remove the air in the chamber. You can't un-pull the first lever after it's pulled, dying from lack of air just restarts you a long way away, and the fact that you can't even descend directly downwards in a narrow passage makes it seem like there's not enough time to get to the wheel to turn it three times. This is where I gave up, as there was no feedback of "you can't un-pull this lever" when I tried it (making me think I hadn't aligned the reticle correctly).

With regards to art, I do like the minimalist surrealist style (though it doesn't appeal to me quite as much as, say, Lemma (demo) or Mind: Path to Thalamus). There are drawings on some walls that seem to reveal some sort of a narrative about a child growing up and falling off a cliff, but given that I gave up during the third level, I honestly can't say more about this.

I'm a staunch believer in gameplay before aesthetics, and this sort of game is why. Nobody is going to care about your game's narrative if they give up playing because the gameplay is too annoying. Get the gameplay right first, THEN you can think about making your game pretty!

In short, a very much un-playtested piece of art masquerading as a puzzle game. For the price of 7 GBP? Not worth it at all; I would hesitate to even play this for free. While this game is no Flow Match or Rolling Shapes and doesn't deserve a place in my "POS" category, you will get much, MUCH more enjoyment for the same price from Linelight, another minimalist puzzler with abstract narrative, instead.

This is a difficult one to review. I actually kind of like Niko: Through the Dream. I know what you're thinking, 'why are you giving it a thumbs down then', but my assessment has little to do with my personal experience with the game. What motivated me to give this game a negative grade is a few control problems that I'm probably not as impacted by as other people, as well as a major mistake of the game's ending. Together they made me unable to give the game a pass, even though I think it's quite good.

Niko: Through the Dream has two major components: puzzle solving and platforming. The puzzles is easily where the game is at its best, as they are imaginative, varied and mostly make total sense if you just take your time with them. Some of the puzzles are a bit obvious or rely on iteration more so than the use of a thinking cap, but only a couple of them made me feel annoyed, which is a neglible ratio as I see it. The platforming, on the other hand, is another matter.

Let's talk about controls. The mouse sensitivity of this game is really high, firstly, which didn't impact me so much because I can easily adjust it on the mouse I'm using. Secondly, the game has pretty floaty movement, meaning that controlling your character mid-air (or even on the ground) feels a bit clunky, a bit like you don't have quite the grip you should have on your character. I'm personally used to this, as I've played other games with similar problems and as such know how to adjust. These problems mean that though I personally didn't have many problems with the platforming of the game, I can very much see that other people might have. The game is much worse when you have to jump around (especially when the game begins to time you, or when it shifts perspective) or otherwise move around the level in a specific way, that much is obvious when you play it. I can see how this might become a major issue for someone, and with how common it is, it shouldn't be. It's that simple.

It's a shame as well, because the levels you do move around in are often really cool. The game starts of a bit sterile, with the white and empty look that is so very bland, but the further you get into the game, the more it experiments with its visuals. Suddenly you can be met with snow or starry skies, with darkness and shadows and moody string instruments, with red-eyed beasts and glimmering butterflies. It's a varying set of atmospheres, going from serene to stressful to beautiful to horrifying, and yet they all manage to make sense with the story the game wants to tell you. It's wordless and as such a bit vague, but the sorrow is unmistakable, the longing for a lost childhood ever-present. The intensity ramps up, more and more as the ending approaches, and the ending...?

The ending is... something. I don't even talk about the final puzzle, which is one of the ones I did dislike. No, what I'm talking about is how it deals with imagery of suicide in a positive light. That in of itself might spoil a bit, but I think it's important to know before making one's purchase. I don't think it was the developers intent with the ending; in fact, I'm almost positive that it's supposed to be allegorical, an emotional act more so than a physical one. But I can't deny what I see when looking at it, and that is a graphic image that does lend to the interpretation of a suicide with a positive outcome. There is no such thing, and many health organisations have stated that graphic depictions and positive framings increase the risk of someone making that tragic choice. As such, it is incredibly irresponsible to include such a scene, whatever the intent.

Some might view me as a worrywort, a spoiler of fun. But I wouldn't criticise it this strongly if there wasn't real, provable harm that could come of it. It is a particularly sensitive subject in that sense. And that is what tipped the scale for me. I can't recommend this game with a clean conscience, especially to people with any mental health problems and/or history with suicidality. If any developer (or other storyteller) reads this, look up the recommendations for depicting suicide in media, because Niko is not it.

Longer review:
I'll start by saying that I actually did enjoy the game for the most part, but I think that objectively it's hard to call it a "good" game. It has some aspects which which may make it a worthwhile experience, but do I recommend it? Tough one. For now I guess that would be a "No".

The graphics are what you'd call "minimalistic" but I found them stunning nonetheless, almost all levels are very pleasant to the eye. The soundtrack, when it's there, is rather good. Some pieces are very beautiful and some I found a bit eccentric, but this worked just fine with the game environment and general vibe, and fit very well to the different levels.
For a long time, though, the soundtrack is actually absent and you'll play in silence except for certain moments.. I'd say it's about 50-50. Not sure if that's intentional or just lack of time\money\ability to make more music, but I guess some of the moments here are best played silent so that's not entirely a bad thing.

On the one hand this is nice, as the experience definitely does not get repetitive and boring. Remember I said puzzles? The game definitely aims to be a puzzle game, with some levels being a bit more physics-puzzle oriented, some more platform-puzzle oriented. That being said, many of the puzzles aren't actually puzzles, in the sense that they're way more dependent on memorizing and timing the actions needed to be done very precisely, and also, of course, your "platforming skills".
This is not entirely bad due to the diversity point I've mentioned earlier, BUT as the game progresses -
(1) Objectives are also getting more and more obscure
(2) More and more puzzles turning out to be with a more "arcadish" nature (I'd say it's about a 50-50 split with "actual" puzzles)
(3) The bumpy controls are not adding much to the joy of it all..
This created the sorry situation of stages ingame which are fairly hard, not due to being challenging but simply due to bad controls and to actually not make much sense.

Still, as I stated earlier, I personally did enjoy the game. I believe most people won't.

One last disappointing point is the "story" the game tries to somehow build, this is even more obscure than the puzzles. You have two "big" cutscenes at the very beginning and very ending of the game, and on each level there are some "mini-cutscenes"(?) you'll find sketched among the game's environment, usually in hidden corners of the level.
In general I think this can still be a nice way to portray a story, but this definitely didn't quite work here as nothing really made any sense, and personally I didn't get at all what was going on. They could leave out entirely the story elements and the game wouldn't have really suffered from it (and maybe even the other way around)..

I want to mention two things before I start:
1: I might be a bit biased because I got this game as a gift so I'm naturally more forgiving.
2: I have a background in lucid dreaming, I'm experimenting with this topic for over ten years now and I've recorded hundreds (If not over a thousand) of dreams so I think I have slightly different view about this game than most people. To me personally, it's not just a walking sim or a puzzle game, let's just say it that way.

Let's go with the bad. I have only couple of issues with it:

-The lack of "comfort of playing" mechanics. Like the nonexistence of a button that allows you to run. The game overall feels... hmm... sloppy. It's not gamebraking or anything but it does feel "amateurish".
-Music can be a hit or miss. There are some great tunes that amplify the mood (Important, but later about that), but there are instances where the music made me scratch my head in confusion.
