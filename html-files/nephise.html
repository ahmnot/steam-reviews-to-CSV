The first thing you should know about Nephise is that it can conceivably be "speed-run" in under fifteen minutes, once you know what you're doing. Furthermore, even clueless ding-bats like yours truly can probably get 100% of the Achievements in under an hour, so I'm not going to pretend it's a game with a lot of replay value.

It is, however, priced at a measly two dollars, less on sale or in a bundle, so only the whiniest of whiners should find reason to complain here. Suffice it to say, self-professed haters of short games and "walking simulators" should abandon this Store page at once, and spend that spare couple of bucks on yet another "skin" for that first-person multiplayer frag-fest you've already wasted three thousand hours of your life on.

(Whoops, is my cynicism showing? Sorry about that. I've got nearly two thousand hours up on Left 4 Dead 2 myself, so I'm hardly one to judge. That said, I would never spend money on "skinning" a character I don't even get to see, haha.)

Anyway, back to the review. Nephise is the fifth game released by Mr Tonguc Bodur, who is nothing if not prolific given that his first game (Drizzlepath) was only released in 2015. In his defence, he manages to keep things reasonably fresh from game to game, and this time out he's chosen to push the puzzles a bit more than before.

This isn't to say that Nephise is anything more than a fairly "light" puzzle game, so again, anyone in the market for a genuine brain-befuddler may want to look elsewhere. There's only a tiny bit more to it than that, so I won't go spoiling what few surprises the game has up its sleeve.

If you enjoyed Tonguc's previous games, there's no reason you won't enjoy this one. I'd say it's one of his slighter efforts, but it made for a pleasant forty-something minute diversion just the same. I'm certainly of the opinion that his games work best when they're longer and more story-oriented - Drizzlepath: Glass and Drizzlepath: Genie being his two strongest efforts to date - but at least you know what you're getting with a Tonguc game. Someone give this guy a decent budget already, so we can see what he's truly capable of.

Introduction
Nephise may just as well be Tonguç Bodur’s shortest creation, yet it still bares the developer’s mark of distinction. Just as its lengthier predecessors, Nephise is both abstract and relaxing. A journey which represents something entirely different, depending on what the players perceive or desire it to be. Having played through all of Tonguç’s games so far, I can safely assure you that each and every one of them, has a distinct and fascinating tale, just waiting to be discovered. You will only need patience and an inclination towards walking simulators.

Story
Just as in the prequel (Nephise Begins), the titular heroine is silent and unseen. Playing from a first person perspective, sure you might say that it’s hard to get a glimpse of the female protagonist (since video game mirrors are notoriously difficult to implement, or don’t match the themes), yet most of the titles in the Drizzlepath series showed the mysterious heroes, at least at the end of each game. So I’ll just have to trust the female narrator when she describes Nephise as a “young orb collector”. In Begins she was more interested in collecting mushrooms and animal skulls, by the way.

In fact, collecting orbs in this title seems more of a secondary objective. Your main goal is to escape a strangely enclosed snowy forest by discovering four statues and tilting them in a certain position. So that is indeed involving some environmental puzzles. If in Nephise Begins, players had to convince a giant fox to stop blocking an escape path, now we have a giant wolf who’s blocking a cave entrance. Finding and collecting all ten orbs present in-game, will unlock a Steam Achievement.

Graphics
Powered by Unreal Engine 4 as with the large majority of Bodur’s previous projects. It looks gorgeous when maxed out, but I cannot undertand what makes the Nephise games harder to render in 4K resolution and decent frame rate, than any of the Drizzlepath games. Both Begins and now Nephise, forced me to run them in 2K resolution and even then, I had frame rate dips as low as 45, at times. Must be UE4 confirming to me once again that eye candy alone ≠ gameplay stability or fun factor. Unity can look almost as good as Unreal or CryEngine without any pretentious side effects or frame rate instability. Kona and Narcosis are just two examples of the beauty which be achieved on the Unity Engine, if a dev team spends enough time with it.

You can finish Nephise in less than an hour, but I took my sweet time and snapped quite a few screenshots. The snow-covered woodland reminded me of both Skyrim and Tonguç’s Bottle. The cave exploration (spelunking) seems to be the one element which is prevalent across all of the developer’s titles. Can’t blame him. Even abtract games deserve some consistency, since there can’t be only dreams and nightmares involved in the creation process. Overall, I was pleased by what I saw and even at 2K, Nephise looks great and runs decently, with no glitches, bugs or crashes.

Audio
The already mentioned narrator is interrupted from time to time by some instrumental songs which had a medieval vibe to them. Those reminded me dearly of the Stronghold series, for some reason. It was pleasant, no matter how brief the overall experience might be. Some more character context would have been appreciated though and I’m almost missing that strange poetry from the Drizzlepath trilogy.

Gameplay
No type of progress saving options. This really annoyed me once I noticed that I missed collecting an orb in the cave section and couldn’t reach it again. I actually restarted the game just to pick up that one troublesome orb and receive its Achievement. I’m not even hunting for these on Steam, but I couldn’t miss the chance to unlock all three “cheevos” in Nephise. They’re low-effort, as long as you “keep the eyes on the prize” and not wander around constantly looking for screenshot opportunities. OCD is a terrible thing, kids. Almost as bad as booze and Nephise can really confirm this. Play the game until you reach its conclusion and you’ll understand.

Being easy to complete and reach even its secondary goals, you cannot complain that this walking simulator bored you to tears. Sure, you won’t get to blast anything or kill zombies/nazis/aliens/dinosaurs/etc, but you should have known that before you purchased Nephise or the other titles in this particular abstract collection. You walk, stop, stare, reflect, or do whatever you want. You don’t have to collect the orbs in Nephise, just like you weren’t forced to collect the mushrooms in Nephise Begins. Though, playing Nephise after its prequel, I have to wonder what were those shrooms exactly? Another unsolved mystery. At least until the next Bodur game shall be released on Steam. I sincerely hope it shall take longer to finish.

Verdict
Like them or not, purely abstract titles are here to stay. Steam and the indie development scene go hand in hand (despite the occasional “mishap”). You won’t find big devs or publishers willing to take risks in the direction of a game that can represent something or nothing, depending on who’s playing it. So be aware that Nephise may disappoint you, if you’re looking for precise answers or concrete action. But you can’t really argue with its price tag. You get far more than what you’re paying, of that I’m certain. Along with Trading Cards and Achievements. Go for it!

Strong Points
+ Abstract experience.
+ Gorgeous graphics and sounds.
+ Steam Trading Cards & Achievements.

Weak Points
- Brief and with no replay value.
- Abstract experience.

Rating 70/100

Alright. My journey through Tonguc's catalog continues. My current stop is at Nephise, a charming little tale that can realistically be told in about 15 minutes, but with which you could easily spend an hour simply taking in the sights.

Story

At it's heart, Nephise is the whimsical, lighthearted tale of a young orb hunter named Nephise, who enjoys a nice brew, gets some sleep, and then goes on an orb hunting adventure. Her exploits could potentially be the stuff of legends! For the purposes of this tale however, she really is starting off on what could be the road to legend.

While quite simple, the story in Nephise is effective and amusing, and as such marks a bit of a departure from the more somber topic matters covered in the Drizzlepath series. While it isn't a fully realized story, I also don't feel it is necessary within the confines of this game. Perhaps if the series is explored in greater depth though, this will become more explicit in its conveyance. Nephise Begins is next on my list, so maybe things are even more fleshed out there.

7.5 out of 10

Gameplay

The gameplay in Nephise is not so dissimilar to previous titles from Tonguc, but does actually rely more on puzzles and less on actual exploration. This is driven home by the small environs crafted for the game, which really just entails a small snowy forest area near an inn, and a brief subterranean system below. There is really just one primary puzzle, but it was pretty clever in design and relies on you doing what you should be doing when playing a walking simulator: taking in and observing your surroundings. If you do so, the puzzle will be pretty obvious in design. If not, you may be wandering back and forth for a little while trying to get it just right.

Now, Nephise is an orb hunter, so you will be advised to embrace the role and find the orbs lying about the forest. There are ten in total, glowing red and pretty easy to find. My advice is: when you feel like you have found all of the ones you can, progress the story. You will find the remaining hidden orbs by doing so. Does this have an actual impact on the story? Not that I can tell. However, why not dive into the role presented, right?

As far as functionality, this is once again another example of seamless programming. No technical issues whatsoever.

9 out of 10

Graphics

As always, Tonguc's use of the Unreal engine is exemplary. This is a snow covered and beautiful world on the surface, with some interesting formations and a rad looking wolf. Once you go underground, the world really becomes stunning and somewhat different than I have seen before in a game. Really a unique and awesome looking experience. Also, the Inn itself, which functions as your intro story spot, looks quite rustic and nice.

10 out of 10

Audio

The audio in Nephise is pretty limited overall. That doesn't mean it doesn't sound great. It is simply limited in scope, which is consistent with the game itself, as it is very focused with limited scope overall. I thought the musical score was nice, and the old lady narrating sounded believable and fun all at once.

9 out of 10

Verdict

I thought Nephise was a charming experience with a sense of fun and whimsy that I didn't expect going in. I quite enjoyed it as the first entry into Tonguc's second series of games. I am very interested to see what will happen next.

8.875 out of 10, which of course rounded up will be 8.9 out of 10. I definitely recommend this if you want a peaceful and charming experience.

"Nephise" is a short and atmospheric puzzle game with narration. You will explore the natural landscape as Nephise, a young orb collector, and you will try to find all the hidden orbs. This game has an interesting plot, amazing visuals, a detailed environment, a voiced narration, 3 trading cards and 3 achievements.
It's a short game as it took me only 15 minutes to finish it and get all the achievements (all the achievements can be acquired in one sitting). It has only one ending and there isn't a save feature, so you will need to complete the game in a single sitting. I would give it a 1/10 difficulty rating as the gameplay is minimal and the instructions are pretty clear during the whole game (you need to explore the natural landscape and listen to Nephise's story).
"Nephise" is a relaxing and mysterious experience especially with the help of its music. Your main goal is to escape a strangely enclosed snowy forest by discovering four statues around the house. Changing them in the right positions, will let you continue Nephise's story. As a secondary objective, you will need to collect all the hidden orbs (there are 10 hidden orbs), but you can finish the game without accomplishing this objective.
'Nephise' is a short walking simulator, with puzzles and items to collect, developed by Tonguç Bodur - the same developer who brought us the Drizzlepath series.
