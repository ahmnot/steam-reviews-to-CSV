The Assembly by nDreams is a short adventure game with an interesting narrative style. An absolute recommendation!

The Assembly is a thrilling adventure game without any physical violence. Carried by an interesting story, in which you switch between two protagonists in several chapters. But the graphics are appropriate and you quickly feel you are in the action. Unfortunately there is a problem with the colliding detection of walls, so it is possible to look through walls by sticking your head inside or using roomscale. The teleportation control is playable in any case and at some point you won't notice that much anymore. Especially since you can move around in your playing area.

The interactions could of course be even more varied, as in any VR game, but that could then only distract from the story.
Beware of trash cans - I often get stuck on them - especially when they are next to doors.

But nevertheless an excellent game!

"The Assembly" is a short first-person adventure with an interesting story, mild puzzles and moral dilemmas, crisp graphics, and excellent voice acting. It can be played in VR or as a regular non-VR game - this review is meant for the latter, as I do not have the equipment for the former.

The story contains two alternating plots that barely intersect, both focusing on "The Assembly", a secret group of brilliant maverick scientists who do self-funded research without governmental control or supervision. One plot revolves around a scientist who has been "invited" to join the group, but needs to solve trials to prove herself - these start with simple logical puzzles, and then turn into moral dilemmas to explore her personality. The second plot follows one of the scientists in The Assembly, who discovers that something is going dangerously wrong, collects evidence, and tries to flee and inform the authorities. Both plots touch on the ethics, chances, and risks of scientific research and progress. While neither goes very deep, these topics kept the game interesting and engaging for me. Some of your choices affect the ending of the game, and you can later revisit single chapters to make a different choice, see a different ending, or search for collectibles that you may have missed.

Many puzzles require a bit of thought and an eye for spotting relevant information in the environment, but they remain easy throughout the game. They succeeded in keeping me engaged without distracting from the story - this game is obviously intended to be immersive rather than challenging.

Graphics are crisp and fitting for a facility that mostly consists of various laboratories, offices, and a few living rooms. Sound effects are okay, though the game sometimes processes them in a weird way - start to play with a Newton's cradle in one room, and you can still here its clacking noise 50m away and through several walls. The voice acting stands out for its exceptional quality, the actors have interesting voices and deliver their lines very believably.

Unfortunately the game insists on WASD movement and does not feature key remapping. I wouldn't consider this a dealbreaker since you never need to move precisely or react quickly, you can get by with just using the forward key and the mouse. But it's definitely annoying that such a basic and for many players important QoL feature was overlooked.

I recommend the game to players who like story-driven adventure games without action elements, and who like to think about the risks and chances of science. However, the price seems a bit high for the amount of content it offers, especially if you have no use for the VR version. I'd suggest to get it on a sale.

The Assembly sees the player take two roles - two characters whose paths are intertwined, both based in the Assembly, an elite and secretive research society where science appears to bypass ethical and moral boundaries, and whose funding seems to be infinite. New employees are kidnapped and have to pass tests before earning the lucrative position, and if they reject it, have their brain wiped of memory of the entire incident before being returned to the real world.

The player is one such, Madeleine Stone, and an existing employee, Caleb Pearson, whose destinies play a vital role in the future of the organisation.

The Assembly is primarily built for VR, but keyboard and mouse works perfectly even if the gameplay and interactivity levels are limited - it works seamlessly, and it really is more of a story with some puzzles and button pressing. It also looks pretty good for an indie game, employing the very solid UE4. And the voice acting? Best I've heard in a video game - the main two protagonists are beautifully voiced, and the supporting cast isn't bad either.

The plot isn't easy to digest; it is deliberately a bit more highbrow than most video games, and a bit of knowledge of science jargon will definitely help; this is a game for a specific audience - most Joe and Jane Punters will not get this, and that is no insult to their intelligence. But if you do grasp where the story is going it'll grip you endlessly, and you'll be captivated to the end.

It's not the longest game, weighing in at about 4 hours, and is a bit pricey for that length, but the sheer quality does compensate for this with essentially a level of production which verges on AAA.

It's not for everyone - with some psychology, science, philosophy, ethical and moral questions in there, but if these themes appeal to you this will be for you.

Highly recommended.
