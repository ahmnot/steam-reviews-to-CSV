"Seven Doors" is a very short (about 1 hour) first-person puzzle game with no story, no room for exploration, and no actual content apart from a few simple puzzles and a trap-dodging sequence. It doesn't really do anything wrong, mostly because it doesn't do much at all, but it's most definitely not worth 6‚Ç¨. 2‚Ç¨, perhaps.

The game's map is a linear sequence of seven rooms, each containing a puzzle or a dexterity challenge. You can't ever go back to a previous room, there is no meta-puzzle or story arc or even just a theme that connects them. Most of the puzzles are disappointingly simplistic and unimaginative in design. I'll try to give some spoiler-free examples (I won't mention anything that the game doesn't make obvious by itself):

The first room is an "empty library" with three interactable objects: one that acts as a key, one "tool", and one single book that tells you what to do. You use the tool according to the instructions in the book, get the key, and unlock the door. Voil√°, the first of seven puzzles is solved.

The second room has four "What am I?" riddles, i.e. texts that describe properties of four objects - one of the most overused and least imaginative puzzle types you can find. You have to find the correct objects among 2-3 dozen that lie around, and place them in receptacles. Once you do that, voil√°, the second of seven puzzles is solved.

There's another puzzle that is advertised as "decrypting a secret language". What that actually means, is that you see a wall with hieroglyphs, and you have to find three notes which translate them. All you have to do is look up each symbol on the wall, one by one, in the three notes.

The final puzzle is advertised as "becoming a part of a giant chess game". What that actually means, is that you need to traverse a chessboard in a certain way, there is no actual "game" to "become a part" of.

Every single puzzle is based on a single simple idea that has no connection to anything else in the game and that isn't elaborated on in any way. The guiding design principle seems to have been "provide varied puzzles with minimal programming effort" rather than "provide an interesting experience for the player". The game feels like someone tried to learn a new programming language (or a new engine), slapped together a few unoriginal puzzles as an exercise, and now tries to sell the result. Which, to be clear, I'm perfectly fine with - everyone needs to start somewhere. The problem is that this game is sold for considerably more than it is worth, and that the puzzles are advertised as something that's not really there.

Graphics are servicable, but the style is all over the place, there is no art direction to speak of. My guess is that these are asset packs from various third-party authors that the developers bought from an asset store, but I could of course be wrong. Sound and music are fairly forgettable. Quality-of-life features don't exist (no key rebinding, no FOV slider, no y-axis inversion, screen resolution can be changed but may lead to parts of the menu being cut off). There aren't even volume controls.

If you like puzzles and don't mind the lack of a story or even just a setting, then you can get about one hour of entertainment out of this game. It's not terrible, just thoroughly mediocre. I'd still recommend waiting until it's at least 66% off though.
