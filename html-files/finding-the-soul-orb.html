A scenic journey through an imaginative story of Werewolves and Wizards

Tonguc's style of story telling through his beautifully detailed and surreal worlds is unique and lovingly crafted with each new game he releases. Here, you follow the story of various characters having to deal with their village under attack from Werewolves and neighbouring countries, corruption, and a touch of wizardry.

He continues to experiment, adding new features with each title. Combat is introduced for the first time, though being a simpler more basic form of it. It was a good step in the right direction, and I hope he uses this knowledge and grows with it for future games.

Puzzles were sprinkled in here and there, most utilizing your crossbow for one reason or another so it was not always used for combat. This gave the weapon a bit more meaning other than butchering every Werewolf that crossed your path. A few puzzles required a bit more dexterity to break up the monotony of following a linear path, which I felt was a smart idea and used in most of his games.

Positives
+ Fantastic atmosphere. From dark and gloomy to relaxing and peaceful.
+ Varied musical scores that transitioned well between chapters and exploring/combat.
+ Inclusion of a chapter selection so you can go back for achievements or even screenshots in a specific area you enjoyed.
+ Linear chapters and direction. But with methodically placed snippets of story, I did not feel the need to wander off.

Negatives
- if you stand a fair distance away, you can pick the werewolves off without them seeing you. They won't react to any arrows hitting them as long as you maintain this distance. Though this made the game easier to get through, it also made the feeling of combat less polished.
- The element of surprise was lacking as the bow would automatically draw when an enemy or something of interest was within range. When there was nothing around, the bow would be holstered.
- I found a few sentences and phrases a little awkward and kind of drew away from the more serious tone.

The story told is interesting and kept me playing to the end. As someone who has played all previous games as well, I will gladly continue with future titles Tonguc releases. The atmosphere, imagery and creativity that is conveyed is wholly entertaining from beginning to end.

I had a lot of hope going into Finding the Soul Orb. We were promised combat, beautiful visuals, and collectibles galore, plus a fun storyline involving a magical orb and werewolves. And, to be fair, this is probably one of Tony Bodur's best games to date because of the inclusion of these elements.

The crossbow combat is relatively frequent in the game, and it's mostly functional. Sometimes your arrows should hit and they go screaming off into the ether, but normally they hit. If you have trouble aiming, the point of impact is the left edge of your targeting reticle. The reload animation is long, but usually this doesn't matter. You normally just snipe the wolves from half a mile away because they don't react to you at long distances, even when injured. One might say that they're not very self-awerewolves.

There is even, supposedly, a crossbow upgrade in the game, though I did not come across it. You can shoot while walking but not while running, which makes sense. Honestly, the combat made this a much more pleasant experience than a normal walking simulator. It's not that I'm out for blood (though certain people on the dev team could be forgiven for thinking that I am)--I just want things to do, and if you don't get close enough to aggro the enemies, you can pretend like it's a fun stealth game.

When you do aggro an enemy and then shoot it, it does an about face, runs a couple of paces, and then performs another about face before running towards you once more. I found this endlessly hilarious, as though the werewolf thought, "Ouch!
Attacking this guy was a terrible idea! But what nerve! Why I oughta--" So even when this happened, I was entertained.

There is some variation in the enemies, though you could be forgiven for not noticing it. They're even placed decently in the maps: sometimes they sneak up on you, and sometimes one will be somewhat difficult to find when you're clearing an area. The combat segments really made me want to play Senua's Sacrifice again, which is as positive as I've felt playing a TB game in a long time.

The graphics of FtSO, as usual, are nice, though this game is the only TB title so far that has required me to turn down a graphics setting. I would get 30-40 FPS whenever there was dense foliage nearby. Turning down the Effects setting solved this problem for the most part. It appears that one of the effects is to have all the grass and trees cast shadows, which is pretty taxing, I guess. Another couple of graphics notes: there is one very misty area, which looks great, there is a very wonky glass-breaking effect that looks terrible, and once again Tony relies on his trick of using huge assets in the background to make you go "Oooh! Ahhh!" This is like a pesticide--it gets less effective every time you use it.

The story is a little sad. There is an orb, which is 100% good. There is a tyrant, who is 100% bad. I won't spoil anything that happens after that, but the exposition given is incredibly repetitive, and about 2/3 of the way through, the story starts becoming vaguer and vaguer until you're not sure what's going on. I feel like this is perhaps an attempt at a horror story for dementia patients. You get drilled time and again with the same starting information, only for the plot to completely lose you later on, inflicting terror of the unknown over and over again.

The dialogue of the game is also problematic. This game takes place in ye olden days, but the dialogue is almost all anachronistic. You have people saying "Yep," "On it," and giving each other high fives. I would not expect complete period authenticity, but I would like it to sound a little more LOTR and a little less Die Hard.

Another little detail of the story that I can't wrap my head around: the game's protagonist does not seem to play as any one of the story's characters. It seems as though the story happens, reaches its happy ending, and then civilization collapses sometime later, and you arrive on the scene in the post-apocalyptic aftermath of its demise, only able to find clues as to what had happened previously. I wish that you would play as the story's protagonist, who gets to interact with other characters, develop as a character himself, and complete specific objectives. It doesn't seem like that much of a stretch, what with combat already implemented and all. Maybe someday we will see a real game from the TB team. Seeing as this is not that game, I cannot recommend it (mostly due to the disorganized, uninteresting writing), but it comes closer than I expected.

If you are looking for a budget game title on Steam that adds actual story interest and/or gameplay while still remaining in the broader "casual" category, I would recommend the following: Antihorror, Life of Griether, and Only If. These games show what a team like TB could accomplish with limited and pre-fab resources, just with a little more creativity. Antihorror is a black comedy walking simulator, Life of Griether is a medieval combat game that's just hilarious and bursting with energy, and Only If is a surreal experience that largely defies categorization, but none of these incorporates any programming genius that would be out of the reach of a team that can make FtSO.

A First-Person Shooter?
What the hay? An FPS from Tonguç Bodur?

We've come to expect walking sims and adventure games, with a slight dip into horror, but an actual shooter?

Holy flying crossbow bolts, Batman!

And, it's good! Not fantastic as shooters go, but very good. Bodur, and writer/collaborator, Mel Dawn, crafted a classic, but engaging fantasy-adventure story. The pacing is nice--if you're used to the molasses-oozing pace of Bodur's walking sims--and the puzzles/traps are decent, intuitive, and show up with just the right frequency. There's easter eggs, cheevos for collectibles, and EXCELLENT music!

One thing I REALLY liked about this game is that the ending isn't some huge plot twist or one of those pull-the-rug-from-under-your-feet moments. It fits and lends gravity to the player's victory, having spent an hour slaughtering Team Jacob.

One thing I didn't like were the explosive barrels. There has to be enough pieces in the Unity bundles Bodur's accumulated to STOP using anachronistic or tech-level-inappropriate furniture in his worlds. Most of his games are just riddled with that type of inconsistency and it's funny or off-putting or plain weird. But in this game, even a little affects a lot. Those two modern-era steel drums sullied the show.

And this game needs an OST DLC. I've loved the music in other Bodur games, but this one...wow! Wanna pick it up.

Évaluation publiée le 28 juin 2020
Ok, so I just finished this right after Bodur's other "walking simulator" from 2020, Education. And I now have a better understanding of his games.

This game isn't bad. But it doesn't have that sense of exploration and/or discovery and/or gameplay as say Gone Home, Edith Finch, Ethan Carter, Tacoma, etc. It's a pretty straight forward (pun intended) "walking sim" w/ combat. The combat is...not good. It's not bad, either. You won't ever die. It's just...uninteresting and the crossbow sucks (bad hitboxes and werewolves constantly move in random directions if you try to snipe). Also, like Education, at the end you just kinda think "alright. well...that was something, I guess" regarding the story or narrative.

Overall, this and Education both have fairly meh stories, involve environmental puzzles (which I dislike), and have their share of jump scares (tho minimal) and creepiness. I'm not sure if all of Bodur's games are like this, but I am sure I don't really care to find out. But maybe some day I will.

I gave Education a bad thumbs down, but this game is slightly better. Better story, some more interactive mechanics (even if drab), and slightly longer (about 1.5 hrs).
