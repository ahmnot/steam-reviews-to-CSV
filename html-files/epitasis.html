In A Nutshell

üîµ Pros
+ Excellent art direction and graphical style, resulting in breathtaking landscapes and visuals throughout the adventure.
+ Well made blend of free-roaming exploration and puzzle game, open enough to give a sense of freedom, while focused enough to avoid being dispersive.
+ Very good soundtrack, changing based on the places players visit, adequate to the style and setting of the game.

üî¥ ‚ô•‚ô•‚ô•‚ô•
- ...but at the same time makes the story, context of the game very obscure, too much open to interpretation.
- Puzzles are generally simple, fans of more complex puzzle games probably will be disappointed by this factor.


Epitasis is an open-world exploration game with puzzle mechanics thrown into the formula, mixing the two genres together, so results a bit different than your run of the mill "walking simulator" style adventure. Set, for the most part, on an unknown alien planet, players will impersonate a lonely space researcher, to find a way to get back home from this alien place they have been brought into.

Without any clue about what to do, and minimal inventory interface for puzzle items, players will find themselves roaming around aimlessly, inspecting the various alien structures, most of which inactive, and try to figure out how to proceed. Just understanding where to go next is a "puzzle" onto itself, at times. Puzzles involving moving around cubes to hold down place, make laser beams converge in specific points, rotate machinery, and activate buttons in a specific sequence, are what players will have to deal with most of the time, while some of them result unique and out of "standard" puzzle schemes. The style these puzzles are made reminded me a lot about The Talos Principle, although way more simple than that.

The main goal of the game will be to gather several artifacts, but there also will be other collectibles, namely purple cubes, that will unlock additional content when all are found, hidden portals to secret areas are also a thing, but are pretty few overall, and do not really lead anywhere that explains better the origin, purpose, or history of the alien structures. This is the main issue with Epitasis: the narration style is too minimalist and obscure, with players not even being able to read inscriptions, to get at least a small clue about the events that transpired on the alien planet, leaving most of the "story" to the interpretation of players, overly so, in this case.

In conclusion, Epitasis is definitely a very good puzzle-roaming hybrid, that will captivate fans of the genre with really stunning visuals, spot-on soundtrack style, and excellent art direction, and whose only flaw is the way too obscure story, even for this kind of games. It's a purchase I can gladly recommend to all fans of puzzle games, simple ones that is, and open-world exploration based games.

In-depth Feature Analysis


üéÆ Gameplay
Gameplay in Epitasis is divided into Exploration and Puzzles.

Exploration is performed in first-person, with only a flashlight at disposal to get some additional light in darker areas. Other tools, usable once, can be found, such as Power Cores, used to give energy to pylons, firing energy beams used to open key locations, and often gained as a reward for solving puzzles. Cubes are a collectible used in a specific puzzle later in the game, giving access to an hidden area if all are collected.
Another tool will be a drone, unlocked at some point, allowing players to use previously inaccessible control panels. While exploring, there are not many secondary locations or secrets to find, as the alien planet's surface is pretty much devoid of any life or content apart the structures serving as puzzles.

Puzzles are generally about opening up energy barriers, or gateways, to acquire one of the Artifacts or power cores.. To do so, players will need to use several machines to re-route energy beams in a specific order, move around cubes to use as weights for platforms, and generally use some degree of logic and method to get the enigma solved. The level of difficulty of such puzzles is generally moderate, people used to this kind of games will have no trouble with them, might even get a bit disappointed by the fact puzzles aren't more complex.

Apart from a starting, brief narration consisting of some text screens, there is no other direct narration, leaving to players how to interpret the storyline. Epitasis is a science fiction/space walking simulator/exploration type of game with light puzzling. Comparisons have been made with The Talos Principle, Portal and Myst. It does have components of these games but was never meant to be at the same level of difficulty.
This game is to be enjoyed for its ethereal beauty and stunningly beautiful graphics. You are meant to explore the alien environments, marvel at the engineering feats and ponder on the sprawling ruins of a lost ancient civilisation. There is no pressure, just a relaxing stroll admiring what is around you. Be sure to dodge some of the drones as you can be killed, but you respawn very quickly.
One warning about this game is that there are no instructions, no dialogue or narration, no help buttons. You are entirely on your own. Apart from a brief introduction, the story is entirely yours to interpret. You can be like me scratching your head wondering what that was all about or you may be able to decipher some of the text and interpret the story.
You are a scientist at a space tracking station and the radars pick up a signal. A portal appears and you enter into an alien planet. Gameplay comprises of activating laser beams to open doors by using weight cubes and getting all the laser beams to converge at certain points. Along the way you adopt a friendly drone who helps you to open these doors and communicate with the other drones. You encounter less friendly ones who zealously protect their patch and will kill you.
You have to collect magenta cubes along the way which go into your inventory. They will be used to open up another portal.
Music is gentle and mostly done by a synthesizer. It really enhances the beautiful visuals. This is a 2 man developer team and they've done an excellent job with this game. I will be interested with their next adventure.
The only negatives is the lack of an understandable story. Not everyone will understand the nuances of the visuals. Other reviewers have mentioned the lack of a map which would have been most helpful as there were often 2 or 3 worlds interlinked with one another and you can get easily lost or confused. The other downside is that the Hidden Garden achievement didn't activate for me which has happened to others.
Other than those small negatives, it was a very relaxing and beautiful game to enjoy by a 2 man team. I encountered no bugs. Highly recommend for those who enjoy walking sims with light puzzling. 8.5/10
Note: I took over 8 hours to play, but faster people have done it in 2 hours. I just took my time and enjoyed all the beauty around me and the changing colours as night fell.

Things you should NOT expect from Epitasis

If you're reading this review, chances are you've checked the game description, the trailers, and the screenshots. Or at least some of these.

Let's start with something obvious that still needs to be addressed: this game is neither Myst, the Talos Principle, The Witness, nor Fez.
It has a bit of this mystery vibe you'd get from playing Myst, the puzzle solving requires a bit of the logic you'd find in The Talos Principle, it will make you feel a bit of the sense of wonder you might have experienced while playing The Witness, and its alien language might remind you a bit of the language puzzles in Fez. But it is neither of these games, and you as a player should definitely not expect Epitasis to be any of these.


Now that this is out of the way...

What is Epitasis?

It is a short (around 3h for 100%) and relatively easy "puzzle" game that gets you playing as a researcher looking for alien life from earth. One day, as you're busy basically watching a screen "just in case anything happens", you suddenly receive a message from another world. Quickly, you start following this unexpected lead and embark on a journey that will lead you on a new planet.

The game does not bother with too much hand holding, which is a good thing in my opinion for this kind of games: you will have to find how to get started on your own. This is especially important since the game can anyway be 100%-ed in less than 3h. No hand holding means that you'll get barred from entering places where you thought you needed to go because of a missing key. It also and mostly means that you will get to experience being slightly lost on an beautiful and vibrant alien world.

As for the ambiance of the game, I just loved the different environments. I also really appreciated the fact that the planet I was exploring felt somehow "real", with both its weather and its day/night cycle. However, I have to admit that the music sometimes fell a bit short for me. This is just personal taste, but I thought that the music wasn't really coherent with some of the environments. Another disappointment for me was that I did not find a way to translate the alien language into anything I could understand.

Overall, I quite enjoyed the game, especially considering there was only one developer working on Epitasis. I would love to see more games from this person.
For these reasons, I would recommend buying it if you're looking for a short and nice game with a bit of mystery. However, you should not expect too much from the puzzles: Epitasis is mostly about the ambiance.

Depending on whether you're a hard-core puzzle fan or not, this may not be the game you're looking for.

  • Length - I clocked in at just over 2 hours with all achievements (I didn't get stuck for more than a few minutes with any of the puzzles in the game). For those who want the price of a game to reflect the gameplay length, you may want to wait for a sale.

  • Lighting during night phase - While having the environment be dark during night-time helps with the atmosphere, it can be a bit too dark for some stages especially when foliage covers the ground and the puzzle structures don't provide very much lighting of their own. No, it's not a medical procedure.

That was my first guess; but then I looked it up and learned that it's actually something from Greek drama, although I still don't see what it has to do with this game.

Doesn't really matter, though, since you're going to be too busy staring at the colors to worry about whether it makes any sense or not.

We're talking overwhelming waves of unearthly, oversaturated, ever-shifting hues, just coming at you one after another. Well, there is a perfunctory little introduction that gives them an excuse to drop you onto an alien planet, but after that it's All Colors, All the Time. It'll make you want to double-check the ingredients on the last thing you ate.

Now in theory this is a puzzle game, sort of a low-key version of The Talos Principle. At least, it's got the same basic combination of open exploration and discrete puzzle zones, although much more skewed toward the exploration side. Instead of trying to dazzle you with deep thoughts along the way, however, this one's more about creating a general ambience.

On that front they're pretty successful. Maybe it does get a little too vivid at times, but they manage to pull off an overall look that's often quite striking. A lot of it has to do with the lighting. One of the nicest aesthetic touches is the dramatic way each area changes as it cycles from day to night. It really livens things up, especially if you get as lost as I did and end up having to do lots of backtracking.

Meanwhile, the static environments, for their part, blend the natural and artificial in attractive proportion. Sprawling fields of grasses and flowers, rugged cliffs and watery vistas are interspersed with mysterious alien structures. Kind of reminds me of the outdoor levels in Halo.

The architecture itself is pretty eye-catching. What do you call this? Sort of a techno-gothic type style, I guess. You've got the usual futuristic gray metallic alloy, but the buildings also feature all sorts of buttresses and columns, soaring up into great spires like alien cathedrals. In a purely technical sense, the graphics struggle to keep up with the art design, but you still get the strong impression they had a real vision here for their world that manages to rise well above the generic.

Can't say the same about the puzzles, I'm afraid. They mostly consist of: drop block onto pressure plate; door opens; proceed. Sometimes you have to rotate a mirror and move beams around, too; and while one or two of them might give you pause to think, overall they're so easy that they don't amount to much more than a nuisance.

The hardest part of the whole thing comes from the absence of a map. I don't know, maybe they wanted to eliminate any interface-related distractions, but that would have really cut down on the time spent walking in circles. There was also this one device that I didn't even realize was a puzzle because it didn't look like any other puzzle in the game.

They should have just dispensed with the lame puzzles entirely and--I can't believe I'm about to say this--gone the quasi-walking simulator route. That is, the interactivity would be entirely a matter of engaging with the alien structures in creative ways, bringing them slowly to life, watching them morph and light up and tell some kind of simple, visual story in tune with their environment.

That sort of style really would have complemented the trancey vibe of the setting and music much better.

What can I say? Sorry, game. You had potential, but you kinda took the wrong path in life. Should have hung around with a different crowd. The Talos Principle wasn't the best influence.

Epitasis

Epitasis offers a serene and tranquil trip into a terrestrial world. A surreal place in the cosmos filled with its own rich lore of ancient races and forgotten technologies to scout out and ponder the machinations of.

Despite its appearance of open and explorable alien worlds rife with beautiful surreal landscapes Epitasis is much more than a cosmic walking simulator. A plethora of unique puzzles and interesting solutions are spread throughout the world to interact with, and the mechanics are never quite clear leaving the player to use their intuition and creativity to feel through the mechanics of ancient technologies.

With a visual presentation that lies somewhere between the simplistic surreal pleasantries of Proteus and the grand sci-fi wonders of No Man's Sky. It's way more active in game play than either, however, with interactions and puzzles that keep you pressing on to the next important piece.

I'm still very early in my journey, and the entire purpose or plot is not entirely clear to me yet but I can easily say this is one that will engage anyone with an explorative side and an inquisitive mind, as this is purely an experience of discovery. Much more to come as I work my way through, but this is an instantly memorable and accesible sci-fi adventure that I can already heartily recommend to anyone interested in such topics.

The game is not very long; with full exploration (i.e. roaming aimlessly to find the edges of the maps, jumping off cliffs, walking into the sea etc), it took about 4.5 hours to finish.
The puzzles were quite satisfactory, although easy (sometimes too easy) and I also absolutely enjoyed the soundtrack; it seemed to match the environment very well and even though the graphics were too simple, they did their job perfectly.
However, there was (almost) no lore or explanation of what happened. The game starts and tells you a few lines of story, and that's it. Then it's up to you to translate an alphabet based on binary code, and get some texts, which are a little too few. We learn that there were some people/individuals (aliens?) divided in groups; some groups made war to other groups (or to everyone) and then they went extinct. Or they left. How did they end up on Earth? One message says that they see us. Also, in the end, we hit a switch and we levitate into a white light. Did we reach the Library on the Moon? From the planet size it seems that we reached the main planet, while we were on the moon, so it kinda confused me. I do love games with lore and I love reading long texts or scrolling through murals with pictures and stories to find out what happened. Stories give a lot of depth to adventure games, after all. Playing puzzle after puzzle made me feel like going forward just because there was nothing else to do.
Also, I encountered some minor issues. There were one or two occasions where I restarted a puzzle due to a physics bug (like throwing a cube on a floor switch, but then the cube rolling away locking me in/out), but that was probably due to my bad aim, and, like others I never got the Hidden Gardens achievement no matter how many times I went back. Still, not much to make me hate this game.

I do have mixed feelings on this one, but I would still recommend it to puzzle/adventure game players, just for the ambience and relaxing gameplay.

Full review and gameplay of Epitasis, a first-person puzzle adventure game in the style of Portal or Turing Test. It's available on PC via Steam.


Epitasis is a sci-fi, exploration, puzzle game, set on a far off distant planet. It was left by it's residents and is now only filled with futuristic structures, mystic energy portals and beams, and treasured relics, that are riddled among the beautiful wilderness. Your goal is to find 4 missing artifacts to open a portal, that brings you back to earth. The game is made in first person perspective and you simply walk around, explore the colorful environment and interact with cubes and switches by pressing the left mouse button.

For me the first person perspective was a problem, as I become motion sick very easy. But luckily, there's an option in the settings to disable the screen movement, which worked fine for me. The gameplay was also a bit confusing at the beginning, as there's no narrator, who tells the story, or any explanations what you have to do, or a guiding thread to where you have to go. So I think for the first 30 minutes I was just running around, trying to find out how the game works. But after a while I got the hang of it and really enjoyed it, even though I'm not a big fan of these extreme colors, but in Epitasis they fit the game perfectly. Every other graphic concept would feel wrong here.

But let me tell you a bit more about the gameplay, that I think needs some explanation. Once you arrived there are 4 portals that lead to different parts of the world. Every part is again parted in 3 or 4 different puzzles. It doesn't really matter with which part you start, but I would recommend to go for the circle artifact first, as this makes the further explorations a bit easier and more structured. The puzzles are mostly solved by carrying cubes to switches on the floor, which then provide energy that you need to open gates. There are also other types of puzzles, but I don't want to spoil to much of the game. The difficulty level is not too hard, even though some puzzles are tougher than others and you need a bit more forward thinking. But overall there's nothing that can't be solved. It only get's harder during the night, as you can barely see anything and even the flashlight you can use by pressing ‚ÄûF‚Äú, doesn't really help. This is definitely a point the devs should consider to change in an upcoming update.

Overall Epitasis is a simple but good game, with nice, colorful graphics, amazing music and a playtime of about 4 hours. Negative points for me are the missing explanations, about gameplay and story, the night, that is too dark and makes the game unplayable in some parts of the map, even though the day-night-switch in general and the weather effects give the game a nice atmosphere. And there's only one save game available, that get's lost once you start a new game.
