war... war never changes. so our painter protagonist chooses painting colorful pictures as escapism, since there's a noticable lack of video games in the 1940s. one night a random stranger shows up at his house and offers him to actually escape into his paintings, the caveat being he has to be back by midnight or get stuck there forever. why he'd want to come back to reality in the first place is beyond me, but hey.

it's your typical linear 1st-person puzzler. walk around, solve puzzles, move on, and put the narrative together yourself, as there's nothing concrete after the intro, until the outro (both voiced). locations are varied, weird and imaginative, but I didn't enjoy any of the puzzles except the escherian castle. too much tedious back and forth in most places, especially in new prague (half my playtime was spent on that one puzzle), plus the usual piano, rotating, etc. mechanics. and way too much stair-climbing. the game could've easily and accurately been called doors and stairs.

tedium aside, the first major problem is the actual time limit of 3 or 6 real-life hours, mentioned nowhere on the store page. why would you want to take your time, explore and look at things for more than a second in such an amazing-looking game, right? sure, there's a war, but the whole point of your journey is to escape from reality, not to get back to it asap. as I found out, explorer mode without a timer unlocks after finishing the game, which is complete nonsense. don't make me waste hours to rush through everything as fast as I can to finally let me play how I wanted to in the first place.

you can shave some time off by replaying earlier chapters faster, because we all know that's what story-based games are all about: speedrunning, repetition and optimization during the first (and for many, the only) playthrough. the time limit is pretty generous, 6 hours is more than enough to finish it, 3 might do it as well (it took me a bit less than that with some outside help), but then why have a timer in the first place? and even if you finish with hours to spare, there's only 15 minutes for the final section, making the clock an even more baffling and unnecessary inclusion.

the second problem is no mid-chapter saving. have to do each section in one go, and all the walking necessary in some of them makes you feel you're wasting too much of already limited time. checkpoints aren't placed at the beginning of new chapters either, so continuing next time you'll have to open a door again, watch the same unskippable cutscenes again, etc. checkpoint-only saving can be done right, but it's not done right here.

either of these things could result in an instant thumbs down, no matter how absolutely gorgeous the game looks (gameplay and functionality come first, eyecandy not necessarily even second), but both of them combined is just a waste and disrespect of the player's time.

it's especially sad because the dev is clearly dedicated and the technical aspects (not just the graphics and the audio) are done really well for the most part, and it runs fine on my pretty low-end hardware, but in the process it forgets that it's just a video game to be played according to one's own schedule, not one dictated by the game.

movement speed is fast enough while sprinting (can be toggled, no need to hold, nor a need to turn it off) and controls do their job but they're not rebindable. you can use a controller, or wasd or arrows for movement, 'e' to interact, mouse to look around, right click to zoom out, tab to bring up the dreaded pocket watch.

various languages, windowed mode, resolution options, graphics quality, motion blur toggle, and a single volume slider are available from the main menu, complemented by an fov slider, invert x/y camera, and even a paint effect slider while playing. audio is mixed properly, so the only slider is not a huge deal, but toggles for sound, music and speech would still be nice. company logos on startup can't be skipped either, and once new game is clicked, there's no way to go back to the main menu after the intro, have to choose a game mode and start playing first.

even though I didn't particularly enjoy the ride, if explorer mode was available from the start and manual saving (or more checkpoints) got patched in, I might cautiously recommend it, the price would be reasonable as well. as it is, I can only suggest watching a let's play on youtube, so you can stop and continue without losing progress, though honestly, you're not missing much by not experiencing it. hopefully, their next game is gonna offer more than pretty things to look at in a well-optimized package.

Perplexing walking Puzzler
Summertime Madness serves a perplexing story of an artist looking to escape the horrors of war by making a deal with the devil to enter one of his paintings. With devil deals, there is of course a catch. The artist has to make it out within a certain time frame or he shall be trapped inside the painting forever. How long the player actually has to accomplish this task is up to the player. By default, you have two choices. Complete the game within 6 hours or to complete the game without a time limit.

There is some outside the box thinking needed in order to solve the puzzles, however the game is forgiving with checkpoints and the time at least on the six hour difficulty is really there for immersion. I honestly feel like there is plenty of time to figure out the world and puzzles within the time frame regardless of your skill. If you feel weary of timed modes you can always play the explorer mode which offers a stress free play style.

The graphics and world is beautiful and surreal. You can tweak the scale of paint in the options making it have more or less of a "in a painted world" effect. The Minor Gripes
This game is another instance of kind of subjective to the player. Although there is a way to play the game without a time limit now, it feels like it was intended to play with the time limit. I personally really dislike time limits in games especially the idea of it being in an exploration puzzle game, but again I felt the time here was not a major push in the unforgiving direction. You are given 15 minutes to solve the last puzzle regardless of your time, which while somewhat disappointing is also plenty of time to figure it out.

There is a fair amount of walking around for your puzzles. This to me was no problem whatsoever. There is a run button you can toggle on and off, but if walking sims aren't your thing, than you may not like the amount of "walking" necessary in some puzzles. Aside from that, the only other real gripe I had was to do with story. I'm not really sure what to say because I don't want to spoil what little there is, but I just feel like it could have been more interesting with the premise.

What would have been a beautiful walking simulator if it wasn't full of time-wasting and pace-killing trial and error "puzzles". My first urge to refund came after starting the game and learning that WASD was mandatory. I found that I could muddle through on a PS controller, and pressed on. An hour in, after being presented with the genuinely impressive revelation of Neo Prague, I fell asleep while trying to navigate the labyrinthine switch puzzle contained within. After waking up and removing my character's face from the wall he had been treadmilling against for the last minute, I gave up. This wasn't fun, nor did the artwork outweigh the chore. +Visually appealing
+Devs responded to a number of player gripes since release, mostly revolving around time limits

~Game only saves when puzzles are complete

~Only about 3 hours long if you're dedicated enough

~A number of collectibles, but they don't seem to do much and you'll probably only accidentally find some obscure ones half-way through - and this definitely isn't a game you'll replay to complete the set.

The Bad
-Forced WASD controls
-Unskippable opening splash screens! Aaaargh
-Most "puzzles" are largely trial and error walking and clicking
