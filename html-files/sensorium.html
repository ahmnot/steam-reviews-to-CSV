Sensorium caught me quite by surprise. It is one of the best 'empty landscape' puzzle games I have played in years. I am reminded most of The Witness. Although that is a much larger game with higher resolution graphics, the idea of arriving in an abandoned area and discovering and solving a variety of puzzles that increase in complexity is similar.

Sensorium is unique in the way it organizes puzzles along the 5 senses. Each sense has an entire area devoted to puzzles which include pattern matching, sound matching, logic gates, block sliding, and gathering visual and aural clues. There is even a maze-like segment which I took great pleasure in solving. I took copious pages of notes along the way which helped me at the end, when clues were referenced in some creative combinations of puzzle mechanics.

I am not a fan of just doing puzzles for the sake of the exercise. In this game, however, Tad Cordle has made solving puzzles a journey of discovery. Each solution opens a new doorway or reveals a new clue and over time, the entire landscape becomes accessible.

The environment is colorful and interesting,, with multi-stored buildings, trees, underground caverns, parks, workshops, and a power station. The visuals are complemented by an original sound track that is a pleasure to listen to. The game saves on exit at the exact point at which you leave a puzzle so rework is never required. There are achievements for completing each major section (sight, sound, taste, touch, and smells) in addition to a number of 'secret' achievements that will keep you playing beyond the basic tasks.

All in all, I highly recommend this game to puzzle lovers who enjoy the unfolding of an experience, without dialog or characters, and with mere suggestions of a back story.

I should add that the developer has been highly accessible during the launch of this game, giving hints and answering questions on the discussion board. I did not encounter any bugs but, if I had, I am confident things would have been resolved very quickly.

Sensorium is a puzzle game based around the five classical senses. Obviously, because Puzzle Solutions Are Spoilers™, discussing how the puzzles work without giving too much away is a difficult task for any reviewer. So I'm going to have to give away a couple of things to continue proper.

After the short starting area, which is mostly about interfacing with logic gates, you're dropped into a hub with five areas; each one representing a different sense. The sound area is about memorising sequences of sounds and playing them back; the sight area is about observing your surroundings; the touch area is about sliding block puzzles; and the other two areas, taste and smell, I won't be giving away here, but are both appropriately themed.

Now, as I've described so far, the game sounds absolutely boring. And indeed, an inept developer who just wanted to churn out a quick game could have stuffed the game with hours upon hours of the same puzzle, over and over again.

However, what sets Sensorium apart from the bland games with the same puzzles, puzzle-wise, is the wonderful twists on the puzzles. Just when you think "yeah, I know how this works", the game throws another ingredient into the mix. Like Calvinball, it never does the same thing twice. To take an example, the sound area first has you listening to sequences of 3 different sounds, and play them back on a 3-note keypad. Then, comes a twist; you have to associate a cat's meow with a sequence of sounds! Then, another twist; now you have to listen to two sequences at once! Then, another twist; now you have to move in a direction depending on what sound you hear! And so on. The interactions between puzzle mechanics are clever.

The problem with a short puzzle game like this one is that I can't go in-depth enough with them without spoiling things. To say too much more about the puzzles would negate the purpose of this review; to convince you to buy the game and play it. So let's talk about the rest. The art style is vivid and cel-shaded, all things I like to see in a videogame. The music is nice. There's environmental storytelling. There's secrets as well to explore.

Look, are you *really* just going to ignore this recommendation from the person who singlehandedly raised the sales of Recursed by an immeasurable amount and whose review was cited in a mathematical paper? Come on, read my other reviews; I have good taste in puzzle games, and I like this one, so you know this one's good (unlike Rolling Shapes and Soko Match, which are both ♥♥♥♥♥). Then buy this game and play it, because it's good and well worth your time and money.

Overall I thought the game was great, and easily worth the asking price. Of course, it's nowhere near as good as The Witness, but that's an impossibly high bar to pass. If you're looking for a good "The Witness" clone, check this one out.

The graphics here are pretty good, especially for a team this size. As you can see in the screenshots, it's overall low poly, and I think that works perfectly well. The biggest issue I had with the graphics was that the SSAO was cranked a little high for my taste, but, what you see is what you get.

Performance
I didn't encounter any issues with performance or bugs in my playthrough. Windows 11 laptop.

PRO

CONTRO

Assolutamente non adatto a chi non ama grosse sfide d'intelligenza. Musiche belle ma un po' troppo ripetitive. Non è un "colossal" come The Witness ma forse non voleva esserlo.

TL:DR: While it has some issues, this game is a short, rewarding experience you should definitely buy it.

While it never quite reaches the level of The Witness, Sensorium is still a pretty good experience. The puzzles were rewarding, with only a couple making me stop in place and give up. The fact that this game apparently only sold ~300 copies is a damn shame, as I think it's worth more than what it asks for (seriously dev, don't be afraid to grab that bag). That said, I do have some issues:

  • The shadows are very low-res and stick out like a sore thumb against the colorful, cel shaded world (you can actually see this in the screenshot of the tree). Given the game takes up a whopping 1.29GB, I would happily "pay" the performance cost for some higher quality shadowmaps.
  • The cel shading is a bit too much for my tastes, and in some cases negatively impacts puzzle design. There is a puzzle where you have to pay attention to the geometry of small objects, and the heavy black outline, coupled with the shadows, makes them very hard to distinguish between them. While it's not as bad as antichamber, it gets close.
  • The world feels too big, and the puzzles aren't designed to accommodate that. Most of the puzzles take place in a small/large room/building that's lit/unlit, with a lot of the striking visuals located in the connecting paths and central hub. I would've liked a smaller but dense world to walk around in, like in The Witness. However, you can bunnyhop pretty far in this game, which fun to do.
  • Some settings won't save after starting the game again, essentially resetting to the defaults. The dev said they fixed this, but I encountered it a couple times myself.

Other than that, I had a great time playing, and plan to finish the endgame content. Performance was solid throughout playing at 4K max settings, with only minor dips due to VRAM usage (I think). For more info, see the PC Gaming Wiki article[www.pcgamingwiki.com] I filled out.
