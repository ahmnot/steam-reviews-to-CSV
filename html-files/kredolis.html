Doch nirgendwo lässt sich eine Menschenseele finden. Die einzige Spur führt zu einem Leuchtturm, aus dem ein Rauchsignal aufsteigt. Dieses Setting klingt äußerst vielversprechend. Insbesondere wenn man bedenkt, dass das Spiel über einen Zeitraum von sieben Jahren von einem Einmann-Studio entwickelt wurde. Jedoch wird schnell klar, dass die Insel ziemlich klein ist und die Rätsel nicht gerade anspruchsvoll sind. Vor allem die Rätsel aus der Zeit von Atlantis, die ich persönlich am interessantesten fand, sind - abgesehen von einer Ausnahme - viel zu einfach und lassen sich in wenigen Minuten lösen. Anders sieht dies bei den Rätseln aus, die das Forschungsteam auf der Insel hinterließ. Diese sind vielschichtiger und benötigen mehr als einen flüchtigen Lösungsansatz. Das Problem ist nur, dass diese Rätsel teilweise deplatziert und unvollständig wirken. Fast so, als hätte der Entwickler noch weitere Ideen gehabt, diese jedoch nicht implementieren können.

Doch abgesehen von den Rätseln ist Kredolis ein Meisterwerk. Der Stil orientiert sich zwar stark an The Witness, weist aber auch genügend Alleinstellungsmerkmale auf. Für 19,99 € ist dies leider ein kurzes Vergnügen und ein überzeugendes Argument gegen den Kauf.

Daher kann ich das Spiel auch nicht uneingeschränkt empfehlen. Es ist definitiv zu einfach für eingeschweißte Myst-Fans und zu teuer für Spielende, die einen Einstieg in das Genre suchen. Vor dem Kauf sollte einem bewusst sein, dass man statt knackigen Kopfnüssen und langanhaltendem Spielspaß eher die siebenjährige Entwicklungszeit und eine potenzielle Fortsetzung finanziert. Wer dies akzeptieren kann, sollte für den Vollpreis zuschlagen. Alle anderen sollten lieber auf einen kräftigen Sale warten oder sich das kürzlich erschienene Rätselspiel Taiji anschauen, das für fast denselben Preis wesentlich mehr bietet.

Évaluation publiée le 16 avril 2023
"Kredolis" is a very short (about 3h) first-person adventure game with beautiful low-poly graphics, a somewhat interesting but very derivative story, and fairly nonsensical puzzles that can only be solved through trial and error.

1. Story & Setting
You are shipwrecked on an idyllic small island that sports various buildings and machines from an unknown ancient civilization (which soon turns out to be Atlantis), as well as modern installations left behind by a research team that has since vanished. You learn more about the ancient civilization's history, as well as the fate of the research team, through documents that you can collect.

Neither story arc makes much sense. The "ancient history" arc is full of anachronisms (like Roman numerals on machines that were built thousands of years before Rome existed), includes obvious references to other IPs like Stargate, and generally feels like the writer slapped together various tropes from Myst-like adventure games, Greek mythology, and pseudo-historical TV shows. Not every game needs to have a fresh and original story, but this one felt rather trite and derivative. The gradual unveiling of the events that led to the civilization's demise was skillfully done, though.

The "modern" story arc seemed to make a bit more sense at first, but ends in a terrible twist that doesn't at all fit the information that was conveyed before.

I did get the feeling that the writer poured their heart into this story and wanted to create something reminiscent of the games and TV shows they loved - but while there's definitely some talent here, my impression is that they did perhaps not collect much critical feedback before putting it in a game.

2. Gameplay & Mechanics
Gameplay consists mostly of exploring the island and solving puzzles. The exploration is limited due to the small size of the playable area, but the environments are beautiful and show a decent amount of variety, and there are enough documents to collect to make it interesting.

The puzzles are partly inventory-driven and partly environmental, often with switches that need to activated in a certain order or in specific combinations. They start out with very simple and obvious small tasks that contribute to larger puzzles. I found the beginning quite promising, but as the game goes on, the puzzles unfortunately grow increasingly nonsensical. Mid- and late-game puzzles are often impossible to solve without trial-and-error - you may get a hint that gives you a good idea on how to solve the puzzle, but that hint can often be applied in a number of ways (like a pattern that could be applied in 4 different orientations), and you get no indication as to what variant is the correct one, so you have to keep repeating steps until you hit the solution. The puzzles generally don't give any feedback that would help you understand them better - either your solution is correct and the game progresses, or it is incorrect and nothing happens.

The end result are puzzles that either feel either obtuse (if you don't understand what you need to do) or like a chore (if you understood what you need to do, but have to go through various iterations to find the solution). My impression is that the game lacked playtesting prior to release, almost all of the endgame puzzles could have been designed better.

3. Graphics & Presentation
The game's environments are rendered in beautiful, crisp, bright, colorful, stylized low-poly graphics with a good amount of variety, they are by far its strongest point. Shadows are often highly detailed (though the grass shadows look oddly pixelated in some areas), and the water shader looks amazing, reflections look very good as well. Moving parts in environmental puzzles are convincingly animated.

The sound effects fit the respective action and are usually of high quality, they help bringing the environments to life. They were implemented with good attention to detail, such as footsteps on various different surfaces, or reverberation in underground tunnels. Music is used extremely sparingly, which was fine with me - listening to the well-done ambiance conveyed the atmosphere of solitary exploration better to me than music could have done.

The notes and documents you find are fully voice-acted, and while the acting has its flaws, it does enhance the overall experience. Sometimes the lines feel a bit too much like narrators reading from a book, and the intonation could have been better to make the lines sound more natural, but for an indie game with a very limited budget, the result is pretty decent.

4. Usability & Accessibility
The game has the usual selectors for resolution and screen mode, toggles for vsync and subtitles, and three presets for visual quality. While there are no detailed graphics settings at all, the game does have an FPS limiter and an FOV slider, which are an important features for many gamers that often get overlooked. Two volume sliders (for ambiance and effects) are offered, but there is no separate slider for the voice volume.

Keyboard controls can be fully rebound, and a mouse sensitivity sliders is present as well as a toggle option for running. It's great to see a one-person indie studio take so much care to provide a good user experience, this is something that even triple-A studios often mess up.

You can save the game at any time (but not at all in the last sequence of the game). Three save slots are available. The save/load interface is implemented in a bafflingly confusing way that makes it easy to confuse "save" and "load" functions.

Unfortunately, I encountered a game-breaking bug right before the end of my playthrough. After arriving at a lighthouse, my character suddenly started spinning around uncontrollably, which made it impossible to play on. I saved, relaunched the game, and reloaded, but the game then placed me in an underwater area far away from the lighthouse. Since the only way to get to the lighthouse is via a tram, and the tram was already at the lighthouse, with me being back on the island with no way of recalling the tram, I had no way of getting back to the lighthouse and had to start a new game from scratch. The developer claimed to have fixed this in the last patch (Oct 22), but it still exists.

In another unfathomably terrible move, the developer decided to DELETE the player's savegame after finishing the story. There is no way to go back, look for documents you may have missed, re-listen to the story, hunt for achievements you may have missed, or just enjoy the beautiful environments. For any of that, you have to start a new game and do all the puzzles or find all the documents again.

At first the developer seemed very active, very interested in bug reports, and submitted patches that fixed some issues. However, they have shown no activity for several months now, and severe problems like the aforementioned game-breaking bug still exist. From their forum posts, it feels like they were very motivated but ultimately unable to fix these issues, grew disappointed, and eventually ceased communications.

5. Conclusion
I really hate to give this game a thumbs-down. From what I gather, this was a passion project of a single developer (with some help), a long-term project to turn their dream of creating a Myst-like game into reality. They showed great skill in creating beautiful environments, but most of the puzzle design is obtuse and lackluster, the derivative story doesn't help either, and fundamental flaws like a gamebreaking bug or the auto-deletion of savegames have never been fixed.

I'd be very interested in seeing what this developer could do if they worked in a team, with others taking care of the story and puzzle design. Or perhaps in another game by this solo developer, in which they don't repeat the mistakes that were made here. But sadly, I cannot recommend this one - though I'd say it's worth a few bucks on a sale for the environments alone.
