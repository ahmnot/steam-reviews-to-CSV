I can see this being one of those games that polarizes people - you're either the type of person who likes this kind of game or you're not. And if you're not, you're probably really going to dislike it.

I love games where I can explore, at my own pace, a place that has a lot to see and experience. I also like puzzles to an extent, and the puzzles in this were either simple (but somewhat meditative) or somewhat annoying (the circular ones). I didn't struggle too long with any of them, but I enjoyed some more than others, even if they weren't difficult. It felt like the point of the game wasn't the puzzles but more the experience as a whole.

And that for me is where this game shines. I found myself really drawn to the art style in the game, both in the illuminated murals you encounter as well as the surroundings. Also, the music and sound effects combine in a holistic way - it seemed that when I was selecting nodes in a constellation-style puzzle, for example, the pitches emitted changed with the background music. I even spent some time (which others might consider strange but I found enjoyable) turning one of the circles on and off repeatedly and just listening to how it interacted with the music. It became less about solving the puzzle and more about the experience around solving the puzzle. The artwork had an "ancient" feel, and yet it was crisply illuminated in an artful way.

There were some parts that put me off a little. For some reason the magenta-y flags in the first level had a slightly negative effect on me. (That was made up for in the next level with a lush environment reminiscent of "The Witness" in its style and colorfulness.) I also found the parts where the music picked up vocals to be slightly off-putting, as it made it less ethereal and felt like someone had just picked a track to go along with the game rather than crafting music for it. (I must admit, though, that I did enjoy it when the reasoning behind the lyrics "lift me up" became clear.)

There were only two glitches I found in my play experience. The first involved the onscreen left/right mouse icons the first time a circular puzzle is encountered in Galileo. I suspect nobody had ever tried what I had. Leave it to me not follow the trodden path. The other minor thing is that when I went back to start a new game (and I did), the text said I would "loose" my current game progress. Clearly, that should be "lose". I have heard some asking if video games can be art. I have experienced that with some, those games that evoke an emotion. This was one of those games for me. If you're expecting a tough puzzler or a fast-paced adventure, then this won't be your game. If you enjoy taking your time to take in the sights and sounds of this game, you'll be in for a pleasant treat. It only lasts maybe 90 minutes or so, but it's well worth it.
