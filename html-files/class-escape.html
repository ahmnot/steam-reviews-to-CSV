For more puzzle game reviews, news and everything puzzle-related, follow Puzzle Lovers and check out our Steam group.

school's out for summer and forever if you manage to finish class escape. typical 1st-person escape room without enemies or combat per se, unless chemistry or gym can be considered enemies (they can). there's also a creepy pumpkin man in the last room, following you with its eyes and teleporting next to you after each solved puzzle. it's harmless and doesn't move otherwise, so it's more of an unnecessary nuisance.

there are clocks everywhere but no actual time limit and they're not all that intrusive, but a toggle to turn them off would be nice. manual saving supposedly anytime, actually it barely saves anything and it's only possible to 'save & quit', wasting a lot of time. you always spawn in the tutorial room, have to walk back to where you were before, and unfinished puzzles get reset. big fat no to that. no autosaves or multitple save slots either.

controls aren't rebindable. besides wasd/arrow movement there's shift to run, 'c' to zoom in on something (look further, as the game calls it), plus the usual mouse-look and left click to interact. no inventory, nor a need to zoom out with right click, as puzzles work without closing in on them. in fact, you can operate things from further away, which is both weird and welcome, as otherwise it'd often be impossible to take in the whole picture, and sometimes it still is. with better puzzle design and layouts it could've been avoided entirely. the cursor is barely visible most of the time and that's the only indication that something's interactable or not. except it also highlights stuff that's not active anymore. esc brings up the pause menu and closes it.

there are 5 main rooms, plus the one you start in, a secret one with some behind-the-scenes info, and the corridor itself also houses things to tackle, including the exit. puzzles are varied and some are pretty hard, sometimes overly convoluted, and a few involve despicable timing. right in the first proper room there's a maze that resets every time the ball hits a wall. it would probably be less frustrating if buttons were 'hold to move', not 'click to move and click other direction to stop', but why even include things like this? it's like adding platforming sections to a point & click adventure (which is not unheard of, incidentally). there's another that flashes things in various places after pulling a lever at the other end of the room, gotta keep repeating it until you get all the necessary info.

I don't necessarily want a cohesive (or any) narrative in a game like this, solve the puzzles, get out, but while each room has a theme, it really felt like a totally random collection of puzzles that mostly weren't satisfying to solve. look at the wall, there are some images, arrange them like so, ding (or not even that). what did that have to do with anything? nothing, just a random puzzle, one of x in the room, do them all and move on. maybe the school setting had this effect subconsciously, though it didn't feel like a real school or schoolwork at all. turns out, it takes more than a few puzzles to make an engaging and enjoyable puzzle game, especially if most puzzles aren't that great.

horse rotation is another example. it involves trying to figure out what the game accepts as north-east or west. 8 directions would've been enough, the puzzle is figuring out where they're facing, finicky degree-by-degree trial & error until you find the sweet spots is just lame. that said, I enjoyed some of them, like the main one in the chemistry lab (much to my surprise) and some of the deductive stuff. not much else, to be honest.

another annoyance is the narrator occasionally commenting on your puzzling, a'la henry stauf in the 7th guest, except this guy is irritating as ♥♥♥♥. at least he can be muted. meaningless and buggy collectibles are also a thing, in the shape of 34 owl statues, only 33 of which can be found, sometimes shoved into places they wouldn't normally fit. the pause menu shows how many you've found.

not that it matters much, but it's evident from the store page that the game is simply ugly and the player's shadow is always in the way. graphics settings don't make much of a difference, ultra looks just as unpleasant as very low, but lower detail gets rid of shadows at least. there's some similarly bad narration, as I already mentioned, a couple of language options, but no subtitles for said narration. separate but buggy volume sliders that reset every time the game starts, resolutions, windowed mode, mouse sensitivity (only while playing, not in the main menu for some reason) and a skip intro toggle. I wouldn't mind brightness settings and mouse axis flip toggles as well, but the dev told me he's not interested in things like that. understandable, as a developer the last thing you should do is improve the quality of your games...

even disregarding my totally valid 'this just isn't fun' feelings, the game needs more polish and quality of life, a properly working save system, plus a different, less generic title that isn't easily confused with another escape room game released recently. 'trapped in school', literally the first second in the trailer, would've worked. and these are on top of not including mandatory arcade 'puzzles' and so many uninspired fillers seen a million times. there are much better escape rooms around, don't waste your time on this.
