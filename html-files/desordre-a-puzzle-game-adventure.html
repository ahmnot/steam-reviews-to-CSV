OK, so a few things first.

Now, for the problems.

Slopes were a constant problem for me. There were times I had to restart the level because I got stuck on the bottom of them and couldn't get out. That was frustrating.

Overall, my personal score of DESORDRE would be a 8/10. My enjoyment far outweighed its downsides and the atmosphere, sound, game design, voice actress, and toilet jokes all combine to make a truly therapeutic experience.

Just finished the game with a playtime of 2.7 hours, as an obvious Portal-like, for the price and what it is it’s quite good. Looks gorgeous with Unreal Engine 5, performance is perfectly fine with DLSS Quality and max settings at 1080p on my 3060ti. Music is serviceable, fine but not standing out. The story is bare-bones and serves as a setting for the world more than anything else and is pretty tropey for this genre- Superluminal and The Spectrum Retreat come to mind as far better versions of this premise, and with actually good endings. None of those of course hold a candle to Portal 2 in storytelling, but gameplay is quite good in all, and while not being as smooth builds off some of Portal’s basic mechanics in some creative ways.

Speaking of gameplay, the puzzles are generally well-designed. Other than the blatant Portal 2 inspiration, it reminded me of Portal: Reloaded, as well as the time-travel section of Titanfall 2. It’s possible to get stuck on some slopes, requiring a reset of the level, which is easy to do and generally not a setback, but ideally of course this shouldn’t have to happen. It’s also possible to glitch the momentum mechanics any time you have a wide portal surface on the ground, by holding shift and jumping from portal to portal with ever-increasing momentum, allowing some of the later levels to be bypassed entirely where careful puzzle-solving was intended to get the momentum required. I didn’t discover this until towards the end of the game, so I feel that I got the general experience as intended, but it’s something that may need to be investigated. There were a few other times where I feel as though I solved the puzzle in a way it wasn’t meant to be done, for example the one with the wide room and the two laser cubes, where instead of using any of the moving platforms I just pointed the second laser across the room and finished it that way. I’m not a game dev, but it doesn’t seem like it’s good level design to have a bunch of testing elements present that either are meant to be unused, or- far worse- can be circumvented entirely. Lastly, I didn’t discover that you could sprint until maybe half-way through the game, which would have been good to know before platforming segments.

This isn’t a very polished review, but the TLDR is if you want a short experience reminiscent of other FPS puzzle games with a gorgeous art style, this is worth a try, as long as you don’t mind unpolished aspects from time to time, and occasional uncertainty as to whether you finished a puzzle correctly. I enjoyed my time with this game.

I tried it on a Steam Deck first, then on a beefy PC. Although it does run on Steam Deck, the main quirk (universe jump) causes a short lag, making it annoying to play. On a PC, there are multiple other issues that put me off this game.

1. It's full of jumps and portal flings, but the controls are floaty. There's no precision in your movement and you have to fight the inertia way too much. If you fall down while aiming at a portal, you're not "pulled" into it, like in Portal. The game requires unreasonable amount of precision for what the controls offer.

2. The puzzle design is lacking. You can easily get stuck at the bottom of a fling tunnel (room 3), you can move the cube to the wrong dimension (room 2) and can't go back. You need to use the restart button to start from scratch.

3. The art direction is interesting in the first few seconds, then it gets annoying. You have to hunt for interactive elements, because they blend in with their incredibly busy environment. As a result, you're not solving a puzzle, but look for a puzzle to solve. Or the elements are hidden behind a gate for some reason, and you need to find them first. In this regard it's similar to Relicta (weirdly hidden cubes).

4. This is the first time a game has caused my monitor to audibly buzz when the game runs. I have absolutely no idea how a game can interfere with hardware, but here we are. It doesn't even stop buzzing when I minimize it, it only stops when I run it in a window in 1080p, so one quarter of what my machine can handle in more demanding games. There's something wrong with it and I'm afraid to even run it now.

5. Returning to main menu takes 30 seconds. How?

In short, DESORDRE aims to be a puzzle platformer with two realities you jump between, but it fails at puzzles and at platforming, and the universe jump can cause lag. Maybe it can work for nice screenshots when you enable all the eye candy... but that's really all this title has to offer.
