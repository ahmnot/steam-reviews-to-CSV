Incredibly self indulgent and pretentious.

Insightful and rewarding.

I'm a big fan of art games, but this one falls into the pitfall of being so desperate to have a meaningful and emotional narrative, that it stumbles over itself trying to stuff a dramatic story into a vehicle that could stand on its own.

As a puzzle game, it is creative and beautiful. The visuals are stunning, the mechanics are interesting and rewarding. The "game inside the game" of Recursive Ruin is a wonderful game that explores concepts of infinity, futility, repetition and even the agency we have in life itself.

Outside the puzzle game there is a wrapping of hyper dramatic, overwrought meta game with terrible voice acting. These games want so badly to be the next "braid" so they chock their story so full of trauma and emotional turmoil that it becomes as shlocky as a soap opera. All the tropes are there, drugs, domestic abuse, will-they-or-won't they relationships.

And of these are told with glacial pacing through AUDIO LOGS, Some of them exceeding five minutes long.

If they could loose the meta-drama on top of the game, There would be a beautiful puzzle game, with a fantastic (albeit repetitive) soundtrack.

The internal game of Recursive Ruin already explores powerful themes and ideas, and is entirely spoiled it's own instance on over-humanizing it's own drama.

I would rather spend an hour hearing the pseudo-metaphysical babble of Anwynn and The Machine than put up with the 2 minute audio drama about the time Pa got high on meth and hit Ma with his belt.

A beautiful masterpiece, spoiled by a trite framing device.

Perhaps this paradox is intentional.

In the spirit of Recursive Ruin, I am in a quantum state of simultaneously Recommending/Not Recommending this game. I will periodically return to this review and switch it between a positive or negative review - because that is truly what the game deserves.

ГЕЙМПЛЕЙ
Игровой процесс представляет собой головоломку с поиском выхода из рекурсивного лабиринта с постепенным раскрытием сюжета.

Рекурсия - определение, описание, изображение какого-либо объекта или процесса внутри самого этого объекта или процесса, то есть ситуация, когда объект является частью самого себя.

Начинаем, насколько понимаю, в комнате героя. Здесь можно немного раскрыть личность нашего персонажа путём осмотра вещей и размышлений протагониста о них.

В комнатке знакомимся с колоритным говорящим котом - Бегемотом (слизали у классика).

Ну а дальше начинается форменное безумие. Герой оказывается в странном месте, которое становится постоянно еще более странным. Внутри ждут необычные существа.

Место в котором герой оказывается всегда рекурсирует, т.е. это что-то типа бесконечной ленты Мёбиуса, чтобы продвинуться дальше единственный выход - с неё сойти. Для этого есть несколько инструментов:

- Имеется что-то типа выключателей, "нажимая" которые меняется местная остановка и, соответственно, появляются новые проходы. Для нажатия на "выключатель" надо кинуть особым кубиком в особый глаз. Часто этот процесс сопряжён с разными хитростями, когда надо кубик куда-то ловко закинуть или где-то оставить.

- "Сдвиг". Это что-то типа выворачивания мира наизнанку с поиском новых образовавшихся возможностей для продвижения.

- Есть опция уменьшения героя. Т.е. увидев маленький проход (что-то типа как в "Алиса в стране чудес") можно туда забраться и исследовать его с применением всех имеющихся возможностей.

Понравился тот момент, что разработчики не поскупились на размеры пространств. Очень много падений с высоты, разных огромных конструкций, по которым надо лазать. Да ещё и "сдвиг" приводит эти массы в движение, полностью перекраивая мир.

По ходу лабиринта раскиданы подсказки (выполнены в виде особых кругляшей), которые призваны помочь разобраться в механиках.
Странный тип.

If you squint you can definitely make out what they wanted to do, but it's difficult to get past the incredibly tropey story and dreadful voice acting. There were several points that I genuinely thought it was a parody.

If you are looking for a geometrically complex puzzler like Antichamber, Manifold Garden, or even Maquette, this will not be satisfying.
If are looking for environmental storytelling as in Gone Home or What Remains of Edith Finch, you will find is sorely lacking.

Mechanics
  • Recursion: Every level in this game is recursive, meaning that each level contains itself and you can loop infinitely. If you have played Maquette, you know that can lead to some interesting interactions with the environment. However, unlike Maquette, every time you recurse into or out of the level, you shrink/grow to maintain your relative size. This might not be so bad if the game didn't abruptly interrupt your movement while changing size, which is incredibly jarring and ripped me right out of any sense immersion I had.

  • Shifting: You can change how the recursed version of the level is positioned within itself, toggling between two states. This proves quite interesting as it significantly changes how the level interacts with itself and how you travel around it.

  • Shrinking: you can shrink yourself. This one feels rather out of place and just shoe-horned in. You will come across tiny doors that you need to shrink to get though.

Puzzles:
The levels are comprised of reskinned button-and-door puzzles, pretty standard stuff. None of it was particularly difficult.

Visuals:
This is where the game really shines and what I enjoyed about it the most. Many of the levels are visually quite stunning, and I often found myself just looking around and exploring the space, though you aren't rewarded for this.

Story:
The story is about a tortured artist whose father abused him and his mother. He fails to maintain a relationship with his partner and everyone else is more successful than him.

It is important to note that you navigate this story entirely by walking around your minimalist apartment clicking on the items, listening to yourself talk about those items, and listening to audio logs ("memories") that get up to 5 minutes long and play over blurry AI generated still images. This gets really old after awhile. The puzzles are mixed in between these sessions to keep you entertained.

Achievements:
All achievements were guaranteed by playing through the whole thing.

Recursive Ruin is a first-person narrative-driven puzzle game where the entire playable level is a giant fractal of itself shifted 0 or 180 degrees, so the level is bounded by the level itself again but rotated. Your special ability is to shift the world to perform another rotation both inside and outside, and you can also shrink yourself down to scale yourself as though you were on the outside level. (It makes sense when you play it!)

In terms of gameplay, think Antichamber, Manifold Garden, Portal, Superliminal. It's box-and-button puzzles, finding your way around, using your gimmick at the right time. Half the puzzle is figuring out the layout of the levels, and where it's bounded and where it loops. In between puzzles there's narrative, plot development, talking to characters and watching the story unfold. The difficulty ranges from putting a cube on a button, to learning to put the cube somewhere so you can pick it up later in a different way to put it on a button. The catch is in how you can change the level layout in order to press on, and it gets really mind-bending at times, which I love. It's AMAZING looking up past a tall tower and seeing it repeat and repeat getting bigger and further away off into the distance.

In terms of narrative/story, there are two stories going on at the same time: a "real life" narrative, and a "symbolic" narrative which is a metaphor for the protagonist's struggle in the "real" narrative. I won't spoil the details of course but - what frustrated me was how disconnected, I feel, the two narratives were. You have this beautiful, gorgeous trick with the levels as fractals and repetition and endless loops, but I don't feel any of that ties in with the story! Which is a much more down to earth, banal (not in a bad way), kind of standard indie story fare, you know? You have your depressed protagonist, their empty apartment, call and video logs of happier days gone by, friendship, conflict, isolation, the apartment gets shabbier over time, a huge setback, digging deep within to find strength, a resolution to start over, better, stronger - yes it's fine, I have no issue with it at all, but it has nothing to do with the higher-level loops and repetition and fractal themes we've got going on everywhere else! :D I find myself wishing they'd maybe replaced the "real life" narrative with something more strongly connected to these themes of repetition, "as above so below", "everything is the same thing" kinds of ideas the gameplay lends itself to. The only thing I found myself wanting is more spectacle, more setpieces that really show off this amazing technology. I wonder if it might be possible in the future to render more repetitions of everything, I think in the game the repetitions are limited to maybe 4, 5, 6? Still amazing but I'm imagining no limits. I'm guessing the reason for the limitation is both for performance reasons and to make it less confusing separating the level from the background.

By far my most favourite thing about this game is the visuals and how they did the recursion. That by itself doesn't make a game great; you need good gameplay to drive the visuals, and good narrative to give you motivation to play onwards; and I think this game gets 2/3 of those down solidly. El juego no presenta ningún problema al introducir las mecánicas y es muy bueno explicándolas.

Yendo al apartado de la historia, he de admitir que en un principio es difícil de entender, no puedo poner mucho sin hacer ningún spoiler, así que me voy a limitar a decir que la historia se cuenta a través de recuerdos en formato de audios y algunos diálogos entre personajes. Si puedo decir que en cierta parte es atrapante querer ir desentrañando todo lo que sucede para poder entenderlo, así que por ese lado no tengo ninguna queja y esta todo bastante bien.

El apartado artístico, claramente lo principal y mas llamativo del videojuego, es algo bastante bonito y muy bien logrado, los mundos son todos interesantes y algunos hasta imponentes, un detalle a tener en cuenta (detalle que se advierte al inicio del juego), es que dada a la rareza visual de los mapas y el movimiento de los mundos requerido para resolver los puzzles, puede llegar marear y hasta incluso provocar dolores de cabeza. Las formas geométricas perfectas, unidas entre si para formar patrones caleidoscópicos infinitos esta perfectamente lograda y dan una sensación única al estar parados sobre aquellos mundos infinitos en bucle.

La banda sonora esta bastante bien, no tengo mucho para decir sobre esto, pero si que me llegaron a llamar la atención muchas veces las distintas canciones que suenan en el juego en los distintos puntos de la historia.

Ahora mi experiencia personal:
Recursive Ruin es... Algo difícil de explicar...

Sin dudas una experiencia diferente a cualquier otro videojuego que yo haya jugado, tanto de puzzles como videojuegos en general. Me hizo pasar por muchos sentimientos y emociones, porque uno puede llegar a verse reflejado en alguna de las tantas situaciones que le suceden a nuestro protagonista. Los puzzles son interesantes, no son difíciles pero tampoco super fáciles, en algunos hay que darse maña. Los personajes son bastante interesantes, siendo que casi siempre estamos hablando o escuchando hablar a "entidades cósmicas" por así decirlo.

Es un juego que me gusto bastante y recomiendo mucho, no esta nada mal para el precio que tiene y puede ser algo que sin dudas queda grabado en tus recuerdos. Sin contar que deja lindas vistas para buenas capturas!

I bought this game alongside Manifold Garden, so I went into this game expecting a first person puzzle game about recursion. This was the wrong way to play this game. I'm not a huge fan of narrative games like these. I've always been of the opinion that if I wanted to experience a story I would rather read it or play a visual novel type game. That's not to say that games like Recursive Ruin can't be good, it's just not the game for me and I did not care for the story at all (thank you devs for putting in a skip button). I did 100% complete the game. Though that doesn't take much as it only needs you to beat the game once to 100% it.

I can tell there was a lot of passion put into this project. The visuals are pretty stunning at times and the mechanics are pretty unique. That being said I had some major issues with this game.

First off, multiple times when talking to various NPCs the dialogue would just stop and I would have to click on them again. Why do this? Why not just continue the dialogue?

Second, there were times when the sounds in this game would suddenly become extremely loud and bass boosted. Almost turning into a screeching sound in my headphones. I had to turn down the volume a few times, just to to stop my ears from hurting.

Overall, I would say that this game is just not for me, nor would I recommend it. I do encourage others to try it, since I do believe others may enjoy this game, far more that I have. Recursive Ruin is a very impressive first-person puzzle game that's somewhat feels kind of like a mix between Myst and Portal. I will also mention, without spoilers, that the game does go to some very dark places with its narrative and its underlying theme is overcoming depression.

Final Thoughts

Overall, I really enjoyed Recursive Ruin. It's rare that I come across a puzzle game with so many puzzles that stump me because many games utilize a lot of the same puzzles just setup differently. This game felt incredibly new and refreshing because you have to utilize the shrinking and shifting abilities and not all of the routes you take are obvious. Definitely recommended
Puzzles: Fundamentally no particularly new ideas, but their combination with recursiveness and the fact they weren't frustratingly hard made them fun to do. I've had to stop and think about what I was trying to achieve multiple times and the fractal nature of the world made it even more confusing but in a good way. I can say I've never played a similar game before. My only complaint is that no puzzle felt like the final one. (9.5/10)

Extra gameplay mechanics: This game has two of them: shift and shrink. Shrinking is honestly not that interesting, though it facilitates a few puzzles. By the end of the game feels slightly forgotten. Shifting is probably the main thing the whole game is built around. It changes how the different layers of recursion are laid out relative to each other. This adds even more confusion to the already confusing recursion. Puzzles heavily using that were my favorite. (9/10)

Story: Honestly, I could do without it. I don't think it added anything to the puzzles but it also didn't take anything away. I'd prefer if the ending wasn't so story-focused but it provided enough resolution for me. (7/10)

Characters: I won't comment on the humans, except that none of them felt fake to me (because they're mostly connected with the parts of the story I didn't care much about). Now, the creatures, or whatever you want to call them, are interesting. They are presented as eldrich and mysterious enough to not feel completely human despite having a lot of human qualities. I can describe the interactions with them with one word: enjoyable. Didn't get enough shivers down my spine when speaking with them, though. (8/10)

Visuals: Easily one of the most visually pleasing games for me. Abstract art is my favorite and it's the main reason I even started this game and it scratched that itch, for sure. A lot of straight lines, sharp corners, bright colors, surfaces either flat or filled with fractals. I don't think I've ever taken more screenshots per hour than in this game. (10/10)

Sound: I've got nothing to complain about. The music always felt appropriate and the SFX satisfying. Another thing worth mentioning is distortion and modulation. The eldritch creatures' voices sometimes get more and more distorted and creepy. Though sometimes difficult to understand without subtitles I always enjoyed it. (10/10)

Overall, it was a great experience, worth the money, if a bit short (a full playthrough took me <5 hours). Would recommend it to anyone who enjoyed Antichamber or any fan of brain-teasing puzzle games, as the story can be entirely ignored. I wouldn't, and didn't, but there was nothing I would regret missing. If you don't care about the story, the puzzles are still great. Overall 9/10
PS: I've heard some people complain about headaches or nausea, I experienced neither myself but it may be something to watch out for.
