Let me start by saying that this game is as fun as it looks. The concept is simplistic, yes, but sometimes a simple core mechanic is all it takes to make a game good. Transpose by developer Secret Location pays homage to the classic puzzle games of the early internet, but this time in VR. Each level involves not only creating and interacting with past selves, but also changing the direction of gravity, making for increasingly difficult puzzles. In the later levels, when gravity changes frequently, it takes a few seconds to understand how the world has translated, but the strangeness of this makes it all the more satisfying piecing it all together in the end. The goal of each level – getting a cube or two into the proper receptacles – seems trivial and intuitive, but along with two strong core mechanics, Transpose provides some of the best VR puzzles I’ve seen.

I have very few complaints about this game on the whole. It does so many things flawlessly: directing the player by spawning “ghost echoes,” narrowing the field of vision during cutscenes to convey that the player’s location is locked, and other things so subtle you wouldn’t even know there was even a choice to be made because they feel so natural. All of the mechanics worked exactly as you would expect, focusing the player’s attention on the actual puzzle-solving aspect of the game rather than trying to get mechanics to work properly. In other words, I was focused on what to do rather than struggling to do it, which is crucial to a puzzle game like this one. I also have to commend the developers for really taking into account how a person would move in VR. Many VR games currently only have a head and hands, and sometimes even a body, but because you need to be able to interpret your past selves from a third person perspective, there was a need for more representation here, and it was done well. The legs, without tracking, are impossible to include, but one really cool feature was that the model of the player had arms attached to it which were (for the most part) where I would expect them to be. Having a scrollbar embedded into my left forearm and a set of bracelets on my right felt somehow more impactful than any other control mechanism I’ve seen in a VR game.

Although the game itself was made extremely well, I did have a few issues. The throwing mechanics, when needed, were extremely generous, but I still wish there was some way to repeat a throw without having to restart from the beginning if the cube falls in the wrong spot. While I applaud most of the controls of the game, the basic movement system took a bit of getting used to. When you pull the trigger, you walk in the direction you’re facing, rather than where the controller is pointed. Because of this, you can’t look around while moving without risking death. I was already stopping plenty often, so this wasn’t usually a problem, but it did sneak up on me occasionally.

On the whole, I think Transpose really nails its aesthetic. The way information is conveyed is extremely clever, and the puzzles themselves are fun and satisfying to solve. Some early puzzles don’t require using all of your available echoes, but as the difficulty ramped up, I found myself repeating my past moves just so I could cut down on iterations. Solving each level gave me a satisfying click (and sometimes even an audible gasp) as everything made sense and became instantly clear. This is exactly what I look for in a puzzle game. No level was too hard that I wanted to quit, but grinding through level after level was certainly a workout for my mind, and I took breaks often. Nonetheless, I would recommend this game to anyone who is a fan of the old in-browser Flash puzzle games or indie puzzle games as a whole. Transpose succeeds at translating this timeless genre into the new medium of virtual reality.

Final Rating: 8.5/10

Transpose is a mind-bending game which records your actions so you can use looped duplicates of yourself to solve puzzles.

The goal is to get special cubes into their bases, but of course the execution is much trickier than it sounds.

Whenever you begin a level all of your actions are recorded. After you’re done with your actions you then choose to discard or keep everything you’ve done.
After choosing that, you then begin the level anew and your actions are recorded again. If you chose to keep the previous recording, you’ll see it played out, and all the actions you did will still occur. The previous recordings are called Echoes.
Early on you’re limited to just a few echoes. But eventually you’ll be using up to 8 echoes at once.
Later in the game gravity will become relative to each echo, and the physics of transfering cubes between different gravity directions adds a new challenge.

I found the overall design of the game creative and refreshing, but especially with interfaces on your arms. On your left arm you use a slider to fast-forward time, which makes waiting for echoes less tedious. On your right arm each echo is represented by a ring. Highlighting a ring will show where that echo is, and pulling a ring will destroy that echo.

There’s sliding movement and teleporting, teleporting is required to cross gaps in almost every level.

The early version that I played only allowed you to slide forward, you couldn’t strafe or slide backwards. Which felt awkward at first but I eventually got used to it.

Based on my progress I estimate 8 hours of playtime. Personally I had a blast playing this. Transpose has become one of my favorite puzzle games. I highly recommend it.

過去の自分達と協力する面クリア型二度手間系VR主観視点パズルゲー。

■概要
リスポン用の台座から一歩でも踏み出せばそこからの行動が全て記録され、リスポンしてさっきとは違う行動をとっても先刻の行動をする自分をエコーとして残せる。

一辺20cm程のキューブを専用ソケットに嵌め込めばクリアなので、スライダを掴んで足場を動かす係、それに乗って向こう側でキューブを拾いこっちに投げ渡す係、スライダ係の横で待機してキューブを受け取り納品する係、などの分担作業を全部自分でやる。

ストアの表記上は着席でも遊べるとあるが、キューブの投擲はプレイヤー自身の身振り手振りで行うため、立ってやるほうが無難。投擲さえ上手ければパズルを無視して遠くのソケットに投げ入れてしまうことも可能。投擲技能が必須なのではとも思えるステージもあり。

またリスポン用の台座は床だけでなく壁やら天井やらあちこちにあり、重力も出現に使った台座の向きに依存する。一度手にとったキューブも持ち手の重力方向に倣うが、キューブが滞空中に記録を止めてリスポンするとキューブも宙空で止まる。


移動はかなりゆっくりなスムーズ移動と指定できる最大距離がやけに短いテレポートの併用。ロード時のTIPSまで「歩きとテレポート両方使ったほうが速いよ」と言ってくるので、歩きつつ前テレポを連打する感じになる。スナップターンは30度のみだが、アナログスティックを倒しっぱなしでリピート入力は可能。

なお日本語字幕をサポートしているが、「未来によって過去を変えられる」「世界は円環」「すべては循環している」みたいな思わせぶりテキストが出るだけだし、フル音声に関してはそもそも言語を問わずボイス自体が存在しないので嘘表記。

初回プレイでクリアまで6時間強。「ゲームをクリアする」の実績が一番グローバル取得率が低いので、普通にクリアするだけで実績は全部埋まるはず。

■感想
悪くはないんだが、決して良いとも言えない。パズルゲーなのに、パズルではない部分に悩まされてる気がしてならない。

例えば、子供向けお菓子のパッケージに書かれた迷路って別に難しくない。すぐ全体を見渡せるから。でも壁の向こうが見えない巨大迷路を自分の足で歩かされたら、迷路の構造が同じでも難しいと思う。移動も早くはないから、行き止まりと分かって分岐まで戻るのだって時間がかかる。

このゲーム、そんな巨大迷路っぽさがある。俯瞰できれば難しくないのに、地上視点がゆえに全体像が見えない。

あるステージでは中央に巨大なサイコロ状の足場が浮いていて、各面にリスポン用の台座があった。サイコロでいう1の目でリスポンした後、足元を掘り抜いた向こう側が6の目なのはわかる。でも前後左右どの地平線の向こうが3の目で、どこが5に繋がる縁なのかなんてサイコロの上に立ってしまうとわからない。

その状態で「2で仕掛けを動かすと5に反映される」ってことに気付くだけでも一苦労。重力方向を変えるには台座からリスポンし直すしかなく、その都度今自分がどっちを向いてるのかを把握するのにも手間がかかる。

レベルエディタだとつまんでぐりっと回転させたら2秒でマップ構造を把握できるんだろうが、こっちは主観視点なんだ。ステージ名を表示する時にその背景にちらっと全体像を表示しとくとかできたんじゃないのか。

パズル相手に苦戦しているのか、システム相手に苦戦してるのかわからなくなる。

I just finished playing through the whole game. As someone who prefers VR platforming and puzzle games rather than shooters, I enjoyed the experience for the most part, and would say this as a pretty good game with some flaws. I would have happily paid for it, and recommend it to anyone who enjoys room scale VR and problem solving.

The good

The game is designed around a time manipulation and replay premise, like you might have seen in Braid or The Talos Principle. Each level contains one or more starting locations and one or more slots in which you want to insert cubes. You pick a starting point, step out of it, do stuff, and then choose to either keep or discard your run. Kept runs all play in parallel, so you can do such things as throw cubes to your future self, or steal them from your past self, as well as manipulate platforms for your other selves. Later, different simultaneous gravity orientations are introduced depending on the starting location. These puzzle elements work well together and the levels are creative and interesting.

The implementation runs smoothly and is largely bug-free. I encountered a glitch on a couple of occasions that made me restart in my death location rather than my assigned start location, but a deliberate discard brought me back to the correct state. I really liked how I was able to "break" the intended solution in a few of the levels by outrunning moving parts of the level, ducking, teleporting to narrow ledges or jumping (no invisible walls). I was a little disappointed that some platforms rejected teleportation onto them for no apparent reason in later levels (or weren't solid), but it wasn't too big a deal.

I loved the controls. All sorts of movement and turning options are available. The ways to keep/discard runs, collect echoes and visualize existing runs are very intuitive and easy to use. There's a slider in the avatar's arm that speeds up time so you don't have to wait too long for your past selves to do their thing!

The ambience is great. The game looks good and sounds good. The vast scale of the hub worlds and the increasing fullness of the music as you progress through many of the levels were very well done.

I'm happy with the length of the game.

The less good

I have two significant accessibility-related qualms. They get in the way of solving the levels even when I already know the solution, which is a source of frustration and wasted time.

First, levels often don't provide enough visual cues about their layout. This is fine if the level is open, simple and/or all the features have a distinctive shape. But if the level is messy and contains repetitive geometry, I would have expected better landmarks and use of texture color and variety to help the more visually inept (such as myself) position themselves. The world 3 level "Enemy" is a perfect example of what I'm talking about--this level contains 8 rooms all of the same size, with 2-3 exits each, all with the same texture and all of which can be accessed from any gravity orientation. The developers themselves admit this level is confusing and provide a 17 step, extremely time sensitive solution. I can pull it off... But figuring out "carry one cube in each hand" or "you have a 3 second window to do this" type solutions on my own while completely lost is impossible for me.

Note: The developers do provide a standardized system of lines connecting cubes and the slots where they're supposed to go for more complicated levels, but it was insufficient in some of the levels.

Second, cube throws. The player is expected to throw cubes around, sometimes across large distances and around obstacles, aided by absolutely nothing but the physics engine and the player's own arms and hands. In my country we don't play baseball as kids (or ever), and throw things basically never. If the cube lands by my feet and I can try a bunch of times per run, that's not so bad. If it falls in the void and goes back to its now-unreachable pedestal, requiring me to discard and start over 15 times... Urgh. Obviously, cube throws are integral to the puzzles, but I feel like this problem could have easily been solved with some engine help. You could provide a throw slider that I can use to fire the cube away from my hand without using my own arm, and that displays a trajectory parabola while held.

Conclusion

Obviously the problems listed above didn't keep me from finishing the game. If you're patient enough to figure out the puzzles, you can make it to the end. I had to look up the solutions for 3? levels. Maybe an in-game hint system for the sequel! I would also enjoy a more varied soundtrack. (Quality's good, but more quantity is always fun.) I'll be waiting for more.

Ogólnie: gra logiczno zręcznościowa, której łamigłówki polegają na zabawie z czasem, grawitacją oraz tworzeniem własnych klonów [w czasie i przestrzeni]. Gra w swoim minimalizmie posiada monumentalnie zaprojektowane poziomy; spokojną muzykę oraz ciekawe mechaniki rozgrywki.

Złożoność łamigłówek / układanek wzrasta w trakcie gry: są trzy dostępne światy (huby); pierwszy jest całkiem łatwy, który zaznajamia nas z mechanikami gry; drugi jest już trudniejszy, wymagający i zmuszający do planowania działań kilka razy naprzód. Przejścia pomiędzy portalami (w hubach) są zbudowane z wąskich półek/dróżek zawieszonych wysoko w przestrzeni (nie ma dna!), a niektóre nawet są zrobione ze światła co potęguje efekt akrofobii (więcej o tym później). Oprawa dźwiękowa pasuje do gry, ponieważ również jest skromna i spokojna - przez całą grę obcujemy z jednym stylem muzycznym - mi osobiście jego monotonność nie przeszkadzała.

Przykład: stojąc przy krawędzi i rozglądając na około już nie dostaje zawrotów głowy tak jak na początku rozgrywki.

Transpose - World of Clones

Transpose is a puzzle game that uses clones, time manipulation and defying gravity to solve puzzles. The puzzles grow in difficulty as you progress, making you think outside of the box.

I have really enjoyed playing VR games where you get to make clones of yourself to complete tasks, or just have fun. This is one of the best clone creating games I have played.

The game is not just about the clones though. Its a puzzle game in an amazing upside down, twisted world. You just have to experience it, to know what I mean. The world will have you stunned at how some of the environments look. Some brilliant VR visuals.

The puzzles are also a real treat. Using clones, time and gravity to complete a task, will really leave you scratching your head and then coming up with a unique way to complete it. You start to learn tricks and master time and clone manipulation over time and learn how to do things more efficiently. Which will be needed, as the puzzles get more challenging as you move on.

Over all I had a blast playing the game, and found myself going back and starting a fresh game to try out new tricks I had learned on the earlier puzzles.

If you are looking for a challenging unique puzzle game, this is a hidden gem. A must have for the VR collection. If you are not to into puzzles, but like messing around with clones, this game will be fun as well.

Its a real treat to stand back, and watch your army of clones work to complete a job, a true VR experience.

Rough around the edges, but a satisfying puzzle experience. $10 is an appropriate price.

The concept is simple: You perform and record actions, then those actions are played back as you perform other actions. This is always in service of getting one or more boxes from point A to point B. You can have half a dozen past versions of yourself running around a stage, gravity being inverted, and it's the building blocks of a solid puzzle experience.

Pros:
I love the atmosphere of the game, such as the minimalist sound design. The strange geometry the world is made of does a great job preparing the player to lose all sense of "down". Gravity flipping and multiple time loops, all combined with required 3D spacial awareness are building blocks for insane challenges.

♥♥♥♥:
-As great as those building blocks are, I don't feel like they were used to their fullest extent. The most challenging puzzles -felt more like busy work than an actual challenge of sequence. It seems like the concept wasn't fully cooked.
-What was most annoying of all, were these art student quotes at the start of each level, and violent breaks in flow where control of your character is lost and your vision turns wide screen. They are annoying and entirely unnecessary. These moments broke all immersion.
-There were also times I'd accidentally grab the cores of my past lives and entirely ruin the chain I was building up to, and have to start all over. All I was trying to do was fast-forward. This happened to me a lot.

Conclusion:
Transpose annoyed me. It challenged me, it bored me. When you distill the game down, there's two moments that will stick with me: The first time I had to throw a box to myself in different gravity orientations, which blew my ever loving mind. Second, the last moment of the game, after spending an hour on the final puzzle, finally getting the door open, I stopped and played the entire winning timeloop. I watched six versions of myself running around completing tasks. Finally the last version of myself slamdunks that final box. I watched myself shoot both hands into the air and do a victory lap. I felt like a satisfied older brother to myself at that moment. That's going to stay with me the rest of my life.

I hope Secret Location creates a sequel to Transpose one day. There's a lot of room for quality of life improvements, as well as the untouched potential of what challenges this is already capable of. Hell, you can run a sequence using four lives, then delete the third life in that chain, and that mechanic as a puzzle's solution was never touched upon! The potential here is limitless.

Transpose is an awesome puzzle game, where each of your play is saved and re-played when you respawn.
So you can slowly build a solution on the shoulders of your past self, or anticipate and get in place, ready for your future self.

The sound track is pretty and soothing, at helping you to gather your thoughts.

The difficulty is well dosed, At the beginning most puzzle can be complete in 30 sec to 5 minutes. As the complexity increase with new elements (multiple orbs, gravity change, platforms, traps,...) as you progress through the game, levels can take from 5 min to 20 minutes to complete.

I got stuck, more than 30 minutes, only on 2 levels; Enemy and Dungeon. I found the most difficult puzzles to be the one with lack of orientation landmarks when using gravity changes.

A lot of level can be solved your way. If you are creative, have a good throw and not afraid to jump to weird space. Brute forcing a puzzle still feels as good as doing it legit.

The end levels feel really good to complete. Especially when you nearly have 0 echo left to finish your plans, but you still see all of your past self working together so your current self can put the orb in the socket.
In total, it took me 7 hours to reach the end. Making it a lengthy VR game, but a consistently good one.

If you play this game, never forget to wave at your future self before you respawn ! Nothing is better than a lot of cheers to accomplish great challenges.
