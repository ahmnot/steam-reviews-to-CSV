This game was reviewed using gameplay from its launch day version. Your experience on future versions may vary.

Short Answer:
A short game that experiments with the idea of a "2nd person" camera scheme, and does so very well. Doesn't quite push the far boundaries of its core idea, but shows that it absolutely has potential to grow. For just ~30 minutes of your time and the low price of free, it's hard not to recommend that you give this unique concept a shot.

Long Answer:
Live Adventure is a game that bills itself as a "2nd person adventure game", in which you follow the journey of two young siblings/explorers as they navigate a forest full of ancient ruins in order to find their missing parents. Unlike a "1st person" game where you see through the eyes of your character, or a "3rd person" game where you see through a camera that moves around the character, the main gimmick of the game is this "2nd person" concept that sort of blends 1st person and 3rd person together--and it does so surprisingly well. Using the twin sticks of a controller, you move both characters at the same time: the left stick controls the character the camera focuses on, and the right stick controls the character that's holding the camera. The control scheme takes a bit to get used to, but once you do, it makes for some very interesting gameplay. Most of the game revolves around solving platforming puzzles, where both characters can perform actions that the other can't, and you need to use their unique abilities to get past each other's limitations and get them both to the end. I'll be honest when I say that the game's puzzles are all pretty easy, but it's clear that their main purpose is to teach you the ins and outs of this new control scheme, and it does a solid job of doing so.

I had fun messing around with this game, but it wasn't without its problems. I'm currently writing this only a few hours after release, so I'm not expecting a flawless piece of software, but there are a handful of things that should be noted that might be of concern to you. I'll be keeping an eye on the game's progress for a while, so if something from this list gets patched out later on, I'll cross it off or delete it so you know it's not an issue anymore.

  • The game has no option to change resolution and will only run in fullscreen, so if you're using a display that doesn't have the standard 16:9 aspect ratio (such as my 21:9 ultrawide monitor), some of the UI will misalign and the display will cut off at the top and bottom. Your only option is to either live with it (which isn't a big deal, it's still entirely playable) or switch your computer's main display to another monitor.

  • The game will not save your data if you exit the program. This includes current game progress, photos taken using the in-game camera, or changes to the game's settings. I found this out while diagnosing the above display issue and lost about 10 minutes of progress. Until it's fixed, try to play the whole thing in one sitting.

  • There doesn't appear to be a way to remap controls, so the ones laid out for you are the only way to play the game. The default controls are fine for the most part, but it would've been nice to be able to, for example, remap the redundant trigger/bumper controls to the jump button so it was easier to move both characters and jump at the same time.

With these in mind, at most they served as a mild annoyance while I was playing, and none of them dissuaded me from finishing the game. Despite the issues, I do still recommend that you give this game a try.

It only took me about 20-30 minutes to complete my first playthrough, and I spent another 20 minutes or so poking around to try and get the Steam achievements. I will admit, given its short length, it ends up playing more like a tech demo than a full-fledged game, but I think that might be the point. Live Adventure feels like its main goal is to test the waters and see if this "2nd person game" concept can get some traction in the market, and I'd say that it passed that test with flying colors. It's an interesting idea that was handled very well, and it lays the groundwork for something bigger and better to be built on top of it. I really hope that this game gets more positive recognition so that, hopefully, one day we can see the Live Adventure Team take this idea even further, and really push the limits on what a 2nd person game can do.

An endearing student's game* that manages to mostly pull off a second person action-adventure. It's basically a proof of concept and about the length of a short demo, but during this the art and characters managed to endear it to me. I would hope to see a full-length game out of this.

*As in it's made by student game developers as a show that they can make a game.

+ Beautiful art direction, with lots of effort put into making the flora lush and diverse.
+ Endearing characters and simple enough story, I felt sympathy for them pretty quickly and was sad to see them go. While the writing isn't perfect, it is clear that a lot of thought and effort went into it.
+ An innovative idea that is well (albeit not perfectly) executed.

= The platforming won't be a big challenge. The only unusual aspect is the second person bit.
= The controls can be awkward, especially at first. It can be difficult if you are not used to joystick-based camera controls. But I managed and the game does not sections that really make it difficult.

- The writing being made by a non-native speaker and/or a bit of amateurish at times. "Bro" and "sis" happens more often than necessary, we got that the two are brother and sister from the intro with the photos, we do not need to be reminded of it in every conversation. Add to this that some of the dialog is rather awkward and there are mistakes like "datas" (the plural of data is data). Getting a professional native-speaking writer or editor for the dialog would help this, especially in making the dialog to be more natural (and yes, better delivery and/or direction on delivery would help).
- It's clear that there is a lot of dialog in the game and it can be hard to trigger it. It would be better if the triggering could be done manually (press shoulder button to investigate) rather than just waiting around. An "investigate" button or perhaps using the snapshot function to do it.

As for why second person: It's not a third person because a third person would be an passive, invisible camera that can move about like a ghost or rigged to watch over the shoulders of the protagonist. Instead, you have the viewpoint of an in-game character who is not only engaged in dialog but who you yourself have to move while controlling another character in a more classic platforming style.

Suggestions:
% Please allow minimizing or disable the overlay stencils (the transparent bars). It would be helpful to better see where you are and what is around you.
% Allow Reel to have reactions. I understand that for a student project there wasn't enough time to do such, but she feels awkwardly stiff when she isn't doing cutscenes. It feels silly that she is on camera constantly and she doesn't do stuff like pose or emote with her face or otherwise react to the environment.
% Give"Steve" more functions, as it feels silly that it hyped as a super-advanced camera but cannot do anything other than take snapshots and record video (I am guessing that interacting with ancient super-artifacts were not original functions). Why not things like night vision or "scanner" function or other things that would actually help the children? Or vision modes that can show the player what the characters themselves cannot see? It would lend credence why they chose to bring Steve along rather than on a whim to emulate their parents.
