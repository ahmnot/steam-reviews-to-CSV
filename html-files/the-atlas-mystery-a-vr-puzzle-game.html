It's a decent, short puzzle game. The story says the theatre is haunted, but outside of 2 story elements (and some oppressive ambiance near the beginning), I honestly forgot it was supposed to be haunted. Roughly 90 min long.

What I liked
+ Hands have physics colliders, along with most items. Being able to casually push junk around is a nice touch of verisimitude. Especially in a puzzle game where you can just trash the room if you get annoyed.
+ All the puzzles are interacted with physically, while not being physics based. (Except 1). I.e. You're plugging wires into different sockets, you're holding film negatives up to light, you're hand cranking dials, etc.
+ the puzzles are about medium difficulty. Enough to make you think for a minute or two, but not enough to get stumped on.
+ It's a fun 20s/30s settings and the music is good.

What I didn't like
- Picking up objects is awkward. There's a goldilocks zone where you can just grab things from a distance. But it never feels right? If your hand is too far, it won't work. (Which makes sense) But If your hand is too close, to the object, it won't work either. Furthermore, there's no natural alignment when holding objects, which isn't a big deal until you try to pick up a piece of paper to read have to contort your wrist to read it, or pass it back and forth between your hands to get to a good angle.
- There is a bit of an over-reliance on physics at times, causing some interactions to be more frustrating than they should be. For example, you have a grid of buttons and it's way too easy to fat finger multiple of them at a time. My hand got stuck inside of a trash can somehow? (in retrospect, that's hilarious)
- Objects you can interact with have a floating circle near them so you can tell what you can and cannot pick up. However, this circle always renders on top of everything, so if you walk up to a locked safe, suddenly 8 circles pop up.
- There's one "puzzle" that's just a physics based "get the ball through the maze" game. Combine with janky physics and/or overly sensitive VR controls and it's annoying as hell.
- There are a few safes that you have to open, but the textures on the dials is extremely low resolution and hard to read? Which is weird because other textures (like on notes) are easy to read.

What I'd like to see more of
= I like the backpack mechanic of stashing items in there, but there aren't any reusable items to really make use of it? I went the whole game with a screwdriver and only used it one time at the beginning.
= Make more junk interactable! It felt weird finding a table with a book and a matchbox on it, and being able to pick up the book and throw it, but not be able to pick up the match box!

Overall: It's good!

The AtlasMystery: A VR Puzzle Game by Top Right Corner is basically a story-based escaperoom.

As the title suggests, the game is set in an old 1940s cinema.

We are supposed to prepare the place for a premiere, but soon we are caught up in the mysteriously murderous past of the historic theatre. While we now try to find out what exactly happened in this cinema, we of course have to solve lots of puzzles.

A large part of the storyline is handled via notes that we can find everywhere. For the sake of immersion and atmosphere, it would have been nicer if some of this had been done via tapes or short films.
It is also a pity that we can only interact with relevant objects, everything else is just for looking at.
The VR-standard backpack serves as an inventory in case we want to keep one or more objects.

If we move through the spacious building by free movement or teleportation, we are always surrounded by a great acoustic soundscape, which contributes greatly to the slightly creepy atmosphere in the light house and is never annoying.
The graphics in The Atlas Mystery are rather adequate, but it has good physics. Our hands have real bodies and cannot reach through objects. Very good.

The puzzles we encounter in the cinema are all easy to decipher and solve. With one exception, there is nothing really new to discover. The puzzles don't always have anything to do with the somewhat flat story. In the end, The Atlas Mystery: A VR Puzzle Game is a short and simple escape room full of puzzles.
Unfortunately, the initially interesting story becomes weaker and weaker, and the ending is not only abrupt but disappointing.

The gameplay of The Atlas Mystery is solid, which gives it a thumbs up. It's probably best kept in the Sale due to its length.

The Atlas Mystery: A VR Puzzle Game is the latest VR escape room title to get added to the pile of similarly designed games in the genre.  It is one of those games that doesn’t necessarily require VR to exist; in fact, I think it might have done better if released as a non-VR title with better graphics and access to a much larger audience.    The VR implementation comes off as more of a gimmick and even a hindrance at times due to some unwieldy controls that affect both movement and interaction with the environment.

The one thing Atlas Mystery has going for it is presentation and atmosphere with its 40’s era architecture and art deco design elements that do a reasonable job of transporting you back to 1951 where you have just been hired as the manager of the Atlas Theater.  Your first day on the job not only requires you to find a way out of your locked office; you’ll also need to solve a murder mystery while exploring every square inch of the theater for clues, combinations, codes, and all sorts of items to solve a myriad of puzzles that stand between you and the truth.

The theater is sectioned off into a few main areas including the initial office that serves as a tutorial area before moving on to the concession stand, lobby, projection booth, and finally the main auditorium.  Each area is loaded with puzzles that require thorough examination of the area for instructions and hints at what to do followed by interesting interactions with those environments to complete the required tasks.  The game might be worth a look if it ever goes on sale, but I honestly don’t think this trip to the theater is worth the $15 ticket price.

Either the newest update of the game or updating to Windows 11 finally allowed me to play the game I bought about a year ago. If you experienced hands glued to the ground in the game, maybe this helps you, too, and maybe you want to buy it again if you refunded it for that reason - because the game is actually really good and for me worth the wait.

Great atmosphere, a captivating mystery to resolve and puzzles that are not too easy but also don't get you stuck for hours on end, all accompanied by a perfectly matching sound track.

Some minor nuisances with the controls and handling (not as smooth as some other similar games, seated position is too higher, standing position too low - means it's not a hundred percent comfortable to play), I would totally recommend the game.

It gets a bit creepy towards the end, but there are no jump scares - so nothing to worry about if this isn't your thing.

Took me about two and a half hours to complete, I don't think a first playthrough is possible in much less than one and half unless you're some sort of puzzle game prodigy. The puzzles may seem a bit artificial at first, that actually makes sense in the story of the game.

We are the new manager of the Atlas Theater. How hard can the first day be?

In The Atlas Mystery we are the new manager of the old Atlas Theater. The game takes place in 1951, and we are tied up in a supernatural murder mystery.

The game is an adventure, escape room game, with a deep story to unfold. You will be exploring the large Atlas Theater, running into escape room puzzles to move on. As you find clues to dig into the dark past of the Atlas Theater.

I have played many escape room games, and never felt so immerged into the world as with this game. From the amazing graphics and sounds, to the very interactive environment. The Atlas will draw you into its world. This is also one of the largest environments escape room games I have ever played. With smooth locomotion and turning and room scaling, you will feel like you are really in the Atlas. The game also supports snap turn and teleport, for those without VR legs yet.

The puzzles are fun and make you think, and will have you exploring for clues. The Atlas is a beautiful crafted world, and will have you breathless as you discover new areas.

Other escape room games I have played, have you confined to a small area. Then load you into a new level to solve. With The Atlas Mystery, you are in a large open seamless world to move around in and solve problems. I can not express how good it feels to wonder around in the Atlas, there is just so much to see and do.

If you like murder mysteries, exploring, escape room puzzles and interacting with the world, this is the next big escape room title. You do not want to miss this VR experience.

Welcome to the Atlas, will you escape?
