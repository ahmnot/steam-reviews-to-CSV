I am a big fan of puzzle games but Platonic has reminded me that I am a clue-based, logical, pattern-matching kind of puzzler. I have spent quite a few hours in this game and have not made much progress.

You are dropped into an abstract and colorful world with zero introduction or instruction. The first couple of puzzles were easy, with clear clues. Then, I hit the slider puzzle. This is my least favorite puzzle type, especially when I don't know what pattern I'm trying to achieve. Turns out that the slider manipulates the environment. So, it is (1) move the sliders, (2) go see what happened, (3) come back and move more sliders, and (4) repeat. This took me more time than I care to admit because I just could not get my head around the solution.

On to the next area. Puzzles continue to configure the environment. For each device located, you must experiment with the options to figure out what it is controlling and what path you are attempting to create. Rarely can you see the effect from the device. Instead, you need to run around and try to figure out what changed. For those with spatial talents, I think this would be a fascinating game. For me, it uses a side of my brain that (evidently) is in need of exercise.

Platonic is a well crafted game that I would recommend to those who enjoy puzzles that require spatial visualization and who thrive on a 'configure as you go' environment. For me, I am more suited to puzzlers in the vein of The Gordian Room or The Trials where the environment is fairly constant and I need to interpret/conceptualize clues found along the way. While I enjoy mechanical puzzles, I prefer to see the results of my actions directly instead of trying to make sense of them.

An added note... Not being willing to quit, I have continued well past the sliders and the next series of puzzles made a bit easier. You definitely need a pencil and paper, as clues are not transportable and are often found quite far from where they are used. This is a challenging puzzler, to say the least!

This is a challenging environmental puzzle game with minimal frills. There is a small hub world that connects to 5 puzzle worlds, each with a different theme. The game is not exactly open world - you have to complete a portion of each level in sequence to open up the next level. But it is usually less than half of each world, and the second half is much harder and sometimes inaccessible until later.

The puzzles range from "not too difficult but still requires some thought" to "real head-scratcher", and the difficulty ramps up nicely. There is no explanation or tutorial for the puzzle mechanics, you just have to try things out and pay attention to your surroundings to figure out how things work. It seems like most of the puzzles are designed so that you cannot just brute force your way though, you have to understand the mechanics for each puzzle. You will need pencil and paper for some puzzles, and screenshots are often helpful as well.

Pros:
  • Good puzzle design and variety of puzzle mechanics. Not a lot of repetition. Lots of "aha moments"
  • It feels like The Witness, but less pretentious
  • Runs smoothly, and I haven't noticed any issues.

Mixed:
  • Not super long, it took about 8 hours where the last hour or so was me trying to decipher a code that I had written down wrong in my notes. But I'd rather have a shorter puzzle game with good puzzles than a long puzzle game full of easy or repetitive puzzles.
  • One of the mechanics in the water area with the red green and purple blocks was really complicated and I'm not sure if there was a better way to solve it other than essentially brute forcing it by keeping notes for each combination. Platonic begins by immersing you in the world without providing any context. Small bits and pieces are revealed in these type of games, which each player can interpret differently. There's none of that here, and the story is absent. While I would prefer a plot, I didn't mind the lack of one because I came for the gameplay.

Following a short tutorial, you'll be brought to the primary location. I had one small gripe here, which falls under the nitpicking category. The tutorial will show gamepad controls if you have your gamepad connected in and choose to play using a keyboard and mouse. Unplugging the gamepad and beginning a new game will fix this. On the other hand, I enjoyed how you could move about using a mouse; hold down the left mouse button to walk forward, and the right mouse button to walk backwards.

Several walls in the hub have geometrical shapes on them, implying that the puzzles can be completed in any sequence. It turns out that the game is very linear, with everything following a set course. The first few chambers demonstrate how the game will function. In each area, the main goal is to locate the shape that leads to a new path. You will, however, have to solve various challenges to get there.

Each area offers a variety of puzzles that are difficult to complete through brute force. So the first step is to investigate the area, which is limited and doesn't allow for much exploration, and search for hints. These come in the form of images or environmental clues. Because you don't have access to an in-game camera or notebook, you'll have to scribble down the hints — or take a screenshot.

The catch is that these hints may not be relevant in the current situation. You may even find the shape without completing all of the puzzles. This happened to me in one of the early spots, and I honestly believed it was a bug or an issue with the design. Later on, I realized that everything was part of a larger picture, and that you can return to a spot after you acquire clues that help you solve previously unsolvable puzzles.

When it comes to puzzles, they're great. Sure, there are times when a lever must be pushed or a block must be placed in the proper location, but everything makes sense. Solving puzzles provides a sense of accomplishment. The game succeeds in making you smart by avoiding abstract answers that demand special knowledge. Only a few times did I get the impression that the game wanted me to interact with things at random.

One thing to keep in mind is that some problems have multiple solutions. While the first is self-evident and necessary for progress, the second is revealed in the second half when you open all paths and discover you're not finished. I’m currently stuck here because clues aren’t as obvious. But I’m definitely not quitting.

In the same way that The Witness feels like Jonathan Blow looked at Myst and said, "I like it, but what if it had less plot?", this feels a little like the creator looked at The Witness and said, "Eh, still too much plot."

In this game, you manipulate shapes and...that's the plot. Why manipulate the shapes? Well, they're there. They're not going to manipulate themselves. So, you know, get going.

In and of itself, that's not really a criticism. (You'll note that I did give the game a thumbs-up.) It does mean, though, that when you're stuck, you can't take a break by looking around and thinking about the world, wondering who the inhabitants were and why they disappeared and what all this architecture meant to them. You're here for the puzzles, and if the puzzles get frustrating, well, that's what there is.

By and large, though, the puzzles are quite good. There's generally a logical connection between things, there are clear wires running from panel to panel so you know what things affect what other things, you can often see the goal you're working towards. There's a lot of back and forth, a lot of "oh, hey, if I used A1 to get to B1, maybe over here where I have B2, I can use it to work back to A2." Which is rewarding, if you like that kind of thing.

The biggest problem is one common to a lot of explore-the-world puzzle games: sometimes it's not clear if you can't solve This Puzzle because you're being dense and you need to stare at it more, or if you can't solve This Puzzle because it's not solvable in its current state and you should be staring at That Puzzle instead. More than once, we were frustrated because we couldn't find something we knew had to be there, when in fact it wouldn't appear there until we did that other thing in that other place with that other thing that would unlock the thing that...you get the idea.

So: imperfect. Occasionally frustrating. But generally fun and rarely if ever unfair. Overall, I'd say it's...

...solid.

FANTASTIC.

一些需要注意的特点：
1.【搞懂原理】是这个游戏的核心体验，机关的作用、符号的含义、区域的空间结构、解谜的目标，都需要靠玩家根据有限的线索进行探究和学习。
2. 完全无剧情，给玩家的反馈非常少，引导方式非常隐晦，整体格调很冷漠，像是玩家在一个寂静的外星世界里独自学习。对玩家的【自主性】要求很高，需要自己去想"接下来要探究什么"。
3. 后期对玩家的推理、观察、线索整理能力要求较高。建议使用手机拍照记录各种谜题线索，并准备纸笔应对一些规模较大且需要审慎推理的谜题。
4. 为了设计上的铺垫，前期的区域一和区域三的内容比较一般，容易让人低估这个游戏的谜题设计质量。后期有一定程度的非线性，需要跨区域寻找线索和解题。

和见证者的区别？
1. 没有量产的题板，并不是那种"先有一套大规则-利用这套规则设计一连串谜题"的自上而下设计思路，而是一个个独立的、置于不同情境之下的装置，每一个装置都是一个【全新的问题】，只是可能共享某一些规律和特性。
2. 更加关注用题板去【操纵环境】，目的往往是理解题板对环境的控制关系并建立一条能够到达下一目标的通路。
3.玩家的学习对象更像是"语言"、"原理"而非"规则"。
4.总的来说更像是冷漠硬核版的塞尔达迷宫设计。

一些小缺点：
1. 离开题板时的镜头移动和操作感受比较迷惑。经常误操作。
2. 内置了没用的跳跃功能。下落判定不一致，有些地方会摔下去，有些地方有空气墙保护。
3.个别谜题的答案"保护"得不够好，可能出现玩家在弄懂某些原理之前就猜中答案的情况，导致跳出作者预期的认知流程。
4.音乐音效比较单调乏味。
