A Fisherman's Tale was an almost ingenious puzzle game that experimented with the player's perspective and the game world, allowing us to become part of a magical world combined with a very entertaining story. In Another Fisherman's Tale, the game principle changes radically - are InnerspaceVR and Vertigo Games on the right track?

In the role of the sailor Bob, we follow his life while his daughter Nina clears out her mother's cellar, using models she once built with her father to try and unravel her father's past. A simple story that works thanks to the good staging.

The entire gameplay revolves around us embodying a doll and working with the character's special tricks. We can swap our hands to solve puzzles, we have claws, talons and so on at our disposal depending on the situation. Particularly interesting is the option to take off your own head and throw it somewhere to get a better overview of the surroundings, headless wandering included.

Another Fisherman's Tale is unfortunately very short and while the puzzles are new and surprising at the beginning, it becomes apparent after a while that they are always repetitive. The puzzles themselves are not difficult and the game usually nudges the player towards the solution. However, there was one situation where I was stuck for a long time because I hadn't internalized the unusual controls. We often have to steer our hands, like the ice-cold hands of the Adams family, through the area to use puzzle elements. We align the dropped hand with our wrist and accelerate using a trigger. This control system quickly leads to cramped postures in tangled environments. However, there is a switchable hint system and control overlays. Towards the end, the puzzle game unfortunately becomes more of an interactive narrative and we no longer have too much to do. But we can listen to the very good story. The story is told in English or French and is easy to understand. There are German subtitles.

Graphically, Another Fisherman`s Tale is not a real treat, but still looks quite respectable with the familiar comic graphics.

It's not a masterpiece, but it offers innovative puzzles, even if it could have done with more variety. The story is fun, even if it gets a bit surreal at the end, but points to a third part.

Fans of special controls are welcome to pick it up in the sale and experience a wonderful story.

All in all, I think I enjoyed it quite a bit. Not as much as the first one, which was in comparison more focused and simpler in all aspects. This one tries to do too much and as usual that comes with risks.
If you enjoyed the first one and feel up to a challenge in terms of VR, get it!

A worthy sequel

Do I recommend it? ABSOLUTELY. It's a very well made sequel to A Fisherman's Tale, and it doesn't just reuse all the mechanics of the first one to tell a new story. New mechanics are added that work well in the game. The parts where you control a large arm with your arm feel suitably heavy and fun to control!

Pros
  • Fully voiced
  • Can Teleport and/or Freemove* (some issues, see ♥♥♥ list)
  • Interesting mechanics
  • High Quality, moving story
  • Simple but satisfying puzzles
  • Immersive experience, very easy to feel like you're really part of the game. The model puppet aesthetic helps with that a lot I think

Now, I did have a problem that caused me a bit of frustration and a bit of motion sickness.

♥♥♥♥

There is no clear way to completely disable free move while swimming, and swimming freemove is very bad for motion sickness because of the slidy-ness of it. Teleport swim does exist, but you can accidentally start sliding by moving your hands the wrong way.

This can be mitigated by moving your hands slowly, or placing one controller on the ground so you don't accidentally activate it. But it shouldn't have been a problem since we can already toggle between teleport and freemove. Also, putting your controller on the ground restricts your abilities, so it's not a great solution. You'll occasionally just have your screen taken over by vignette as you slowly side because you moved your hands the wrong way. Or you'll find that your body isn't where you left it because you moved your hands while your head was detached.

On the topic of movement, I found it irksome that throwing your head was only implemented with actual motion. It would have been a lot less nauseating to just teleport the head the same way that teleport move works. It was just a lot of flinging my camera around at high speed, which is a huge no-no for people who get any kind of motion sickness. But I can see it probably being pretty tough to allow both if one is using the physics engine.

Conclusions
Regardless of those issues, and as someone who is pretty resistant to motion sickness, it's a phenomenal game, and I'm looking forward to future titles from these guys!

First off, you don't need to have played the first one (which was a good game). The order doesn't matter - there are a few callbacks to A Fisherman's Tale, but the whole thing feels like the slice of a wider set of stories and A Fisherman's Tale is just one of those other stories. That said, A Fisherman's Tale was great too (and had similarly inventive puzzles).

If you HAVE played the first one, the puzzles in this one use a totally different mechanism. Where A Fisherman's Tale had some weird world within world stuff going on, in this one you literally rip yourself apart to accomplish tasks. You can pop your hands or your head off easily and send them off to do things, and you can find new hands to accomplish different tasks (e.g. a crab claw that can cut things, or a robotic hand that controls a crane overhead). You can also launch your hands and have them crawl (or swim) around to grab stuff or operate levers and then reel them back in.

The puzzling is good - the grip control on the index was maybe a tad too sensitive but it was only minimally frustrating (though... some puzzles could have been made massively more frustrating if I'd accidentally lightly brushed the grip while using the thumbstick). It says I've played 2.6 hours, and I did play to the closing credits. Only a few of the puzzles had solutions that weren't immediately obvious (and these were all of the "find the things" variety, not of the "use your brain" variety) - it's a casual puzzler with a good plot.

It was a good 2.6 hours - and a game I'll probably revisit once it has faded from memory (though it was, fortunately or unfortunately, very memorable so this might be a while). It was enthralling enough that I played it from start to finish in a single session. Adjust your expectations accordingly, and you'll enjoy it.
