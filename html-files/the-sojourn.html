Although I enjoy a mental challenge, The Soujourn has finally worn me out. Gameplay can be summarized as 'solve puzzle' and then 'move to next puzzle.' Although the scenery is lovely and the sound track enjoyable, the repetitive nature of the challenges is exhausting. This is compounded by the fact that there is no reward for solving a puzzle. Instead, you just move along to the next one.

The story begins with following beings of light into an environment that unfolds before you. At first, it is delightful and I had a real sense of discovery and wonder. Each area has an average of 5 puzzles. 5 'scroll challenges' are added to these as you enter the second phase. From this point, each area then has around 10 puzzles. Multiply this by numerous areas and the number of conundrums is a bit daunting.

Puzzles consist of managing light and dark to power devices that direct energy, swap positions, and open bridges. Some have gates that turn light/dark on/off as you pass through. Others have barriers that change according to whether you are in light or dark mode. Lastly, many have a stationary or movable power source. Solutions are a mix of physics and resource management to create a path from each start to finish.

Although there are multiple scenes with statues, there is no narrative and no real story to follow (other than what can be inferred from the statues). Each scroll has a philosophical quote but these do not tie to any theme that I could follow.

At this point, I have over 26 hours invested and am approaching the completion of the third (out of four) major sections. I do not regret buying the game and have gotten my money's worth. But, I am not inclined to continue. While it is possible that 'all will be revealed' in the end, I don't have the stamina to complete this marathon.

This is not a bad game and I experienced no bugs or issues. However, I would recommend it with a major caveat. If you are a gamer who enjoys puzzles for the sake of puzzles, then The Soujourn may be right for you. If you prefer an adventure with a sense of forward motion or discovery, then you may want to look elsewhere.

Update: As noted in the comments, after a 10 month break, I circled back to The Sojourn and played to 100% completion. The fourth (an final) section of the game was easier but my original thoughts remain the same.

The Sojourn ist ein kreatives und schönes, jedoch überwiegend ermüdendes Puzzlespiel.

Wie so oft bei Spielen dieses Genres ist die Story nicht gerade vielschichtig. Die oder der Spielende übernimmt die Rolle eines namenlosen Protagonisten, der in einer mysteriösen und surrealen Welt erwacht. Über vier Kapitel hinweg geben zwei Lichtsphären bruchstückhafte Einblicke in das Leben der einstigen Bewohner des Landes. Dazwischen gilt es ~70 Puzzles zu lösen und 46 optionale Herausforderungen abzuschließen.

Das Rätseldesign ist äußerst innovativ. Jedes Level setzt sich aus einer Licht- und einer Schattenwelt zusammen, zwischen denen der Protagonist über Portale (begrenzt) und Tore (dauerhaft) hin- und herreisen kann, um Brücken zu errichten und Zäune zu überwinden. Sobald das Ziel erreicht wurde, wird das Level erweitert und es erscheinen neue Wege, neue Objekte und ein neues optionales Ziel in Form einer Schriftrolle. Nun kann das Level entweder verlassen oder die zusätzliche Herausforderung angenommen werden.

Dieses Konzept wurde im ersten Kapitel und der ersten Hälfte des zweiten Kapitels exzellent umgesetzt. Noch nie habe ich in einem Spiel so viele durch Out-of-the-box-Denken geprägte Rätsel hintereinander gesehen wie in The Sojourn – nicht einmal bei Genregrößen wie The Witness oder The Talos Principle. Doch ab der zweiten Hälfte des zweiten Kapitels wird das Spiel monoton und langweilig - vor allem dann, wenn man die optionalen Herausforderungen abschließen möchte, denn häufig muss man dafür das ganze Level erneut lösen. Verstärkt wird das Gefühl der Langeweile dadurch, dass das Lauftempo des Protagonisten qualvoll langsam ist. Zum Ende hin wird das Spiel regelrecht zum Gedächtnistrainer, da man Lösungsansätze während der langen Laufwege nicht selten wieder vergisst.

Außerdem nimmt der Detailgrad der Zwischenwelt rasant ab. So erkundet man zu Beginn eine facettenreiche bunte Welt und schon im zweiten Kapitel steht man nur noch auf einer langweiligen Fahrstuhlplattform. Diese Entwicklung gipfelt darin, dass man im letzten Kapitel einen dunklen Berg erklimmt, der an jeder Ecke gleich aussieht. Vermutlich sollte dadurch die Handlung untermauert und die Atmosphäre gehoben werden, doch stattdessen wird die Motivation zum Weiterspielen gemindert, denn nirgendwo gibt es Abkürzungen oder Geheimnisse zu entdecken. Es bleibt nur der lineare Weg von Puzzle zu Bonusherausforderung zu Puzzle zu Bonusherausforderung.

Es ist schade, dass das Spiel die anfänglichen Stärken nicht beibehalten konnte. Für mich fühlten sich zwei Drittel meiner Spielzeit an wie Arbeit – und nicht wie Spaß.

The Sojourn, a high quality puzzle game with some interesting and well made mechanics that just feels off somehow.
The positive:

I love the mechanics, the dark world, the introduction of new elements is very fair and slow to get you used to it before hitting you with harder stuff. There's about 5 core ones and all are original, any more than the amount they have would be too much for my mind to handle.

The ability to cheese puzzles is great in this, and both a blessing and a curse. Because the mechanics are not as "solid" as other games, you get a chance to pull some stuff the developer did not intend to solve the puzzle. How often this happened I'm not sure, but some puzzles I definitely felt that I had skipped some things via this. I do not want to call them glitches since they are so uncommon and they do fall within the rules of the game, but they are common enough to break the monotony of the levels. There's a lot of places you can just barely squeeze some of the visual based mechanics through, some of it is intentional, some definitely isn't but it feels fair. This reminds me of the Talos Principle in that way, and it's definitely a good thing. The only real complaint about this is that during the first time playing, it could lead you to believe that some of the objects are red herrings, how many or if any actually are, I still don't know.
(POSSIBLE GAMEPLAY SPOILER)
For example when you fall/jump off a ledge, you respawn where you first fell off, but what I figured out on accident, is that if you start a harp playing before falling, it will still be playing when you respawn -2 seconds. So you could use a dark mirror beam over a pit by hopping into it and using that split second to activate the harp, respawning with it still playing and cross the bridge. Please do not fix this devs, it's only really useful in a handful of levels. (END SPOILER)

A Beautiful World, it's almost similar to Monkey Ball where the levels are the same, but when you look around nearly every level has a beautiful semi-cartoonish environment filled with buildings, castles, mountains, It really makes me want to jump out and start exploring that instead of the actual game.

Actually challenging! It's hard to find a puzzle game that's this challenging but fair and you'll get levels and levels (and levels) of this. Some are easier than others, even nearing the end you get hit with some real brain teasers and some softballs to follow them to help you not feel like you're stuck.

Another page taken from the Talos book, BONUS PUZZLES. You can skip these and come back to them after you beat the game which is a plus. Some levels are criminally easy, and once you've mastered them, new sections open up with new objects and a new goal, increasing the difficulty of what you've already mastered. These are where the game shines in its creativity.

Soundtrack does its job and I barely noticed it aside from a few ambient noises.

Both a positive and negative is the content, there are so many puzzles in this game, I thought it was going to end 2 or 3 times and it just kept going.

The Negative:
Beautiful and well made game, there's a lot to be desired when it comes to keeping the player on board.
There is little to no sense of progress, you complete an area, go back to the hub, unlock new area in the hub, go to new area, repeat. For how beautiful the game is, there isn't much to do between puzzles besides walk at an absurdly slow pace and look at things.

You just get ~80 puzzle marathon of one after another, there is some sort of story told by various statues throughout the hub, but I failed to find it compelling and didn't bother with it for the most part. There is a section of the hub where I travel the same looking hallway over and over, the second I get enough levels to unlock a new area, another of the same hallway. It was really discouraging, even some sort of mountain or tower where you can look back to see how far you've come would've alleviated this.

Backtracking, this is especially bad with bonus puzzles, there are many situations where you know what you need to do, it's just a matter of taking the 2 minutes to backtrack through the puzzle to get to where you need to change it. Worse, what ate up too much time for me was when I had clicked something and immediately messed up the puzzle, forcing an essential restart and with the ungodly slow walking speed, it was truly frustrating. A simple undo button would have been nice, or at least some sort of sprint. By the end I really started to fell like the slow speed wasn't to keep you from exploits, but to pad the game time.

These issues wouldn't be as big a problem if there was more to the game than just puzzles, think marathoning a comedy show like I've done with stuff like Curb Your Enthusiasm, it stops being funny when you see the bigger picture and clear patterns arise and ruin some of the magic.
The puzzles started to boil down to a simple logic problem, yes there's a bunch of different mechanics but they all boil down to you needed to be somewhere, swapping something else for yourself, and having the right variables when you arrive at your destination.
This isn't a bad thing as it's the bread and butter of most games like this but the monotony makes it weigh on you more.
The more I played the more it felt like I was playing dozens of the same variation of the Seven Bridges of Königsberg problem especially due to how the game works mechanically.

This feels like a good quality game and there is a certain amount of fun the average person will get out of it, but I doubt it will be able to keep interest past a few hours, since you've basically seen all there is to see by then.
I hate to not reccomend this game, but there's really only a few people I would give this the go ahead to, and those people would probably give a similar list of issues despite the polish and initial quality of the game. I really enjoyed the first 1/3 of the game, the last bit began to felt like a chore, and that's really not what you want from a game.

Good game. Very good puzzles based on original concepts.

I wouldn't say it is similar to The Witness as many reviewers have done. The Witness is a masterpiece on many levels. The Sojourn does not reach that level, but it is still good. One notable difference between the two games is that the structure of the games is very different. The Sojourn is essentially a series of "puzzle chambers" (although these chambers are actually outdoors) connected via a mostly linear progression system where once you solve one chamber you have a very short path the the next one.

Sometimes there are mini-hubs where you can choose which of say 5 chambers to try next, but you need to complete all of them to proceed to the next mini hub. So unlike the Witness, The Sojourn has no single large seamless world to explore, and there are no interconnected puzzles that span multiple areas/chambers.

The music of the Sojourn is very nice. I think it was all Classical style with lots of haunting Cello. And the environments you move through between puzzles and within puzzle chambers are generally beautiful, and yes, the art style is sometimes reminiscent of that of The Witness.

There is an artsy story told via statues that you come across between puzzle chambers that didn't make a whole lot of sense to me or maybe I did not really care enough about about to try too hard to understand. It seems like a vaguely philosophical story about "the journey of life" as far as I can tell. The statues don't speak or present any text or anything like that.

Aside from the main puzzles in chambers that you need to complete in order to progress, there are optional challenges. Usually these optional challenges are in the form of secondary objectives in a puzzle chamber that appear after you have solved the primary puzzle. In a few cases, an optional challenge is essentially given it's own puzzle chamber. Solving these optional challenges yields a scroll with some pearl of wisdom written on it. Some of the pearls are are better than others and let's leave it at that.

In the end, the reason I give this game a solid thumbs up is that it is is series of puzzles solved in first person view, and the puzzles are fresh, often challenging (but not ridiculously hard), and the environments and music are very pleasant to be in.

Finally, to give you an idea of the difficulty and length of game: I completed every puzzle including all the optional challenges in 22 hours without needing any help. I would say I am a pretty seasoned puzzle game player.

Ich hatte mich sehr auf The Sojourn gefreut. Sicherlich muss ein Spiel mehr bieten als eine schöne Optik.

Es geht letztendlich eben immer darum, wie man wann und wo die Energie aktivieren kann.

Pros
- Grafik und Musik sehr schön
- viele fordernde Rätsel inkl. ungefähr der gleichen Anzahl Bonusrätsel
- wunderschöner Abspann, für den es sich nochmal lohnt

und

Contras
- Rätsel nicht 100% sauber, manchmal gibt es mind. zwei Lösungen oder eine unsaubere, einmal habe ich durch Zufall ein Element ausgelassen
- Rätsel, speziell Bonusräume, sind in ihrer Schwierigkeit merkwürdig inkonsistent - meist wechselt sich leicht und schwer ab (das Ganze aber eher zufällig statt gewollt)

- wenn man einen falschen Schritt geht, muss man Rätsel teilweise wieder (fast) von vorne aufrollen

Fazit: pures Rätseln ohne viel Abwechslung, welches sich spätestens ab der Hälfte wie eine spielerische Pflicht statt einer Kür anfühlt
Wer mit einem Abarbeiten von Rätselräumen einverstanden sowie mit Rätseltalent, Erfahrung und evtl. Frustresistenz und hoher Motivation gesegnet ist, sollte es aber probieren.
