Project Chemistry is a barely a game (but there are objectives so at least there's something to do). This is more educational software (with a hefty price tag) than a game. A few things done right and a few things not. And while I think this is not the worst bit of educational software I've seen, we have to remember that we're here on Steam, for PC games, and all games must be held to the same rigorous standards (including, you know, are they fun, or would it be better to spend your money on some paint, so you can watch it dry).

The crux of the game is synthetic chemical reactions. You spawn elements and molecules and you can trigger reactions at the click of your mouse. Two H2 and an O2 molecule will let you create two H2O molecules, you get the idea if you took even basic chemistry. Ok. Is this fun? Nope. What I will say is that at least some extensive effort went into the depth of the chemical reactions you can create, there's quite a lot and it's very deep. There's a sandbox mode, and a challenge mode, which is why this barely scrapes into the "It's a game" category of whether things are games or not. The periodic table is mostly locked and you unlock missions and achievements by figuring out different kinds of chemical reactions. Congratulations, chemistry nerds, someone loves you.

So while I admire how much went into this, there's also problems, besides just the "Is this actually fun?" issue.

From a technical perspective, the game doesn't meet basic minimum requirements that most PC gamers expect as standard.

The game features lazy minimalist/untextured low-polygon "retro" assets and visuals, making this look like a barely functional 3D game from the early 1990s. The lack of textures is a method that lazy devs often use to disguise their lack of talent/interest in doing the graphics properly and trying to disguise it under the name of "art", or "We made it look bad on purpose", which really isn't something gamers should have to put up with. It's unclear why the developers weren't willing to arrange high quality, high polygon count contemporary assets and high resolution textures for the game. It looks bad as a result of their decisions, and that's just another reason to avoid it. I know in this case that's the best way to represent the game, but then that's also a problem, because, you know, we're on PC. For PC games. They should look like PC games. If it's fundamentally impossible to do your game concept and have it look like a PC game, this is not a problem for PC gamers to have to wrestle with (and yet here is this game on the Steam store, asking for gamer money).

The controls can't be customised, which will be an annoyance for many, but it can also render the game unplayable for differently-abled gamers. What really killed this for me on the topic of controls is there's no mouselook that I could see, and you can't really get a good 3D view going on just pushing forwards, backwards, left and right with WASD. I should be able to see the environment like a game of Homeworld or something. That's clunky and I found it awkward.

These technical defects push this game below acceptable standards for any modern PC game.

Project Chemistry didn't appeal much to the people who own a copy of the game, either. It has achievements, and they show us a very clear picture that the game didn't really capture any interest from gamers. The most commonly and easily attained achievement is for synthesising HClO (this does take a small amount of effort, about 5-10 minutes of gameplay), but only around 17 percent of players bothered to get that far before uninstalling the game. Hardly a success story, gamers just weren't all that interested in the game enough to play it for about 10 minutes.

Reviewing SteamDB to check how popular this game was with players reveals a surprise... there's a very healthy spike in player counts for the game. But this isn't consistent with the achievement stats, that show only 17 percent of players bothered playing the game for any reasonable amount of time. How is it possible for this game to have so many concurrent players who weren't even playing the game? Trading cards. Players will use card idling software to collect the cards and sell them, but this won't trigger any achievements in-game. That tells us people only really bought this game for trading cards, and that's a damning indictment of the woeful quality. A closer look at the numbers shows the game just has a couple of players every week running up the game and idling it for cards, then deleting it. We must ask how it benefits gamers for there to be so many games like this, with no merit as a serious game, that only generate sales from people idling and selling the trading cards.

Project Chemistry has the utterly unrealistic price of $13 USD, for which you could easily get any number of older AAA games, like Shadow of Mordor, Frostpunk, Mass Effect, etc etc. That's a LOT of money to be asking for. $13 would alternatively buy a lot of paint. Which you could watch drying. While the developers of this game tell you what's happening chemically and materially to the paint.

This is also competing with over 9,000 free games available on Steam, many of them far better than this paid product. Given the defects and quality issues with the game, coupled with the unrealistic price, this is impossible to recommend.
