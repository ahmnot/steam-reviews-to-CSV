Hourglass is a puzzle game which has an Egyptian theme, and is played from a first person perspective which allows you to move around and view the puzzles from different angles while trying to identify the solution.

If you’ve played any similar first person puzzle games, the puzzle mechanics will feel familiar. You’ll need to press buttons and place cubes on pressure plates to open doors and activate moving platforms, which will allow you to cross bottomless pits and rivers, or reach higher ledges. Later puzzles also include teleportation discs which let you instantly move between two locations.

The unique twist of Hourglass which complicates each puzzle, is your ability to create a ghostly clone of your character and record it performing some actions, which will then be replayed while you do something else. For example, you might need to stand on a moving platform while your clone stands on the pressure plate which activates the platform, carrying you to the destination.

In a way, this is a cooperative game where you control both characters. The concept is similar to Talos Principle’s time recording puzzles, although Hourglass isn’t as complex because each recording is limited to around 10 seconds. The final area can be quite tricky though as it requires thinking of ways to allow both yourself and your clone to use the same object almost simultaneously but for different purposes.

Each area has five puzzles arranged around a central hub which can be solved in any order, which means if you get stuck you can try a different puzzle and return later. You also don’t need to solve every puzzle to complete the game, but if you can solve everything you’ll gain access to a hidden area. It took me just under 8 hours to achieve 100% completion.

The story isn’t really worth mentioning as there are only a couple of short cutscenes. I didn’t have any problems with framerate, bugs or crashes.

Overall Hourglass is a fun game which fans of puzzle games should enjoy.

Very enjoyable first person puzzle game.

The puzzles are all based on the ability to record your actions for about 10 seconds then allow them to be replayed by a clone while you can go about your business, with the assistance of the clone. There are some interesting subtleties to this mechanism that you will need to figure out to solve some of the puzzles. Ultimately you solve puzzles so you can move forward in the game world.

There is a barebones story about trying to find your father who was "lost" in this world.

The game world is organized as a hub with three domains to visit. At the beginning of the game, you go through a few tutorial type puzzles to get to this main hub. You have to solve each domain to the point of unlocking the next domain. To do that, you need to solve puzzles in the current domain so that you acquire at least 4 crystals. All the crystals in a domain are visible from afar, and you have to solve a series of puzzles to get to each crystal. Each domain contains 5 crystals, so on average you can skip the puzzles leading to one crystal in each domain. However, if you acquire all the crystals, it will unlock a secret area at the end of the game with a final puzzle which was quite challenging.

There are few game mechanisms: cubes to be placed on buttons or to jump on to reach someplace higher, and various switches to open doors or move platforms. Later in the game you will get one upgrade related to the clone-recording and and one new type of device / game mechanism required to solve puzzles that is pretty cool but I don't want to spoil.

The game looks quite pretty, with low poly graphics. The main attraction is the puzzles. They are good - it was surprising that with such few game mechanisms, the puzzles were quite creative and never really got old. Most were not too hard but there was a handful of head-scratchers. You will also come across puzzles where you think that the 10 seconds of clone time you get are not enough to solve the puzzle ... which usually means you are missing a different solution. However, there is also handful of puzzles that had a tight timing requirement that you had to fit within the 10 second window, because that was the only solution. However with some trial and error and figuring the optimal time to start the recording, these are not too too hard. You will also need some basic platforming skills.

The closest game that I think I would compare Hourglass to is "The Sojourn". Though smaller in scope, I found Hourglass more enjoyable because of the creative puzzles, and each puzzle is quite short (once you figure out how to solve it) and focused. The game autosaves after each puzzle (or is it at the beginning of each puzzle), and there is a puzzle reset/restart mechanism.

8/10

Optisch schlichtes Spiel mit abwechslungsreichen, motivierenden und belohnenden Rätselideen

Hourglass ist ein Indiegame eines so kleinen Entwicklers, dass es optisch schonmal extrem minimal daherkommt. Gebaut in der Unreal Engine, sehen Dinge wie Wasser und Reflektionen schick aus, der Rest ist sehr polygonarm.
Die Story ist überschaubar: Papa ist in Ägypten bei einer Entdeckung verschollen, der Spieler folgt ihm als Tochter, die ihn wieder freirätselt.

Das, was für mich in Hourglass punktet, ist die Spielidee zusammen damit, dass sie wirklich vielfach und abwechslungsreich präsentiert wird. Soll heißen ich mag das Aufnahme-Element, welches man aus dem Talos Prinzip kennt (und Portal 2 Spieler aus der Mod "Thinking with Time Machine"): man erstellt eine Aufnahme von sich, die dann abgespielt wie eine Art imaginärer Koop-Partner hilfreich ist. Sie aktiviert Dinge und bewegt Objekte.
So rätselt man sich durch die ersten Räume und merkt gar nicht, dass hier schon ein sehr subtiles Tutorial angewendet wird. Es werden ein paar einfache Wege aufgezeigt, wie man an die Sache rangeht. Das Interessante ist, dass während des ganzen Spiels die Rätsel immer in eine andere Richtung gehen. Es ist nicht so, dass man Basismechaniken beigebracht bekommt, die dann immer wieder Anwendung finden, nein gefühlt geht jedes Rätsel eine neue Richtung. Das schätze ich sehr an dem Titel. Für die kurze Spielzeit wurde bei den Lösungen auf maximale Abwechslung gesetzt. Deswegen halten sie mich bis zum Ende gut bei der Stange.
Nun denke ich 80% des Spiels lang, dass das Ganze gut durchgeht und ich mit dem Prinzip gut genug vertraut bin (ohne dass es zu einfach ist), bis es mir dann die letzten Rätsel der finalen Welt ordentlich geben. Es ist hervorzuheben, dass die Rätsel IMMER übersichtlich präsentiert werden und in der Zusammenstellung nie zu komplex werden. Es geht immer darum, das entscheidende Fünkchen wieder neu um die Ecke zu denken.
Schwer wird es dann, wenn man auseinanderhalten muss, was noch real und was imaginär genutzt wird; man kann nämlich sowohl die Aufnahme als auch Objekte sozusagen nur temporär nutzen, um in der Realität nichts wirklich hergeben zu müssen.

Die Grafik schaut sich leider nicht weg. Mir war es bis zum Ende ein Fünkchen zu schlicht.
Die schöne Musik gehört in die Kategorie "leider nicht dauerhaft vorhanden".
Zum Finale gibt's atmosphärisches Gewitter, da hat man mit einfachen Mitteln nochmal etwas versucht zu bieten.
Insgesamt sollte man keinen Story- oder Atmokracher erwarten, sondern rätselfokussiert sein.

Leider lässt sich die Steuerung nicht remappen, und aufgrund dieser mangelnden Option kann man die Tastenbelegung auch im Vorfeld nirgendwo sehen. Aber viele Tasten sind's nicht, deswegen seien sie hier kurz erwähnt:
Laufen = WASD / Pfeiltasten. Springen = Leertaste / Num 0. Aufnahme starten/stoppen = r.
Leider kann man eine Aufnahme nicht löschen und auch nicht neu abspielen wie in Talos. Einmal abgespielt war's das. Hat man sie falsch aufgebaut, muss man sie laufen lassen oder überschreibt sie schnell mit einer neuen.

Der wichtige Aspekt ist, dass Hourglass für mich aufgrund seines spielerischen Inhalts punktet. Wie gesagt sind die Rätsel super abwechslungsreich; alle fühlen sich individuell an und fordern auf neue Art heraus. Das Ganze läuft übrigens sehr intuitiv ab, man hat immer das Gefühl, den logischen Schritt auf natürliche Weise selbst gefunden zu haben; das ist der belohnende Faktor an Hourglass.
In den drei großen Pyramiden gibt es jeweils fünf Kristalle zu sammeln. Vier benötigt man, um weiterzukommen, d.h. von fünf Rätseln könnte man ein beliebiges auslassen, sollte man partout nicht weiterkommen. Fair!
Löst man alle inkl. des letzten Extrabereichs vor dem Ende, hat man auch schon alle Errungenschaften freigeschaltet.

TL;DR:
It's a "Talos Principle" lite with a focus on well-timed platforming. If that sounds fun to you, you'll probably enjoy this short game. But wait for a discount, for the full price you don't quite get your money's worth. Play the demo if you're undecided.


What you do and don't get
Hourglass is a short puzzle-platformer set in Egypt. The story is serviceable for this type of game but not more than that, which is to be expected. The grapics, sound design, music and the occasional voice acting have similar quality standards: fitting but nothing remarkable.

The puzzles themselves are solid and fair. Easy at first but the later ones get quite tricky and test your understanding of every game mechanic. On several occasions I was stumped and sure that I was missing an object or switch, but it always turned out that there was something I hadn't tried yet and in the end, I finished all puzzles without a guide (but sometimes I was really close to looking it up :). The tag "puzzle platformer" should be taken seriously, it is as much a platformer as it is a puzzle game and the timing on later puzzles in terms of hitting switches or making jumps gets tight (within reason). If you are not willing or able to do some timed platforming, you will not come far. You also have to have a bit of tolerance for the controls that can "eat" inputs here and there. Not often and the setback is never more a minute, but you will encounter it.

Speaking of controls: No key rebinding. There are several layouts implemented at once (WASD, IJKL, Arrow keys), those are not listed anywhere from what I saw.
The settings in general are very spartan. Volume sliders, some game options, some screen resolutions, that's basically it. It runs well and smooth on my system, though I am well above the system requirements so your mileage may vary.

As for the amount of puzzles: You get the tutorial + 3 pyramids with at least 5 individual puzzles in each + a couple puzzles on the roads to each pyramid + the "secret" cave. Get any 4 crystals in each pyramid to beat the game, get all 5 for 100%. For the second and third part of the game you each get a new toy/mechanic, other than that everything works with what you see in the tutorial. The length of the game is just right for the amount of content it has, the mechanics fulfill their potential and nothing feels like filler.


Is it worth it?
The regular price of €18 is too high for what you get. I got it on sale for -60% off (€7,20), that feels like a good deal, €10-12 would be ok as the regular price. For €18 I'd expect more content, way more polish and eye candy or some truly unique gameplay. I know the team behind the game is just 2 (3?) guys and that it's self-published, I get that it's not easy. But both developers claim to have decent industry experience and apart from the successful Kickstarter campaign, they got both a state and a corporate sponsorship from what I understand. This is not little Jimmy's first game made in their spare time, they are professionals working in this business, yet the finished product, while not bad, doesn't meet the expectations I have for that price and feels too bare-bones. There is certainly a lot that could have been done better and I have hopes that the devs can and will do better in their future games.

PROs:
- Hourglass is a good first person puzzler revolving around the "time-recording" mechanism: you have the power of recording yourself making actions and then cooperate with yourself to solve puzzles.
- Good variety of puzzles: the main mechanism is exploited quite well during the game, with new elements added throughout the three main sections.
- You can solve the main section even without completing all the puzzles (or leaving one, at least); completing them all, however, is not useless...
- The Egyptian setting fits well into the overall atmosphere.

♥♥♥♥:
- Some puzzles rely less on logic and more on you only being able to walk at a max speed, with long platforms / path only meant to prevent you from solving puzzles in a certain way and forcing to understand the required solution. Sometimes that's ok, even if it requires a bit of timing (I HATE timing in puzzles :-) ), but sometimes it feels more artficial than puzzling.
- Some sections should have been designed to avoid being soft-locked or, otherwise, the reset command should be implemented better (I'm thinking of Talos Principle)

A nice game, not too hard, perfect for puzzle lovers looking for a break from tougher challenges! Recommended.
