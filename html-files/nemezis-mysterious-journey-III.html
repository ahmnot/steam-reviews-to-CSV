游玩之前智力超群，游玩之后弱智王者
首先这里是不推荐这款游戏，具体的理由也会在下面诉说。如果说你是一个智力超群的玩家，那么你尚可尝试一下本作；但如果你只是一个很平常的玩家，并且不打算看攻略来进行无意义的重复劳动，那么还是请省下你口袋里的毛爷爷来购买其他游戏。简单的来说，这款游戏根本就不是给正常人玩的，如果没有通过各种剧透式提醒，那么在本作中你将感受到智商被按在地上摩擦。对自己智力特别自信的玩家大可一试，看看自己没有提醒究竟能走到哪一步。

毫无感情可言的剧情
游戏以冒险解密为主题，本可以开展出不错的剧情，但是在本作中剧情却是这样的。一个博士发明了一个隐形传送技术，然后男女主一起进了传送机，记好了，是一起进入的。然后正式进入游戏，就会忽然发现，小伙伴只活在对讲机和幻想中，不到结尾是碰不到面的。是的，这是一个孤独的，只属于一个人的解密游戏。而游戏的背景也好，剧情也好，平淡如水，不但没有看出对于未来科技的幻想，反而感受到的却是这些科学家没事干把心思全用在设计谜题上。同伴之间几乎毫无感情，对话完全没有任何感情，完全的捧读。

糟糕的互动
解密游戏也是接触了不少款，但是像本作一样奇葩的这还是第一例。玩家是在3D的场景中去探索解密，但是玩家所需要做的事情只有3件，寻找谜题，按下H（帮助），解决谜题。是的，本作中除了这3样，就完全没有其他任何的互动了，或者说有，也是毫无意义的互动（完全没有逻辑的对话）。本身游戏设定就足够糟糕，但更糟糕的互动方式让这个感觉更上一层楼，玩家想要和某人，或者某件物品互动，首先你需要靠近，然后点击，当中间的实心圆圈变成指针了，才能再次点击来进行互动。非常麻烦，而且繁琐，在某些解密的环节，甚至会误触发判定，让本不该移动的物体移动起来，耽误很长时间。

富有误导性的翻译与帮助
作为解密游戏，拥有帮助是一种很常见的东西，比如各种恐怖游戏中的解密，或者是一些专门解密的游戏，多少都会给玩家提醒一下，要达成什么情况才算解决谜题。但是在本作却是另辟蹊径，一上来就没有任何的提醒，谜题的解决完全靠过场对话来进行阐述，而且还都说的迷迷糊糊。尤其是在这个糟糕的翻译下，剧情甚至都有些看不明白的，更何况这些玄之又玄的谜题呢？而本作中本应起到帮助作用的H键，却相反起到了嘲讽的作用。个人在体验的时候对于这个帮助真的是非常的厌烦，尤其是从第四关开始，当你按下H键就会发现，游戏的提醒与实际解决谜题的关键完全不相干！没错，不是提醒，也不是答案，而是直接一句完全不相干的对话。就像是我问你吃早餐了没，你说刚喝了咖啡一样。并且还有部分地方翻译错误，英文原文是1、2、3可以按下，而到了中文则变成了1、2、4按下，至于我说的哪里，到了第四关的玩家自然明了。前期解密尚有迹可循，到了第四关开始，就开启了碰瓷型的解密，只能利用排除法一步步来，几乎就与动脑无关了，就一个个排除尝试。

只有画面还算是不错
游戏的引导差劲，互动差劲，主体的解密也差劲。但是游戏的画面还是相当不错的，充满了奇幻风格的风景贯穿着整个游戏，让你在厌烦的时候，可以看看风景散散心，不去想那讨厌的谜题，不去思考那根本想不通的过关方式。虽然游戏中场景内的东西都无法互动，但是通过虚幻系统来制作的游戏画面确实可圈可点，尤其是在比较靠后的关卡中，那些惟妙惟肖的动物，给人感觉是真的不错。游戏的配乐只能说是尚可，不是很搭调的配乐难以给人留下深刻的印象。捧读的CV配音，让人难以想象这是一款价值70R的游戏。

末尾：解密游戏，最大的乐趣就在于解开谜题后的那一瞬间的恍然大悟。给出一个题目，让你慢慢思索，这个过程会让人十分享受。但是在2、4、6关卡中，却完全得不到这种享受，反而挫败感更加浓重。非常喜欢解密的玩家也可以来尝试，但既然是给差评，也就注定了这款游戏在我个人的审美中并不适合数量庞大的普通玩家。本篇评测仅适用7月9日的版本，之后是否会改善，还请对本作依旧有兴趣的玩家关注官方信息。

While I didn't recommend it at first I now completed a playthrough on "hard" difficulty. There were a few puzzles actually made me think for more than an hour. Start on "hard", don't pay to much attention to dialogues as they sometimes reveal the solutions. As I've played various puzzle games before no spoilers were given to me at least.



**Issues**

I sadly still have several issues limiting the joy.

1.) Voice dialogues explain more or less how to solve the puzzles. Sorry, but that is a big no-go unless you are already familiar with puzzles.

2.) During dialogues movement speed is reduced unless you skip them.

3.) Alphawalls and hitboxes are strange as I often get stuck while I should easily fit unless my character is about 2m wide.

4.) It feels as if there are few puzzles in relation to the size of the environment. However some levels with only one puzzle have it spread across the associated map.

5.) The dialogues feel robotic like simply read without the voice actor being in the role and are annoying as hell if not turned off.

6.) At least one puzzle [4-lane circle shifting] is already difficult to overview as the textures blend together to some degree. I'm actually writing this with that puzzle open in the background and myself being frustrated of the terrible overview and a few other issues.

7.) During several actions like moving in an elevator or standing on/leaving a moving platform I can't move. There can also be only one action be performed at a time. Pulling a lever for instance will make the player wait for whatever action is bound to it is done. I don't see any reason for that. There is nothing to skip. It's just a nuisance to me as many of these actions are quiet slow.

8.) The NPC feel out of place. NPC like these don't belong in puzzle game in my opinion as the atmosphere is different than being all alone in a place filled puzzles to solve.

9.) The controls are one more big issue. There is an interaction mode which needs to be entered. As soon as you move in any direction [camera movement not included] the mode is turned off again. I had several situations where I couldn't enter interact due to the camera deadzone [there is a limit to how far you can look down] which prevented me many times from interaction with a low placed object as I had to reposition myself turning off the interaction mode.

10.) It also appears that the first three levels weren't counted as complete. Possible reason would be having the first chapter done in the demo. None of the 6 achievements [completing and completing without hints] were accounted. At the same time I have not even the desire to replay the levels. Reason for that are once again the issues listed.



**Verdict [72/100 points]:**

For the price of less than 20$ with a rather small developer team the experience was forth the money. I still see a greater amount of unused potential. I for myself had fun with most puzzles to an extend where I had "Victories" without any hint [the dialogues weren't spoilers in my case]. Just don't start off with the "regular" difficulty as puzzles are getting toned down/altered, which most likely will spoil the "hard" difficulty.


**Proposals:**
-an option for not start any dialogue
-rework Alphawalls and Hitboxes
-being able to move while having interaction mode on
-reduced deadzone for the downward camera movement
-visually rework the first puzzle of the fifth chapter to easier see the boundries of the plattforms and fences [different colors for instance for each circle]

Nemezis: Mysterious Journey III ist ein wunderschönes Myst-like-Abenteuerspiel mit ein paar Schwächen.

Die Vorgänger Schizm: Mysterious Journey und "Mysterious Journey II" sind wohl jedem Myst-Fan ein Begriff. Ich persönlich habe bisher leider beide Spiele nicht gespielt, dafür aber das etwas weniger bekannte Werk desselben Entwicklerstudios: "Sentinel: Descendants In Time". Dieses Spiel hat mich mit seiner spannenden Story sowie den einzigartigen Welten und kniffligen Rätseln enorm in seinen Bann gezogen. Als bekanntgegeben wurde, dass Urgesteine der ehemaligen Spieleschmiede ein neues, fast gleichnamiges Studio eröffneten, um ihr Können mit einem neuen Spiel unter Beweis zu stellen, konnte ich den Release kaum abwarten.

Die Story des Spiels ist - wenig überraschend - untere Mittelklasse und eher Mittel zum Zweck. Amia und Bogard möchten zusammen den Planeten Regilus erkunden. Dazu nutzen sie eine neue, noch nicht ganz ausgereiften Teleportationskapsel und es kommt wie es kommen muss: die Protagonisten werden voneinander getrennt. Die oder der Spielende schlüpft nun abwechselnd in die Rollen von Amia und Bogard und versucht, das Paar wieder zusammenzuführen. Dabei gilt es anspruchsvolle Rätsel zu lösen und sieben Welten zu durchqueren.

Die Reise kann in den Schwierigkeitsgraden normal oder schwer angetreten werden. Was nicht klar ist: die Schwierigkeitsgrade unterscheiden sich signifikant - dahinter verstecken sich zwei völlig verschiedene Erlebnisse. Der erste Modus war vermutlich dazu gedacht, neue Spielerinnen und Spieler für das Genre zu gewinnen. Jedoch bezweifle ich, dass der Plan aufgeht, denn die Rätsel sind oberflächlich und jede Aktion wird mit unnötigen Hinweisen kommentiert. Folglich wirkt jede Welt wie ein Tutorial und es kommt kein Spaß am eigenständigen Lösen auf. Schlimmer noch, die Monologe stören sowohl die Konzentration als auch die Immersion, denn nicht selten verraten Amia und Bogard unaufgefordert - und noch vor der ersten Interaktion - die komplette Funktionsweise eines Rätsels.

Der zweite Schwierigkeitsgrad ist wesentlich besser. Zum einen sind die Rätsel komplexer und zum anderen nimmt die Anzahl der Kommentare stark ab. Das ganze Potential, das der normale Modus verschenkt, wird hier genutzt. Leider war dies beim Start des Spiels nicht ersichtlich, weswegen ich zunächst den normalen und erst danach den schweren Modus abgeschlossen habe. Anmerkung: Mittlerweile haben die Entwicklerinnen und Entwickler auf das Feedback der Spielerinnen und Spieler reagiert und einen kleinen Hinweis hinzugefügt.

Grafisch ist das Spiel wunderschön anzusehen und der Soundtrack verzaubert. Auch die Atmosphäre ist gut, wenngleich sie nicht das Niveau älterer Spiele erreicht. Dies liegt abgesehen von den anstrengenden Kommentaren daran, dass die Entwicklerinnen und Entwickler unzählige Tiere und Charaktere ohne wirklichen Sinn ins Spiel integriert haben. Überall sitzen starre, teilweise überproportionale Vögel, Echsen und Katzen, die ab und zu den Kopf bewegen. Es war bestimmt gut gemeint, doch die Bewegungen wirken erzwungen und erinnern an mechanische Konstruktionen aus Freizeitparks. Auch die NPCs sind eintönig und treiben die Story nicht voran. Sie stehen an seltsamen Orten und bieten nicht mehr als ein paar Zeilen generischer Dialoge. Hier wäre weniger mehr gewesen.

Die Rätsel sind abwechslungsreich und anspruchsvoll - zumindest im schweren Modus. So müssen auf der Reise durch die sieben Welten verborgene Tore geöffnet und komplexe Apparaturen bedient werden. Leider ist der Spielablauf überwiegend linear, was vor allem daran liegt, dass die Welten in abgeschlossene Level aufgeteilt sind und keine Zwischenwelt existiert, über die in Gebiete zurückgekehrt werden kann. Hier wurde viel Potential verschenkt, denn alles was bleibt ist die offensichtliche Reihenfolge, in der Rätsel gelöst werden müssen. An zusätzliches Entdecken und Erforschen ist kaum zu denken.

Der Grund für diese Genre-untypische, überwiegend lineare Level-Struktur ist vermutlich der Rollentausch zwischen den spielbaren Charakteren, den es auch schon im ersten Teil gab. Während diese Mechanik anfangs eine willkommene Abwechslung bietet, verliert sie mit der Zeit an Reiz, da keine Interaktionen zwischen Amia und Bogard möglich sind. Außerdem gibt es enorme Unterschiede in der englischen Vertonung der beiden Protagonisten. Während Amias Stimme angemessen und angenehm ist, klingt Bogard grundlos aggressiv, was auf Dauer sehr anstrengend ist. I am a huge Detalion fan, especially Schizm 1 holds a special place in my heart as one of my all time favourite puzzle adventure games. Detalion games are famous for their devilishly difficult puzzles and imaginative world-design, and Detalion had a good run of games between 1999-2005. Since their last game Sentinel was released more than 15 years ago, I gave up any hope of expecting more activity from them. That is why the announcement of Nemezis (a.k.a. Schizm 3) took me with a complete surprise and since then I have been eagerly waiting for its release. After the game was released I took two days off to clear it (took me two sessions and over 8 hours without any hints) and started to write this review to share my excitement with you. The wait was worth it! This is an amazing comeback and a game worthy of the Schizm franchise.

Because of the copyright issues, Detalion couldn’t use any stories and characters from the previous games, but the puzzle style and look of the game is completely in line with previous Schizm entries. We play the game alternating between two siblings, Borgad and Amia, who teleport to the far planet of Regilus for vacation. However, something goes wrong during the teleportation and the brother-sister duo gets separated.Throughout the rest of the story they try to get back together, while discovering that Regilus is not what it seems.

Positive:

- I liked that the story keeps the theme of “dual characters” from the previous games. In Schizm 1 you also played two different protagonists who were separated from each other. In Schizm 2 you played a single character, who disguised himself as two separate people. This narrative device creates a nice motivation for driving the story of the game. Overall, I liked the story and characters of Schizm 3.

- Puzzles are amazing. If you are a puzzle adventure fan like me, then you know how rare it is to find games with truly difficult logic puzzles. Somehow the genre of mind-bending Myst-like games got less popular throughout the years, puzzle adventure games nowadays focus more on visuals and usually have average or above-average puzzles at best. This is definitely not the case with Nemezis. Following the style of previous Detalion classics, Nemezis brings forth many challenges that require very careful deduction, inference and calculation. I was literally crying with joy in the second half of the game, where many of the difficult puzzles await you. It is amazing that most of the puzzles look impossible to solve at first look, but with sheer dedication you can overcome every one of them and obtain a level of satisfaction that you can’t easily get from other games. The level of brilliance and depth doesn’t quite reach the levels of Schizm 1, but I can say that it is mostly on par with Schizm 2. I strongly recommend that you play the game on Hard so that you can enjoy solving the puzzles without any hints.

- Graphics and sound design is excellent. Although I still miss the pre-rendered graphics of Reah and Schizm 1, I can also appreciate how far real-time 3D graphics has come from the days of Schizm 2 and Sentinel. Textures look great and architectural design is very intriguing. I also liked the addition of animals and NPCs to the game, which makes the world feel more alive.

Negative:

- The game desperately needs a run button. Default walking speed is way too slow for the larger environments in the latter parts of the game.

- I am surprised that you can’t switch between characters or there aren’t any puzzles that require you to coordinate both characters. It feels like there is some wasted potential there.

- Although the length of the game was fine, after such a long break I was kind of expecting a larger game of epic proportions. I simply couldn’t get enough of the final acts of the game.

- One game mechanic that can be improved is how balls, levers etc. are changing positions. Whenever a lever/ball can slide into two different positions, clicking on it only moves it in one direction and you need to go to the limits of that position and then reverse it to go into other direction (this is really difficult to put it into words, but you will get it if you played the game). This really slows down getting around some of the puzzles, improving the controllability would make the gameplay much smoother.

Nemezis (or Schizm 3 as I would like to call it) is a must-have if you enjoy difficult Myst-like games. Do not be discouraged by the negative reviews that complain about the puzzle design, all of the puzzles are completely logical, it just takes time and experimentation to understand the underlying logic. I think many people want to quickly understand the objective of a puzzle and then spend the most of their time solving it. In Schizm (and other Detalion games) at least 50% of your time is spent on understanding the objective of the puzzle, which is the most fun part! If this sounds like your type of game you will definitely enjoy Nemezis.

Rating: 9/10

DISCLAIMER: I received the steam key for Nemezis: Mysterious Journey III for free in exchange for a detailed and honest review. My opinions and the contents of this review are my own.

Nemezis: Mysterious Journey III is a first person science-fiction exploration based adventure puzzle game developed by Detalion Games S.A. on the Unreal Engine 4. Taking place on the remote, otherworldly planet Regilus, the player commands geniuses Amia and Bogard on a touch and go adventure to reunite after being separated by an unknown meddler.

Nemezis comes at the tail end of multiple distinct legacies, only some of which I can say I'm familiar with. On the forefront is its relationship to the previous "Mysterious Journey" games, which I had never heard of until receiving the key for this game. The setting, Regilus, also appeared as "Argilus" in the first title, and was the invention of Australian genre author Terry Dowling (not present for this title's development). Meanwhile, the co-founder of Detalion, Roland Pantoła, returns as lead designer for this title after both prior entries. But, behind that legacy is another apparent influence, being Cyan Worlds' seminal video game Myst. You don't have to take my word for it, in a press update titled Memories, for this title's Steam News page, Detalion Games wrote: "Why are we inspired by the game Myst? Because we consider it a groundbreaking game."

It's this legacy that I'm more familiar with. Having played Myst, and its sequel Riven, one doesn't have to look hard to find its thumbprints on this, and hundreds of other first-person adventure puzzlers. Even the subject of my previous review, The Wild Case, bore the legacy of Myst through its point-and-click-based first-person control scheme. What this title inherits from Myst is less superficial than that game, however. The landscapes of Nemezis are vivid, awe-striking and rich with a spirit of wonder and fantasy. Being so familiar with Myst and its Ages, it was difficult not to notice the shared sense of sincere vision that went into the settings and visuals of Regilus. When I first booted it up, and found myself engulfed in a lush ecosystem with animated, impossible flora and unafraid animals observing my wandering intrusions, I found the experience almost overwhelming, such was the potency of the visual design's inspiration, and the effectiveness of the tribute to the science-fantasy puzzle-boxes of Cyan Worlds' design. There's a soul behind the visuals that feels, literally, taken from another era. The real-time rendered, high fidelity dreamscapes of Regilus have, behind their conception, the same sense of experimentation, whimsy and creative devotion as the prerendered graphics of Riven's landscapes.

This much, I want to stress that I cannot overstate. There's a lack of cynicism behind the conception of Nemezis's visuals which was genuinely overpowering for me at times. There are plenty of games who's visuals are more awestriking and exacting in their construction as this one, but Detalion crafted a setting here with an uncommon, remarkable spirit. I was surprised by how much Nemezis evoked the feelings Myst and Riven did, even as it was that inspiration that lead to me playing it in the first place.

With all of that being said, however, the magic wears off fairly quickly due to a number of interlocking factors. While I enjoyed certain puzzles a lot, the longer the game went on the more the design drifted further into ceaseless trial and error as opposed to observation and deduction. Puzzles which initially amounted to applying knowledge the player gleaned through careful exploration transform gradually into exercises in applying written down arbitrations, discovered through sheer, repetitive fiddling. Far from the Stranger in a Strange Land-esque compulsion to learn the secrets of the environment and its past in order to understand the devices found within it, the puzzle-boxes placed in Regilus's various sectors feel historyless, ultimately requiring the player to disconnect from their environment and context in order to devote their attention to them.

Unfortunately however, there isn't much to disconnect from. Regilus feels like a black box, abandoned and, while teeming with life, apparently wholly unpopulated outside of outside interference. There's an eeriness which wears off to the emptiness of Nemezis's environments, which becomes replaced with a kind of nihilistic, surface level appreciation. This happens even as the characters remark on the strangeness of these uninhabited cities, which are spooky in isolation, but combine into something that resembles a concept-artbook more than a world.

The vocal direction is very poor, whereas the writing is somewhat of a mixed bag. There are ideas and concepts in the writing which surprised me, and there's an overall feeling of ambition and forethought to the themes that is easy to respect. But I wouldn't say I found the storytelling itself very compelling. I would occasionally find myself mentally drifting into fanfiction, where I had constructed a presentation of events which would have been more emotional, exciting or mysterious. I've called the storytelling of games "wanting" before, but never before have I meant it so much as in this case, where I surprised myself with my own uninvited attempts at correction.

Overall, Nemezis: Mysterious Journey III is a difficult game to recommend. In spite of its shortcomings, I respect and admire it very much, and I fully appreciate having played it. But there's an emptiness within the title that is hard to patch up. While it was, at times, an overpowering tribute to the appearance and atmosphere of classic, artful graphic adventures like Myst, I found myself yearning for a title which had inherited the captivating storytelling and lastingly considered puzzle design of Cyan's worlds, rather than just the visuals.

Ce myst-like va mettre l'accent sur la résolution d'énigmes mécaniques plus ou moins complexes et va laisser de côté tout l'aspect exploration qui est réduit au strict minimum. Les férus d'exploration pourront toujours essayer de retrouver tous les extras cachés dans les niveaux, mais ceux-ci ne sont pas nécessaires pour terminer le jeu. Un jeu d'énigmes pur et dur donc.

Les énigmes sont toutes extrêmement logiques, même si cette logique ne saute pas aux yeux de prime abord. Il est rare de pouvoir tester la mécanique élémentaire qui sous-tend chaque puzzle et donc il faut la deviner au fil des premières manipulations du mécanisme. Il en résulte que beaucoup de ces énigmes peuvent être résolues par essais-erreurs, sans vraiment avoir besoin de comprendre la logique intrinsèque du puzzle (il suffit juste d'avoir de la patience) voire tout simplement par hasard! Cela a un petit côte frustrant de résoudre une énigme sans avoir entièrement compris son fonctionnement, mais cela peut permettre d'éviter les blocages, pour autant que vous ayez la patience de manipuler les mécanismes pendant un temps certain. Niveau énigmes, rien à redire, j'en ai été satisfait, elles ont présenté un challenge intellectuel suffisant à mon niveau.

Le jeu se divise en sept niveaux indépendants, niveaux assez limités en étendue, ce qui limite de facto les possibilités d'exploration. C'est un peu dommage, on cerne très vite les 2 ou 3 puzzles présents dans le niveau et donc l'exploration passe au second plan. Il n'y a aucun indice caché dans l'environnement, il faudra se débrouiller seul avec sa logique. Les quelques PNJ sont assez peu convaincants, tant par ce qu'ils disent que par un jeu d'acteur peu investi, sans parler de l'animation de ceux-ci... j'ai l'impression qu'on faisait déjà mieux au début des années 2000. Si l'on excepte les PNJ, le jeu est esthétiquement plutôt réussi selon mes critères et on prend plaisir parfois à juste regarder les paysages.

Bref, les ingrédients sont là pour en faire un bon myst-like, mais la sauce ne prend pas. Le scénario est assez indigent, il évolue très peu d'un niveau à l'autre et l'épilogue reste nébuleuse. De nombreuses questions restent sans réponse et la cinématique finale finit un peu abruptement, sans véritable climax. Malgré l'esthétisme des environnements, j'ai eu du mal à m'immerger dans cet univers, sans doute parce que les niveaux sont trop petits et trop différents les uns des autres, un peu comme dans myst III et V pour ceux qui y ont joué.

Le jeu est-il mauvais ? Non, loin de là. Il fait le job pour les amateurs du genre. Je le situe au niveau d'un Obduction (Cyan) mais il reste largement en-deçà de la claque que m'avait mis Quern : undying thoughts il y a quelques années.

Bref, un jeu à réserver au inconditionnels du genre!

For clarity, I played this on the hardest difficulty with hints/story off, I'm confident my feelings would be very different had I played on a different setting.

Roland Pantoła does it again! Been a fan of his contributions to the puzzle-adventure genre since I played Schizm in the early 00s, and this clearly has the same DNA of the puzzle design in Schizm/Sentinel/etc, though definitely on the harder and more unusual end of the spectrum in my opinion. It consistently reminded me of my favorite puzzles from those games, and pushed things even further into unusual territory I wasn't expecting. If I was rating every vaguely Myst-like I've played purely in terms of puzzles alone, this is near the very top for me!

To be clear: Schizm 1 is one of my favorite games of all time, like top 3 ever, and Mysterious Journey II also had some of the most consistently appealing puzzles to my tastes of any game I've ever played. This game reminded me very much of those two games in particular, it really did not disappoint, at the time of writing I'm feeling it was on par with them.

This really is about one of the most creative and challenging collections of puzzles I've experienced in recent years. However, I absolutely would not recommend it for everyone. It's riiight at the edge of feeling incomprehensible, but I did complete every puzzle with no hints, and every solution made sense to me after. I can imagine many people thinking this stuff makes no sense or is tedious however, since it really did push the limits. Almost no puzzle felt "stock", even things I'd seen variants of before in other games would bring some new layer of creativity to the table here, and I'd say that very avoidance of familiarity is in itself responsible for quite a bit of the challenge. I found that very welcome and I wouldn't rather have it any other way, but be warned!

The visuals and environmental creativity those old Detalion games had was still in-tact. Absolutely so much detail in every environment to look at it was almost endless! I even found myself taking pictures of the wildlife and stuff. I could wander around these places all day. Often disappointed I couldn't return to older areas, but greeted with new areas that kept having even more alien beauty to offer! This game totally nailed it.

Also even with a different composer, the music still created the very distinct atmosphere those games had to me. I loved Daniel Kleczyński's work in those 90s-00s Detalion games so much that I was worried no one else could live up to it, but Paweł Górniak did a great job crafting a perfectly fitting sound. It's rare to be able to say that music this atmospheric is "memorable", but I repeatedly found myself feeling excited when certain parts of tracks would play, zoning out to these surreal synthetic sounds that really helped sell the alien visuals too.

Since I played with the story off, I won't comment on that, but it seems almost amusingly contrasting from the atmosphere of the rest of the game from what I've peeked at ahaha.

I would say, if you enjoyed Reah/Schizm/Chameleon/Sentinel, this game is for you. If you didn't---well those games only work for a very particular type of person imo, this is no different. Even avid fans of this genre often found those games to have unbelievably strange/difficult/convoluted puzzles compared to others of the time. I would only recommend this (and those) to fans of very, very difficult puzzles, with very vague, minimal information. but if you're the sort of person who enjoyed getting through even those with 0 walkthrough and have the patience to enjoy being stuck on initially incomprehensible things (or simply don't mind if an unusual/difficult puzzle may make you resort to a walkthrough). Well, this brings me a feeling I've been craving that I had scarcely felt since Schizm back in the 2000s. I'm overjoyed by the fact that this could bring me that, and blasted consistently fresh puzzle concepts at me that put me just right to the limit of cracking an initially impossible-seeming challenge.

I would say it's a very particular type of game for a very particular type of player that isn't common. I wouldn't even necessarily recommend it to most Myst fans, but I was absolutely who this game was meant for: a hardcore Schizm fan. Thank you for the experience! I'm very thankful a game like this was released and I hope it's not the last we see from Ronald Pantola in this style!
