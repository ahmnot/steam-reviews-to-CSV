I had an ok time with this game. Recommended but kind of 'meh.'

The Fall of Lazarus is more a trip through the protagonist's psychological state than a work of science fiction. It takes place on a space ship, but other than the setting there's not much sci-fi about the story. I found the protagonist somewhat unlikeable, but given her psychological state perhaps that's intended.

The game itself it partly story heavy walking simulator and part puzzles. The space ship environment was a bit interesting and very basic. The puzzles lacked much instruction at all. For the most part they weren't too difficult to figure out once you understood how they worked (which was not trivial in some cases). However, I think where were some missteps in the puzzles. A guide for the constellation puzzle was included but I couldn't find a way to refer to it once I started the puzzle so it wasn't helpful at all. The star map puzzle desperately needed to inform the player that you can rotate the pieces. The last major puzzle utterly stumped me and I had to visit the Steam forum for the game to figure out what to do.

The game performed well enough for me (i7-3770, Nvidia GTX 1060, 16 GB RAM, most settings on Uber but a few on High, acceptable FPS). I didn't have any crash issues. The game does provide key binding, but that was broken for me. My key bindings didn't work for movement, but did work when I manipulated the tablet device you use throughout the game. Since you use the WASD keys for both, being able to rebind WASD to a Dvorak layout (,aoe) meant I had to swap keyboard layouts (in Windows) every time I used the tablet device. Seemed broken to me so I gave up on the key binding and just swapped the keyboard layout in Windows when playing the game. Fortunately the game only uses a few keys so this wasn't a big deal.

Saves use a checkpoint system without any notification that the game has made a save. Every time I came back to the game I had to redo parts I had done before. Not ideal but I didn't have to replay much.

Grammar wasn't that great, but it didn't bother me either (perhaps translation issues?). The voice acting was pretty good for this type of game. The writing (specifically the dialog) however left a lot to be desired. The writers tried to be witty and insert some humor. I think the story would have worked much better without the attempts at humor. A sarcastic AI (think GloDOS) is actually difficult to pull off and the attempts here were awfully awkward and generally failed.

The game took me about 5.5 hrs, which includes the 'First Passenger' tutorial/prelude to the main story. I actually liked the 'First Passenger' story; much more of a horror bent to it.

TL;DR - if you are into story rich games and don't mind the inevitable quality issues with indie/small team/small budget games this game is enjoyable enough. Just don't come in with AAA expectations.

我可能是这个游戏的第一个中文评测。（笑）

注意
这游戏限定win7/8.1/10 64位，如果你的机子不在这个范围内，那买了也没法玩。

回到正题
背景设定
故事发生在2185年，主角所在的飞船正要前往火星殖民地。
但在这途中似乎发生了什么，整个设施空无一人，除了女主角和一个AI。（其实都在休眠仓里头）
女主角醒来后发现了异样，为了探明真相，故事就此展开。

游戏内容
一个很纯正的解谜游戏，整个游戏出场的只有男女主角和AI（不算女主自己产生的幻觉，男主序章被杀）。
解谜内容包括：走迷宫，接水管，一笔画，找关键词，推箱子，凑数字，等等。
有大量有关于游戏背景的文本，多数以邮件的形式表现。
完整游戏流程大约是3～4小时。
有少量收集要素。
有5小时时间限制。但就算时间到了，也不会有什么事情发生，AI会说一句：你的时间已经到了，但由于我对你感兴趣，所以再给你一次机会。（只是为了增加紧迫感紧张感而已。）
（方块和AI是万恶之源。）

个人感觉
环境多数压抑，但是大场景做的还算不错，减缓了这种感觉。（制作组这虚幻4做的场景好像和unity没什么区别，不过太空和大场景需要）
全程第一人称步行模拟，主角都有配音，语调抑扬顿挫，还挺有感情的。
使用物品的操作有点变扭，不过好在只有4个功能键，就是需要点时间适应。
然后就是独立游戏很多都有的小毛病：指引不明确和场景细节稀少，这导致代入感很是缺失。

总结
可以说是一个集推理，探索，悬疑，太空于一身的优秀集合作品，以解谜为核心推进剧情发展。由于没有中文，大量文本看的很辛苦。幸好多数解谜要素并不需要阅读大量文本，观察，试探一下就能发现规律，解谜要素也简单易懂，谜题旁也有小纸条提示，甚至告诉你答案，不过卡关依旧是常态。剧情上面，AI或者说飞船的真实目的我打通关了也不是很明白，好像方块是所有问题的根源所在。最让我对这游戏赞赏的是女主进入幻觉的场景，气氛营造，和恰到好处，和剧情融合的谜题。这个我也不怎么会用文字描述，预告片和序章DEMO里有完整演示，短小但却很吸引人（至少我很喜欢），有盗梦空间感觉。
但是游戏题材和风格导致玩家受面小，流程时间短，所以建议打折入手。

It went off the wishlist twice, first time last summer when the reviews were still mostly negative whereas now they're just mixed; on seeing that he game has a troubled origin and past I was somehow mesmerized by the anatomy of disaster. But be warned - I am almost quite sure rarely anyone may repeat my deep, rewarding and unique experience with this game and I believe that is the basis for its failure to deliver that to the mass audience that is so overpriced these days yet so vital for anything than needs to sell just not to become a financial flop.
So where do I really begin with this one - and that is when things become complicated. So let's make it a recipe - take that TV series "Threshold" which was scrapped early on because it didn't have "appeal to the mass audience". Take the "Twilight Zone" series, some of Kubrick's "Space Oddysey", the first "Cube" movie and most of all tat movie "Event Horizon" starring L. Fishburne and Sam Neill, but remove gore and leave a deep layer of onthological horror. That is where you slowly start getting to imagine a little what "TFoL" may be. Not is - just may be to you the way it became to me. I am almost sure that at least some of those references are not even intended and even the designers may not be aware of such possibilities. Just like the Wachowsky sisters (then still brothers) when asked about "Matrix": what gnosticism? We were after kung-fu duels ...
There are some strong aesthetical visuals and vistas in this game that are most of all the descendant of Lovecraftian "geometric horror" than anything else so if you're favourite monster is a dumb zombie - an epitome of shallow symbolism of modern pop culture - "TFoL" is probably not going to cater for you. Aim much more for Lovecratf and Ambrose Bierce or even Poe if anything - but in hard core modern sci-fi design and setting. The moment I saw the gigantic rings off the window and the trapezoids hanging up in the blood red sky I looked at the armchair, was struck by the contrast of those two images and realized that the armchair does not really fit the human anatomy. I think there isn't really anything wrong about that and that was only an impression, but that is when you know the game has started to grown on you and it's now really getting to you.
I have been a wargamer, startegy gamer and combat and flight simulator guy for years because I like the games to make me think, plan , anticipate. I have never been much - if anything - into adventure games, because I wasn't particularly impressed with having to uncover what sort of twisted logic lies beneath and behind each time. I often stuck at unnecessarily difficult puzzles - difficult for theirt own sake, not for the sake of the story or intrigue in his game - and yet I am telling you that it's the first time I have discovered such intuitive and rewarding puzzles like in here. I almost gave up at the tree and then it clicked in exactly in its place, becaue I wanted and did give it time. You know, having to resort to a walkthru just to progress in a game is like getting car keys from your dad for the weekend and then have to give it back each time ... Not fun, but an unpleasant tease. Then that birthday reception riddle - wne you discover its simplicity on the fly when you are already at the level of dissasembling the world completely and you're horrifed because on reassembling they match, but don't add up ... Did I mention deep layers of onthological horror?
The first introductory chapter is a great idea and a tool to insert you deep into the experience; just think of that trip Jodie Foster takes in the movie "The Contact" at its end, but ... did I mention deep layers of onthological horror? Well, horror is also not the most just reference - its much more awe, mystery and amazement. I have not a single problem to make an interface with the game and the story, either in technical or aesthetical meaning, and on having to enter the cube again when starting the main game I already felt a deep sense of disquiet and was extremely mesmerized at the same time. Like in a phobia or a nigthmare when you just can't fight off the fascination when everything alarms you to beat feet and run like hell. That sort of stand-alone mini game as the introductory chapter is one of the best stylistic devices if used properly to build atmosphere, suspense and tension and it all is already in there when you start the game after that first chapter. Its end is also a tiny horror in itself, a great tip what to anticipate afterwards.

I mentioned strong aesthetical values in terms of visuals; what was lost in "Gray Dawn" in a flood of lavish religious and esotheric symbolism that at some boint broke under its own weight is much more subtle - pardon my reference if you must, but I'd say it's the difference between an intercourse and an orgy , "GD" being here the latter, an acquired taste. "TFoL" is way more synthetic, sterile and obscure in its design and outlook, not just because it's the already mentioned HI-FI sci-fi setting - it's because -hear me all you pixel hunters now - sometimes "less is more".

So how do I end with that? I haven't finished the game yet so - as it often happens when the expectations grow high - what if the game in the end fails to deliver? That'll be a disappointment that there's not more, but more of a kind when you want to carry on because something feels great and not because it didn't at all at any point. For me the game has already delivered, even with its first chapter alon, but won't to the naysayers who want everything straight out right on their plate in the end, because "they didn't pay to remain unsure". Come on, people, reality is a mysterious phenomenon with more questions that answers. Just allow yourselves be enchanted as I let this game. I paid what - two or three bucks? For a game that offerd me a deep sense of achievement and fullfilment the first evening with that even without finishig? A game I went to sleep thinking about and thought about the first thing in the morning? That is ART. I'll pay ten times more for those guys so they can move on to future designs, just gimme your bank account number. Now I know that yet another rule applies here; the act of creation, art making is often a painful and harmful experience and is rarely recognized, priced or respected in its time.
Why didn't this game work here? Simple: in my country we have a saying that the answer to the question why popular vote yield such pitiful results depends on your answer to the question whether there are more wise or stupid persons in the world.
I have read thousands of books, many more articles, watched thousands of movies and listened to thousands of records and the like. All I am saying again - you may not be able to repeat my experience even with a similar cultural background - because that is how this game is unique. It's like with a revelation or a manifestation of a deity - either it speaks to you or it remains silent forever and there's noithing you can do about that.

I don't write many reviews and this was probably the longest and most emotional one I have ever written just to give this game its due credit - or one of many due credits, including those harsh ones prehaps, too; everyone can have his own story with this game and that is its true and measurable greatness. Like all great pieces of art this game allows you to develop that.

La Historia

La premisa fue sin lugar a dudas lo que me intereso desde el momento en que vi este juego, despiertas de una criogenizacion y te encuentras ♥♥♥ una IA que te indica que la perdida de memoria es normal. Gameplay

Todo se desarrolla dentro de una nave espacial y el gameplay consiste en explorar la nave para realizar diversas tareas (incluyendo algunos puzzles), en el proceso es donde aprendes mas del trasfondo de tu personaje y experimentas ¿Visiones? ¿Alucinaciones? no tengo nada en contra de esta idea pero una vez terminas el juego te das cuenta que no tuvieron nada que aportar, lo cual aporta a mi principal queja: no se conectan los puntos de la historia.

Aventura narrativa espacial que plantea una serie de buenos puzzles, backtracking, así como la posibilidad de desbloquear varias terminales en las que podemos descubrir información del resto de la tripulación, códigos, así como poder leer algunos interesantes reportajes y entrevistas que nos darán una idea del contexto de la exploración y habitabilidad del espacio. Que por cierto recuerda a Glados. Sin problemas de rendmiento. Buenos controles. Su duración ronda la hora y media a dos horas.

The fall of Lazarus, a pesar de ser un juego realizado por un pequeño grupo de personas y de haber sido financiado a través de crowdfunding, resulta una excelente historia bien contada gracias a la complementación que existe entre las mecánicas y los recursos narrativos que poco a poco van reverlando la trama y el trasfondo de un artefacto misterioso.
