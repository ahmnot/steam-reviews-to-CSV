You start out in an airlock with a voice talking to you and having to answer questions from your crew mates to avoid being killed. This was a great intro to the game and it has some great mechanics. There are a couple different endings based on the few choices you had but I keep thinking of what the game could have done but didn’t or choices we didn’t have. As it is I enjoyed the story and kind of felt like it was a reverse of The Thing where we are playing the creature rather than the hero albeit less violent but still having to manipulate others to survive. There are things you have to do when possessing characters but it’s usually spelled out pretty clear. I can’t say I loved the endings but they were decent enough. Vessels is a short and sweet sci-fi story which I never found to be pretentious or preachy, which is becoming increasingly rare amidst very narratively-heavy science fiction. The graphics are dog-ugly, but this game is short, cerebral, and the graphics sufficiently convey what's what. Would I have liked it to be prettier? Sure. Did it need to be? Not at all, and in my opinion, it did not lessen the experience at all. In fact, I would argue that the low-quality graphics are a testament to this game's writing. How so? Well...

The characters aren't particularly visually detailed, aesthetically pleasing, etc., yet by the end of the game, the writing gave me reasons to like and dislike each of them; reasons to be frustrated with each one, as well as to pity them. Given that the game took less than 2.5 hours for me to finish - and I took my time, too - this was some rather impressive character depth for such a brief game to convey to the audience. Furthermore, who, or even "what" you play as makes the mixed emotions players will likely have toward the characters on the ship all the more interesting.

You see, you play as a man with a voice in his head, guiding him and encouraging his actions. Throughout the game, you are mistrusted and use clever means of obtaining more information to further your agenda. However, you *do* have free will in that the voice with you cannot force you to kill someone, for example. Thus, you get to learn about your crew mates and either spare their lives or kill them to make your goals easier to complete. Mind you, this isn't a Hitman game where you can do any elaborate kills or have much in the way of options of how to kill people, nor is it the focus of the game, but I found the option of killing or being merciful to be refreshing. I tend to be pretty merciful in games, and being railroaded to kill would be aggravating, just as being railroaded to being a good guy would also feel too forced.

The game has at least two endings that I'm aware of, depending on whether you intend to help the crew, or help the voice, and like I said, the crew is well-written enough that there are reasons to want to spare them just as there are reasons to want to smite them. Now, is this the best sci-fi story I've come across? Nah, and I'd have a tough time choosing, too, but Vessels does a great job at telling a short story and really fleshing out characters in an itty-bitty timespan. Whomever wrote the the characters for this game really should make more games or write other content. My hat's off to the writer. =D

If stories in games aren't a big focus for you when you game, then Vessels might not be for you, but if you like science fiction, short games, well-written characters, and being in a position to be the villain and being given the option to wreak havoc or be merciful, then Vessels might be right up your alley!

Quick:

5/5. What an amazing game. Vessels is one of those games that comes along every now-and-then, and just knocks it out of the park. I played the game through from beginning to end in one run. Admittedly, it is a short game. You can complete a play-through of this in roughly three to four hours. I was completely engaged from beginning to end, and I would have stuck with it for another four hours. The choices in the game gave me the impression that there are different endings and scenarios to experience, but I do not know this for sure.

More:

Vessels is a very unique game. I don't know how to categorize it. If I had to, I would call it a story-driven walking simulator. That is not entirely accurate, because traversal isn't really a big deal in this game. Events take place entirely on-board a small space ship. There are five main characters. Their interactions and back-story are the meat-and-potatoes of the game. Graphically it's unimpressive, yet aesthetically pleasing. You're presented with low-poly graphics, and basic lighting. That's not to say that the game doesn't look good. It looks great. The sound is pretty good too. I didn't hear any songs that stuck out to me, but the audio does convey the appropriate atmosphere. Game-play is simple. If you've played point-and-click games before, then you should know what to expect here. Just mix that in with a little bit of FPS style movement, and you've got the idea. The story is where Vessels shines. I loved the story in this game. One of the things that I enjoyed most about it, is that everything isn't explained to you. There are no exposition dumps, or long audio logs detailing what is going on. You have to figure things out via investigation, and piece together your own theories on what is happening.

The graphics here are what I would consider serviceable. Meaning that they're as good as they have to be. You will not be wowed at any point by the visuals, nor will you be distracted by them. After the first few minutes of playing, you will probably be used to the look of the game, and hopefully appreciative of it. Personally, I liked the way it looked... but I'm under no illusion that most people will.

Most of the music in the game is unimpressive. It isn't bad, by any means. But, not once did I stop to take note of the music. The soundscape did make me feel as though I was on board a creepy space-ship. I definitely got the vibe that something strange was going on, and that the tension in the air was thick.

I had no problems with the game-play. There were no glitches during my play-through. I did not get stuck anywhere and the game didn't freeze. It worked as intended. You select different dialogue options to progress the narrative and gain information about the characters and their intentions. The main mechanic is character possession. You take control of different characters to gain access to their quarters and designated areas of the ship. Death is not a fail-state in this game, it is a progression mechanic. One of my favorite things about the game is that there is not a lot of wasted time. When you think that you have failed, the game uses the information you learned during your last run to keep the story going. This was very well done, and an interesting choice.

Imagine playing a game that is a mixture of different science fiction movies, and narrative driven games. This is what Vessels feels like to me. It is a science fiction interactive mystery novel. All of the characters have different personalities and this can be used to your advantage. Throughout the game you are tasked with manipulating the other characters on the ship, to achieve your goals. The main story of the game is best left unspoiled, and experienced first-hand.

The game has quite an example to make of the seemingly selfish agendas from your fellow human beings and makes you distrust the people who have the power to decide to pull the airlock lever or not. Even if the were once your friends. You manipulate people to escape but as I played it became clear that the entity giving you your powers just might also be manipulating you. It will show you things that will make you think: "man why shouldn't I save myself at their expense? They'd do the same to me too", but the devil for everyone and everything's motives is in the details. Who will you side with if offered godlike powers? Your friends who are so cautious about events you don't recall that you have to convince them not to kill you just to to be safe? Or the entity within that offers you so much more than just being a mere curious oddity in other humans' eyes?

Criticism and "not huge spoilers" ahead but skip this if you feel that knowing the room and general choices you can make there is too much of a spoiler:

Mild spoilers:

For criticism I will say that in the final act (as of June 2021) I tried out making certain choices differently in subsequent playthroughs. Choices that the game seemed to log and record in the user interface too help me understand where the entity is coming from but that did not end up going anywhere significant even though they were huge revelations that you'd think would change the ending. It even got to the point where I seemingly unlocked dialog from a character who I locked in the airlock that magically found themselves able to suddenly open the airlock door to hand me a random item (a poker chip or something), only to go back to pretending that they can't open the airlock to free themselves. Wtf? Where did this gambling item come from? What does it have to do with anything? Why can they open the door at all? Let alone to hand me an item... This character even asks if it was the "missing piece" and that you should check in with a different character in the reactor room. You do so and get no new dialog or action options. It is as if it was to be implemented but the idea was scrapped. There is another gambling chip you can find in someone else's room but...nothing seem to come of it.

If you play your cards right, that character in the reactor room will trust you and also tell you that there must be a manual to handle the situation in the reactor room. This also leads to nowhere when you go look at said manuals. I also made a choice to spare everyone from dying by my hand too and yet I haven't found an ending where that decision made any impact. What am I doing to miss the "everyone lives"-ending? Unless I am missing something, that aspect feels as if it was scrapped, If I ever get corrected on this fact, I will update this review.

End mild spoilers:

Even with that out of the way, I had a great time with this unique game and recommend that everyone experience it.
