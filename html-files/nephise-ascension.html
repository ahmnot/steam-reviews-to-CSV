Warning: This review contains no outright spoilers, but it contains critique on the salient themes of the game.

It's been way too long since I've played a Tonguc Bodur game. Now that I finally have time to, I am warmed by the loving embrace of the Nephise series, and honestly, it's like I never left.

Less than two hours of gameplay? Check. Collecting orbs of dubious purpose and worth? Check. Spinning statues around in mystifying different directions? You got it, baby. But wait, there's more. This time, Nephise gets two bangin' additions to the canon: pronunciation of her name (neh-FEE-see) and a character model.

And, let's just be honest here, there is more gameplay in Nephise: Ascension than in most of Bodur's catalog combined. You have to avoid enemies, navigate mazes, pull levers, unlock doors--this game is basically on par with Metal Gear Solid. I am accustomed to getting a Skinner box wrapped in mere sooty newsprint; this third installment of Nephise adds a few googly eyes as well.

So first I ought to talk about what I liked about this game. There were a few platforming-esque puzzles, like swinging bridges, rotating walkways, and sliding ledges, which were really something. I love a good platformer Unfortunately, this game doesn't take its platforming beyond these most basic of challenges.

I also enjoyed getting a wee bit more backstory on Nephise and the orbs that I have grown to know so well. We learn about the connection between Nephise, her sister, her father, and her impetus to go on these adventures. Unfortunately, these don't amount to any significance other than "Nephise loves her family; Nephise knows that being a good person is good; Nephise likes it when good things happen to her." I try to assume that a given stranger is generally good, so I probably would have assumed these things about Nephise since I don't know anything else about her after playing her other two games, but thankfully Ascension clears up any doubts left to linger in my mind.

Finally, I am digging Nephise's physical affect. She looks like what would happen if the hairstylist for My Chemical Romance started doing MMA fights in her free time. And between this and the rune puzzle, is anyone else getting the distinct impression that Hellblade: Senua's Sacrifice took some artistic cues from Tonguc Bodur??

Okay, onto the less positive aspects of the game. Firstly, the scale. Though the graphics are nicely assembled, and the lighting is mostly sensible and pleasant (minus the extremely overdone shine of the orbs, to the point that searching for them was trivial in this game), every character, including Nephise, looks like they are approximately the size of an army man. You can barely see over the grass the whole time you're in the valley, which is the only reason why this game isn't just an hour long for the first-time player, and when you encounter enemies, their Lilliputian stature completely deceived me into thinking "Oh, what cute widdle zombies!" Then I went up to one to give it a hug and died -_-

Secondly, this voiceover wasn't doing it for me. TB has had some fun narrators before (namely the immortal Drizzlepath), but this VoiceBunny.com person needs to turn the cheese factor down. Not that the writing needed some brilliant actor, and I'm glad it's not a whiny younger woman like it sometimes is in these, but the grandma aesthetic just seemed incongruous. I am glad, however, that VoiceBunny.com's voice was clearly distinguishable from the other voice in the game, though I won't spoil whose it is. In all reality, though, I would rather have had that other voice as the narrator and the grandma voice as the other part. It would make much more sense story-wise if that were the case.

My main beef with this game, though, is the lore itself. We have known for quite a while now that Nephise is an orb collector, but now we find out that orbs help her to do all sorts of magical things: healing, offensive capabilities, general strength, perception...in the bleak void that is the worldbuilding of the Nephise series, they just do everything. That takes everything else these games try to do out of the "fantastical fictional universe" sphere and chucks them into "childish power fantasy" territory. Contrast that with something like LOTR, for example, wherein even the most powerful of objects merely grants one power. The problem with a do-everything easy button from a storytelling perspective is that it obliterates any semblance of risk, stakes, or conflict, which means that any story you may have had up until that point is rendered void to the audience's emotions. Not that this game tried to create much of a story other than "Nephise does a thing she always wanted to do," but what little it puts on your plate is rendered sugar free and mushy by the orbs, which tell you right away that she will be able to do anything she wants to. Then, when she has no trouble accomplishing her goals, your main gut reaction is, "Oh. I guess I figured that would happen. What's next? Do I have something worthwhile to play? No? Just walking sims and hidden object games? Well, that about figures."

The music was also very repetitive. I wouldn't have minded it were it not for how quickly it started to repeat. The music in the torch section was probably the best there was.

All that said, I guess this isn't the worst thing this studio has churned out. But this is the sort of project that a studio such as this does just churn out while waiting for the next big, meaty game. I can't wait for Tonguc Bodur to buy up the rights for MGS, though. It's right up his alley, and he's got what it takes.

Was alle Spiele gemeinsam haben, ist eine wirklich wunderschöne Spielewelt mit passender, stimmungsvoller Musik, die zum Entspannen und Erkunden einlädt. Zum Glück war dies aber nur in kurzen Teilen des Spiels der Fall... und zum Glück war das auch wirklich eher harmlos ausgestaltet, sodass auch ich als bekennendes Angst-Häschen das Spiel durchziehen konnte. Was ich unbedingt noch loswerden möchte, ist ein Lob für den Indie-Entwickler, Tonguc Bodur, der sich auch als Gestalter und Entwickler im Laufe der Nephise-Reihe deutlich gesteigert hat. Überhaupt empfinde ich es aber als wirklich große Leistung, wenn man als Einzelperson ein Videospiel erschafft und dabei wirklich alles selber macht.
