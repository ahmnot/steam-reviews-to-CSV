Dr. Livingstone is a very well thought out adventure that tells a fictionalized account of the friendship between Henry Stanley and David Livingston. You play from the first person perspective of Henry and have arrived at David's house in Africa to check on his well being. You find the house in disarray and must work your way through looking for your friend.
The game is like an 'escape the room' exercise with an ongoing narrative told through David's letters/memos and Henry's comments. You solve a variety of puzzles in each room in order to find the key to the next room. In this manner, you work your way through the house.

The puzzles are interesting and diverse. There are some familiar puzzle types but these are uniquely framed and integrated into the story. The house is filled with African art and artifacts and there is a lot to look at and examine. There are a lot of drawers and cabinets to open. Some have items of interest, others do not.

Ever the achievement hunter, I played through the game twice. My first play through took me about 8 hours total (without a guide or hints).

The game has an automatic save-on-exit and you do not have the ability to save 'on demand.' When you enter the final room of the house, you cannot return to previous areas to finish achievements. At that point, a 'new game' is your only option.

'Hot spots' are not indicated except by the cursor. So, prepare to be 'scanning' all areas watching to see if the cursor changes. You have a 'hand' cursor to open drawers/doors and to manipulate objects. You have a 'cog' cursor for puzzle areas. You can move towards or away from a puzzle and use items from your inventory items by dragging and dropping. For some puzzles, inventory is used when 'close up' and for others, it is used when further away. At times, the hand cursor is very small and is controlling an equally small area on the screen which presents some challenges. While this did not detract from the game experience, I found the controls to be a bit of a struggle all the way through.

I recommend this game to those who enjoy puzzles with a story. It is well written with quality voice acting and a sound track that adds to the game experience. Like most new releases, you may encounter a glitch during game play. The development team is highly engaged with the Steam Discussion forum and is actively working with players when/if an issue is discovered.

The game plays sort of like a robust escape-the-room adventure game, where you start in a small space, but progressively gain access to more rooms of the house. What's cool about this approach is the inventory management - everything you find in the current room/location is only used within it, so you don't have to worry about missing something and having to backtrack all the way down to kitchen or corridor. Additionally the inventory items can only be used on closeups of certain places/things, so you don't have to worry about the usual "use-everything-on-everyting" adventure game trope. The graphics are presented in a low-poly style, and the ambient tracks are absolutely excellent - ranging from calm and mellow tunes, to more sinister and tense, depending on your progress. The instrument choice is impeccable as well - definitely fitting into the setting and aesthetics of the game.

The only thing I found a bit annoying was the visibility of some items necessary for progression (like the corkscrew or screwdriver), but usually a second (and more careful) glance at my current location revealed anything I could've missed, so it wasn't that big of a deal.

Overall if you're looking for an intriguing FPP adventure game, definitely give DLIP? a chance, I'm sure you will have an excellent time!

I don't know what was the intended control method for the game, I played it with a mouse and keyboard and found sometimes the door-opening/turning mechanic a bit awkward sometimes, for example I had trouble with closing the valves in the second-tho last room. Maybe could you implement an option in the settings that these kind of movements could be done automatically? (I am not a programmer, I don't know how difficult is that).
I liked the games aesthetic, it is really something unique and gave a really interesting atmosphere. Sometimes because of this cluttered aesthetic though it was difficult to locate objects that just "blended into the background".
Even though the puzzles didn't require combination of objects, I think they were versatile and well-designed with varying difficulty. The surgery room was my favorite, I spent at least 10 minutes on that 4 wheel-key puzzle just to realize that it should be mirrored and after solving it I found out that I didn't really need the magnifying glass because I solved the head puzzle earlier without it.
What I wish this game had more of is interaction between the main character and the environment. You can look at all the paintings but you rarely get back a reaction and the paintings aren't part of any puzzle. It felt a bit like a wasted opportunity. I know that good voice acting is expensive, but I would've really enjoyed hearing more about the opinion of the character. This would be also a good opportunity to give clues because I know that some people are really against asking for hints.
Some specific thoughts:
In the kitchen the hint says that the corkscrew is in a drawer. It is on the top of a crate...
The very last puzzle was a bit disappointing for me because I expected that I will need to collect all 5 objects to get the key and not get the 5th from the diorama. Maybe it would be better if you could place the objects one-by-one in the diorama and not get them back in your inventory if you leave the puzzle unfinished (for me this said that I would need to place in all 5 objects at the same time).
Tl;dr: Even though I found some things negative, I enjoyed this game overall.
