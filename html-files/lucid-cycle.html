I have always enjoyed Tonguc Bodur's games and have played all of them. This title is quite a departure from the peaceful, beautiful journeys experienced in his other games.

If we view dreams as unrelated bursts from our mind, then Lucid Cycle does an admirable job of capturing this. It is a collage of short scenes that are all different in terms of the visual style and game play. Some sequences are easy (cross an area to a portal) and others are more time consuming (eliminate objects in an area, navigate past obstacles, build a bridge). In some, you will keep getting returned to the starting point until you figure out what you are required to do (or not do). In between sets of dreams, you wake in your apartment, perform a couple of tasks and then go back to sleep.

For me, it was very jarring to play, as it involved short bursts of attention and then total shifts. I am wired to be more focused and I do not do well with too many inputs coming all at once. Thus, bouncing from scene to scene and from task to task was not in my comfort zone. Since I had no idea when (or if) my game was being saved, I played straight through (in just over 2 hours) to avoid the risk of starting over.

I would recommend Lucid Cycle to those who seek a diverse, experiential interlude. Those who thrive in a more cohesive game world and like having a sense of purpose might want to select another one of Tonguc Bodur's titles.

Évaluation publiée le 4 avril 2021
DISCLAIMER: I know my playtime appears as 0.1 hours, but I had to play Lucid Cycle in offline mode because the "synchronizing with the cloud" step in launching the game kept freezing. For me, at least, it truly was an achievement to open the main menu successfully. I trust, though, that the contents of my review will make it plain that I did play the game in its entirety.

Lucid Cycle is a game where you dream, paint, and talk about your dreams, except all the painting happens off screen. The dreams consist of short, surrealistic gameplay segments, most of which are puzzles of some sort or another. When you awake, usually after 2-4 dreams, you talk to a character about one of the dreams you just had.

When I began this game, I assumed that all the dream sequences would just be mini walking simulators, similar to Tonguc Bodur's other games, but I was pleasantly surprised to find the puzzles more and more frequent, and even a couple of platforming segments. Most of these are not complex or progressive puzzles (a la The Talos Principle, Portal, or The Witness where each puzzle builds upon a skillset), but they are all mostly different, which was a big shocker. Normally these games have one puzzle type per game, but this game had well over a dozen unique puzzles. One of them (I won't spoil it, but it was the one where your cursor turns into an eye) was even a puzzle I had never really seen before in another game, so that was fun. Some of the puzzles involve firing projectiles, and some of them involve driving.

Another plus to this game is that it lacks the developer's typical whiny narrative style. Instead, you get some commentary on the possible meanings of some of your dreams, but this does not form an overarching narrative about the protagonist in any way, which is refreshing. The game is mostly focused on the experiences of the dreams themselves and figuring out the mechanics of each puzzle. Don't get me wrong, some of these puzzles are extremely basic or insanely tedious (for example, the maze where it looks like you have cataracts the whole time, and the blurriness forms the basis of the "challenge"), but it was just nice to not get talked to the whole time like I had been expecting. If you want to wade into "probably reading too much into the game here" territory, I suppose you could say that Lucid Cycle presents some organic storytelling that explores the importance of dreams, but even that, if it was intentional, was minimal.

The game's graphics are pretty, although I could not get it to run at 60 fps consistently even with all the graphics settings on their minimum levels (with a GTX 970), so get ready for a little choppiness. The assets used, though, *are* typical of a TB game in that they lack the slightest hint of subtlety. Walking around the dreams feels like you're playing a game made by a teenager who just dropped some acid and spent the whole trip selecting textures for Unreal for some reason. What I liked about the graphics, though, was the first-person adjusting to different light levels effect. That looked genuinely good.

The music is pretty much garbage, to the point that during some of the longer puzzles, I put on my own music in lieu of listening to it. "Soda City Funk" did not exactly match the game's mood, but it still made for a better playing experience. During the dream sequences, there are not many instances of in-universe sound effects, so you won't miss much by making your own sound track.

The controls in this game are alright. Normally I would say they are pretty floaty, but you do play in a dream for the vast majority of the experience, so there's a good excuse for it.

Now, I do want to make a note here on the attempted surrealism. Perhaps when computer graphics first started looking decent, it was enough to shove random stuff together on a screen or move the camera around at random and call it surreal, but I think in the 2020s something more is required. I generally like what Tongy's going for here, but it falls short by quite a bit. Surrealism really means that what you're looking at appears as though it should be realistic, but one or more parts of the imagery makes it clear that it could never be real. A house full of man-sized leeches does not look as though it should be realistic--a more surrealist take on the idea would be to see a figure in a suit drinking tea at a table, who, when seen from the front, is revealed to have a leech's head for a face.

Again, I sort of rate my surrealism on a curve based on the time period and medium. For example, I often thought of a much-beloved game of mine, LSD: Dream Emulator, while I was playing Lucid Cycle. LSD came out in the late '90s, I think, and it has a very similar structure to Lucid Cycle: you experience a sequence of dreams from a first-person perspective, and when you wake up, you get some manner of information about the dreams. LSD obviously did not have the graphical finesse or the ease in programming different mechanics that the Unreal Engine offers, but I think it did more with what it did have in a lot of ways. For example, the things you can interact with in LSD act differently each time you see them, which creates an urgency in your gameplay because you want to see what happens when you reach certain spots. This mimics very nearly the feeling of urgency that you get in many real dreams. All the dreams in Lucid Cycle have very similar pacing. Even the one that you have to beat under a certain amount of time does not make you feel hurried, which is largely due to the horridly slow gait of the protagonist. This is just one comparison, but many others can be made. Instead of making every single one, I will just suggest that you at least watch some let's play footage of LSD: Dream Emulator.

Anyway, in a lot of ways I feel like Tons-O-Fun Bodur has been listening to my humble pleas: I have been asking for more puzzles and platforming, and Lucid Cycle does that. I have been asking for fewer narrative elements, and TB pulled through. So, with all of these pieces, Lucid Cycle is pretty much Bodur's finest game. He's evolving from a walking sim hack to a puzzle game hack. At least he's not trying to evolve into a FPS developer, though, because this game gives you a very good picture of what that would look like, too! I feel like I've watched him complete a journey over the years. That said, I would not confuse Lucid Cycle with a good puzzle game. I love puzzle games, even if they aren't great. This one bats about 50-50 in terms of puzzles that are just annoying, though. Half are just generic, or decent, or even good, and the other half make you wish you had a skip button. Notably none of them seemed to glitch out, which was nice, but some of them were just too slow or too long. Even so, if I had to recommend one TB game to a random stranger, Lucid Cycle would certainly get second place.

Another disclaimer: My co-pilot for my playthrough of Lucid Cycle made a comment that is completely true about the game, which is that it is the most Air Control-esque of any Tonguc Bodur game to date, and that is the totality of the basis of my recommendation of this game.

In terms of the narrative, you play 2-4 dreams (some are walking simulators, some are shooters, some are movement puzzles) and then you wake up and talk to an AI lamp who interprets one of your dreams. This definitely goes down as my worst gaming experience of the year. I would be embarrassed to publish this, especially when there are so many Steam games that cost as little as $1 and offer a vastly superior experience.

Lucid Cycle is a bunch of Unreal Marketplace assets shoved in a blender and arranged into a kind of first person adventure/walking sim. If the screenshots and visuals look pretty good in this game, it's because the "developer" had nothing to do with them, which also explains why they look so mismatched, some detailed, some lazy/low poly, etc.

So we've established you can take the Unreal Engine, and throw your credit card at the Unreal Marketplace (it's a fun game matching all the store assets here, from the deep sea diver to the leeches) and come up with what looks like an actual video game.

The thing about games is while it's vital for a game to be well presented and take advantage of the PC's considerable gaming capability, it also needs to be a fun game, and this is just a lazy, directionless walking sim with a few light puzzles where you guess how to progress to the next set of stock assets.

This is really an attempt to turn a bunch of stock assets into something profitable by slapping them together hard enough and calling it "art". It's not like walking sims can't be art, take a look at Dear Elise for an example... but that's the thing, the best games are about storytelling. Which doesn't take place here. The objective of this wasn't to make an actual game for gamers, it's a profit orienatated experience. If this was free, it might even almost be worth recommending, more as a demo for Unreal Engine than a game, but asking $4 USD for this is just not going to work.

It's not really a game. It's a "I bought a bunch of Unreal assets from the marketplace, so give me money!" exercise.
